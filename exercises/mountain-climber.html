Mountain Climbers

A dynamic, full-body exercise that combines core strength, cardio, and coordination in one efficient movement

Core, Shoulders, Hip Flexors
Beginner-Intermediate
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Core (Rectus Abdominis, Obliques), Hip Flexors
  • Secondary Muscles: Shoulders, Chest, Quads, Hamstrings
  • Equipment: None (Bodyweight Exercise)
  • Type: Dynamic, Compound, Cardio
  • Experience Level: Beginner-Intermediate
  • Force Type: Core Stabilization + Hip Flexion
  • Benefits: Cardio, Core Strength, Coordination

How to Perform Mountain Climbers

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Begin in a high plank position with your hands placed directly under your shoulders, arms straight but not locked. Your body should form a straight line from head to heels, with your core engaged and gaze directed slightly forward.

  2. 2

    Core Engagement

    Before initiating movement, brace your core by drawing your navel toward your spine. This core engagement is crucial for maintaining proper form and getting the most out of the exercise.

  3. 3

    Knee Drive - First Leg

    Maintaining the plank position, quickly draw your right knee toward your chest, bringing it as close to your right elbow as comfortably possible. Your foot should remain flexed throughout the movement.

  4. 4

    Return to Starting Position

    Return your right leg back to the starting plank position while maintaining core engagement and keeping your hips stable (avoid letting them rise or dip).

  5. 5

    Knee Drive - Second Leg

    As soon as your right leg returns to the starting position, drive your left knee forward toward your chest in the same manner, bringing it as close to your left elbow as comfortably possible.

  6. 6

    Establish Rhythm

    Continue alternating legs in a running motion, finding a rhythm that challenges you while allowing you to maintain proper form. The pace can range from slow and controlled to quick and dynamic based on your fitness level and goals.

  7. 7

    Breathing Pattern

    Breathe normally throughout the exercise, avoiding breath-holding. Some practitioners find it helpful to exhale as each knee drives forward (similar to a running breathing pattern).

  8. 8

    Complete Your Set

    Continue for the prescribed time (often 30-60 seconds) or number of repetitions per leg. Keep your form strong throughout the entire set and avoid letting your hips rise as you fatigue.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Maintain a neutral spine: Keep your back flat and head in a neutral position aligned with your spine. Avoid dropping your head or looking up, which can strain your neck.
  • Engage your shoulders: Press your hands firmly into the ground and engage your shoulder blades to prevent shoulder slouching. Think about pushing the floor away from you.
  • Hip position: Focus on keeping your hips stable and level throughout the movement. Your hips should neither sag toward the floor nor pike up toward the ceiling.
  • Control the tempo: Start with a slow, controlled pace to master form before increasing speed. Even at faster speeds, prioritize control over momentum.
  • Modify as needed: If you're new to the exercise, you can perform it with your hands elevated on a bench or sturdy surface to reduce intensity while building strength.
  • Focus on the core: While your legs are moving, think of this primarily as a core exercise. Maintaining tension through your trunk is more important than how fast your legs move.
  • Hand positioning: If wrist discomfort is an issue, try placing your hands on dumbbells or pushup handles, which create a more neutral wrist position.
  • Knee placement: Aim to bring your knee toward your same-side elbow rather than to the center of your chest for better oblique engagement and to maintain proper hip alignment.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Lifting the hips too high: This reduces core engagement and turns the exercise into more of a downward dog position with knee drives. Keep your body in a straight line from head to heels.
  • Letting the lower back sag: This puts stress on your lower back and disengages your core. Maintain a neutral spine throughout the movement.
  • Taking too large steps: Your feet should move relatively vertically rather than taking large horizontal steps, which can disrupt your balance and reduce core activation.
  • Moving the upper body excessively: Your shoulders and hands should remain stable throughout the exercise. Avoid rocking back and forth or shifting side to side.
  • Holding your breath: This restricts oxygen flow and can cause unnecessary tension. Establish a consistent breathing pattern as you would when running.
  • Placing hands too far forward: This puts additional stress on the shoulders and makes the exercise harder to control. Keep your hands directly beneath your shoulders.
  • Focusing on speed over form: While mountain climbers can be performed quickly for cardiovascular benefits, sacrificing form for speed reduces effectiveness and increases injury risk.

Mountain Climber Variations

Slow Mountain Climbers

Performing the movement at a slow, controlled pace to maximize core engagement and improve form, perfect for beginners or as a form-focused warm-up.

Cross-Body Mountain Climbers

Driving each knee toward the opposite elbow to increase oblique engagement and add rotational core challenge while improving coordination.

Slider Mountain Climbers

Using sliders or towels under your feet on a smooth surface to reduce impact and increase the core stabilization challenge.

Spider Mountain Climbers

Bringing each knee to the outside of the same-side elbow in a wider motion that further engages the obliques and hip flexors.
