Dips

A powerful bodyweight exercise for developing upper body pushing strength and muscle definition

Triceps, Chest, Shoulders
Intermediate
Parallel Bars, Benches, or Sturdy Chairs

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Triceps Brachii
  • Secondary Muscles: Pectoralis Major (Chest), Anterior Deltoids (Front Shoulders), Rhomboids
  • Equipment: Parallel Bars, Dip Station, or Two Sturdy Surfaces
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Push
  • Force: Push (Vertical)

How to Perform Dips

  1. 1

    Equipment Setup

    Find parallel bars or a dip station. If these aren't available, two sturdy chairs or benches of equal height placed side by side can work. Ensure whatever equipment you use is stable and can support your body weight.

  2. 2

    Starting Position

    Grip the bars with your palms facing inward (toward your body). Jump or step up to straighten your arms and support your body weight. Your arms should be fully extended but not locked out. Keep your shoulders down away from your ears.

  3. 3

    Body Position

    Depending on your focus, adjust your body position. For more triceps emphasis, keep your body upright with minimal forward lean. For more chest engagement, lean forward slightly (about 15-30 degrees) and allow your elbows to flare out a bit more.

  4. 4

    Lowering Phase

    Inhale as you slowly bend your elbows to lower your body. Keep your shoulders down and back, and maintain control throughout the movement. Lower until your upper arms are approximately parallel to the ground (elbows at about 90 degrees).

  5. 5

    Bottom Position

    Pause briefly at the bottom position. Be careful not to go too deep as this can place excessive stress on your shoulder joints. Maintain tension in your arms and chest.

  6. 6

    Pressing Phase

    Exhale as you push through your palms to straighten your arms and return to the starting position. Focus on using your triceps to power the movement, keeping your core tight throughout.

  7. 7

    Top Position

    Return to the starting position with arms extended but not locked out. Prepare for the next repetition by maintaining proper shoulder position and body control.

  8. 8

    Repeat & Dismount

    Perform the desired number of repetitions. After completing your set, return to the top position and step or hop down carefully. Avoid dismounting while in the bottom position to prevent shoulder strain.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Shoulder position: Keep your shoulders down and back, away from your ears, to prevent shoulder impingement and maintain proper form.
  • Body alignment: Your body should remain relatively straight during the movement. Avoid excessive swinging or kicking with your legs.
  • Grip placement: Position your hands directly under your shoulders or slightly wider for a comfortable, balanced position.
  • Elbow path: Keep your elbows close to your body for more triceps emphasis or allow them to flare out slightly for more chest engagement.
  • Depth control: Lower until your upper arms are approximately parallel to the ground. Going too deep can stress your shoulder joints unnecessarily.
  • Core engagement: Keep your core tight and engaged throughout the movement to stabilize your body.
  • Full range of motion: Aim for a full range of motion within safe limits for maximum muscle development.
  • Breathing: Inhale during the lowering phase and exhale during the pushing phase to maintain proper bracing and power.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Shrugging shoulders: Allowing your shoulders to rise toward your ears puts excessive stress on the shoulder joints and reduces triceps engagement.
  • Insufficient depth: Not lowering your body enough (often due to insufficient strength) reduces the effectiveness of the exercise.
  • Excessive depth: Lowering too far beyond 90 degrees at the elbow can place undue stress on the shoulder joints.
  • Body swinging: Using momentum by swinging your body to complete repetitions reduces muscle engagement and can lead to injury.
  • Locking elbows: Fully locking your elbows at the top of the movement shifts stress to your elbow joints instead of keeping tension on the muscles.
  • Wrist hyperextension: Allowing your wrists to bend backward excessively can cause wrist strain. Keep wrists neutral.
  • Drooping head: Looking down or allowing your head to droop forward can lead to improper spine alignment. Maintain a neutral neck position.
  • Fast, uncontrolled movement: Rushing through repetitions reduces effectiveness and increases injury risk. Control the movement, especially the lowering phase.

Dip Variations

Bench Dips

A beginner-friendly variation performed with hands on a bench or chair behind you and feet on the floor or elevated on another bench.

Ring Dips

Performed using gymnastic rings, adding an element of instability that increases the challenge and engages more stabilizing muscles.

Weighted Dips

Adding external resistance via a weight belt, weighted vest, or dumbbell held between the knees to increase the intensity.

Korean Dips

An advanced variation where you rotate from a forward support position to a reverse support, requiring significant strength and mobility.

Straight Bar Dips

Performed on a single horizontal bar, requiring more stability and often placing more emphasis on the chest muscles.

L-Sit Dips

Performing dips while holding your legs straight out in front of you, significantly increasing core and hip flexor engagement.

Dip Progression Path for Beginners

If you're new to fitness or currently unable to perform full dips, follow this progression path to build strength gradually:

  • Bench Dip With Feet on Floor: The easiest variation, with hands on a bench behind you and feet flat on the floor in front. This reduces the amount of bodyweight you're lifting.
  • Assisted Dips with Resistance Band: Loop a resistance band over the dip bars and place your knees or feet in the band to reduce the effective bodyweight you're lifting. Use thicker bands for more assistance.
  • Negative Dips: Focus only on the lowering phase. Jump or step up to the top position, then lower yourself as slowly as possible. This builds eccentric strength that will carry over to full dips.
  • Partial Range Dips: Perform dips through a partial range of motion, gradually increasing depth as strength improves. Even shallow dips build strength that transfers to full range.
  • Full Dips: The standard dip as described in this guide. Start with small sets (even 1-2 reps) and gradually increase as you build strength.

For students, consistent practice is key. Aim to incorporate some form of dip progression in your routine 2-3 times per week, with at least one day of rest between sessions to allow for recovery and adaptation.

Incorporating Dips Into HIIT Workouts

Dips are an excellent addition to HIIT workouts, providing an effective upper body push exercise that can be scaled to any fitness level. Here are ways to include dips in your high-intensity interval training:

  • Dip AMRAP (As Many Reps As Possible): Perform as many dips as possible in 20-30 seconds, rest for 10-20 seconds, and repeat for 4-8 rounds. Use bench dips if parallel bar dips are too challenging.
  • Push-Pull Supersets: Alternate between dips (push) and rowing movements or pull-ups (pull) with minimal rest between exercises. Rest 60-90 seconds between supersets and repeat 3-5 times.
  • Dip-Jump Intervals: Perform 10-15 seconds of dips followed immediately by 10-15 seconds of a jumping exercise (jumping jacks, squat jumps, etc.), then rest for 20-30 seconds. Repeat for 8-10 rounds.
  • Tabata Dips: Perform dips for 20 seconds, rest for 10 seconds, and repeat for 8 rounds (4 minutes total). Use bench dips or assisted variations to maintain form throughout all rounds.
  • Upper Body Circuit: Include dips in a circuit with push-ups, pike push-ups, and plank shoulder taps. Perform each exercise for 30 seconds with 15 seconds rest between exercises. Complete 3-5 rounds.
  • Descending Rep Ladder: Start with 10 dips (or a number appropriate to your level), rest 15 seconds, perform 9 dips, rest 15 seconds, and continue down to 1 rep.

When incorporating dips into HIIT workouts, prioritize proper form even when fatigued. For beginners, focus on quality reps rather than quantity, and choose appropriate variations that allow you to maintain good technique throughout the workout.

Dips for Students: Practical Applications

As a student, dips offer practical benefits beyond just building muscle. Here's how this exercise supports student life and how to fit it into your busy schedule:

  • Dorm Room Setup: No gym access? Use two sturdy chairs of equal height, placed back-to-back with some space between them. Place a towel or cushion on each chair to protect your hands.
  • Study Break Exercise: Use brief dip sessions as active study breaks to improve circulation, boost mental alertness, and reduce the negative effects of prolonged sitting.
  • Improved Posture: Regular dip training strengthens the muscles that support proper sitting posture during long lectures or study sessions, potentially reducing back and neck pain.
  • Backpack Strength: The pushing strength developed through dips transfers to daily activities like carrying a heavy backpack around campus.
  • Stress Relief: Incorporate dip training during high-stress periods like midterms or finals. The release of endorphins can help manage stress and improve focus.
  • Time-Efficient Training: A simple circuit of dips, push-ups, and bodyweight squats can provide a complete workout in under 15 minutes when time is limited.

Remember that as a student, your recovery ability may be affected by factors like sleep quality, nutrition, and stress levels. Listen to your body and adjust your training volume accordingly, especially during demanding academic periods.
