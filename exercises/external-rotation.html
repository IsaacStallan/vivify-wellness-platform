External Rotation

A targeted exercise to strengthen the rotator cuff muscles for improved shoulder health and stability

Shoulders, Rotator Cuff
Beginner to Intermediate
Resistance Band or Light Dumbbell

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Infraspinatus, Teres Minor (Rotator Cuff)
  • Secondary Muscles: Posterior Deltoid, Rhomboids, Middle Trapezius
  • Equipment: Resistance Band or Light Dumbbell
  • Type: Isolation
  • Experience Level: Beginner to Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Isolation
  • Force: Pull (Rotational)

Video Demonstration

How to Perform External Rotation

  1. 1

    Starting Position (Standing with Resistance Band)

    Attach a resistance band to a stable anchor point at elbow height. Stand with your side toward the anchor. Hold your elbow close to your side, bent at 90 degrees so your forearm is parallel to the floor, with your hand positioned in front of your body.

  2. 2

    Alternative Starting Position (Seated with Dumbbell)

    Sit on a bench with a light dumbbell in hand. Rest your elbow on your knee or place a small towel under your elbow for support. Maintain a 90-degree angle at your elbow with your forearm pointing toward the floor initially.

  3. 3

    Movement Execution

    While keeping your elbow fixed against your side (or on your knee if seated), rotate your forearm away from your body. For the resistance band variation, this means pulling against the band's resistance. For the dumbbell variation, this means lifting the weight by rotating at the shoulder.

  4. 4

    Range of Motion

    Rotate until your forearm is approximately parallel to the floor (for seated dumbbell variation) or as far as your mobility comfortably allows (for standing band variation). You should feel the muscles in the back of your shoulder working.

  5. 5

    Hold and Contract

    At the end position, hold briefly for 1-2 seconds and focus on contracting the rotator cuff muscles. This isometric hold enhances muscle activation and strengthening.

  6. 6

    Controlled Return

    Slowly return to the starting position, resisting the movement on the way back. This eccentric portion of the exercise is equally important for developing rotator cuff strength.

  7. 7

    Repetition

    Complete the desired number of repetitions with one arm, then switch to the other arm. Typical rep ranges are 10-15 reps per side for 2-3 sets.

  8. 8

    Breathing Pattern

    Inhale during the initial phase and exhale as you rotate your arm outward. Maintain normal breathing throughout the exercise without holding your breath.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Maintain stable posture: Keep your spine neutral and avoid leaning or twisting during the movement. This isolates the rotator cuff muscles properly.
  • Elbow position: Keep your elbow pressed firmly against your side (or supported on your knee if seated) throughout the exercise. This prevents using momentum and ensures proper muscle targeting.
  • Use appropriate resistance: Start with very light weight or resistance. The rotator cuff muscles are relatively small and easily overwhelmed with heavy weight, which can lead to improper form or injury.
  • Focus on controlled movement: Perform the exercise slowly and deliberately, avoiding jerky or ballistic motions that could strain the shoulder joint.
  • Range of motion: Work within a pain-free range of motion. If you feel any pinching or sharp pain, reduce the range or consult with a healthcare professional.
  • Wrist position: Keep your wrist neutral (not bent) throughout the movement to avoid unnecessary strain on the forearm.
  • Balance training: Always train both shoulders equally, even if one seems stronger than the other. This helps maintain shoulder symmetry and reduces injury risk.
  • Gradual progression: Increase resistance gradually over time. Rotator cuff strength develops slowly but consistently with proper training.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using too much weight: The rotator cuff muscles are small stabilizers that don't require heavy weights to be effectively trained. Using excessive weight leads to compensatory movements and defeats the purpose of the exercise.
  • Moving the elbow away from the body: Allowing the elbow to drift away from your side introduces momentum and reduces the effectiveness of the exercise. The elbow should remain fixed in position.
  • Recruiting larger muscles: Using your back or trunk to generate momentum instead of isolating the rotator cuff. This often happens when the weight is too heavy or when fatigue sets in.
  • Insufficient range of motion: Not rotating far enough to fully engage the rotator cuff muscles. While you should stay within a pain-free range, most people can safely rotate until the forearm is perpendicular to the body.
  • Moving too quickly: Rushing through repetitions reduces the time under tension and may cause you to use momentum rather than muscle strength. External rotations should be performed with controlled, deliberate movements.
  • Inconsistent training: Treating rotator cuff exercises as optional or only doing them sporadically. Consistent training is necessary for developing and maintaining shoulder health.
  • Improper shoulder position: Elevating or rounding the shoulders during the movement, which can cause impingement issues. Keep the shoulder blade retracted and depressed slightly.

External Rotation Variations

Side-Lying External Rotation

Performed while lying on your side with the working arm's elbow resting on your torso, providing excellent isolation of the rotator cuff.

Cable External Rotation

Using a cable machine provides consistent tension throughout the movement for more uniform muscle recruitment.

Prone External Rotation

Performed lying face down on a bench with the arm hanging off the side, adding the challenge of working against gravity.

90/90 External Rotation

Performed with the shoulder abducted to 90 degrees and the elbow also bent to 90 degrees, challenging the rotator cuff in a more functional position.
