Lateral Raises

An isolation exercise that directly targets the middle deltoids for broader, more defined shoulders

Middle Deltoids (Side Shoulders)
Beginner-Intermediate
Dumbbells, Cables, or Resistance Bands

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Lateral Deltoids (Middle Shoulders)
  • Secondary Muscles: Anterior Deltoids, Trapezius, Supraspinatus
  • Equipment: Dumbbells (Primary), Cables, or Resistance Bands
  • Type: Isolation
  • Experience Level: Beginner-Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Isolation
  • Force: Push (Lateral)

How to Perform Lateral Raises

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, knees slightly bent, and core engaged. Hold a dumbbell in each hand at your sides with palms facing your body and a slight bend in your elbows (about 10-15 degrees).

  2. 2

    Proper Posture

    Maintain a neutral spine with shoulders pulled back and down. Slightly tilt your torso forward (about 5-10 degrees) to better target the lateral deltoids and reduce trap involvement.

  3. 3

    The Lift

    Keeping your elbows slightly bent, raise the dumbbells out to the sides in a controlled manner. Lead with your elbows rather than your hands, imagining that you're pouring water from a pitcher as your pinky rotates slightly upward.

  4. 4

    Top Position

    Raise the dumbbells until your arms are approximately parallel to the floor or slightly below (about 80-90 degrees). Your arms should form a "T" shape with your torso. Hold for a brief moment at the top, focusing on contracting your lateral deltoids.

  5. 5

    Lowering Phase

    Slowly lower the weights back to the starting position with control, taking about twice as long to lower as it took to raise. Maintain tension in your shoulders throughout the movement—don't let the weight simply drop.

  6. 6

    Repeat

    Repeat for the desired number of repetitions, maintaining proper form throughout the set. Focus on the quality of movement rather than using momentum to lift heavier weights.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Use appropriate weight: The lateral deltoids are relatively small muscles—use lighter weights than you might expect to ensure proper form and targeting.
  • Maintain the slight elbow bend: Keep the same degree of bend throughout the movement; don't straighten or increase the bend during the lift.
  • Control the movement: Aim for a 2-second raise and a 3-second lowering phase for optimal muscle tension and development.
  • Limit range of motion if needed: If you feel your traps taking over, reduce the range of motion to where you can maintain proper form.
  • Wrist position: Keep wrists neutral or with a slight "thumbs up" orientation at the top to maximize lateral deltoid activation.
  • Breath control: Exhale as you raise the weights and inhale as you lower them back down.
  • Focus on mind-muscle connection: Concentrate on feeling the lateral deltoids working throughout the movement.
  • Consider alternating arms: If maintaining form is difficult, try performing the exercise one arm at a time, which can help improve focus and control.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using momentum: Swinging the weights up with body movement takes tension away from the targeted muscles and increases injury risk. Lift with control.
  • Lifting too heavy: This common mistake leads to poor form and shifts the work to other muscles. Choose a weight that allows for proper technique throughout all repetitions.
  • Raising too high: Lifting the dumbbells above shoulder height shifts the work to the upper traps and can cause impingement. Stop at or slightly below shoulder level.
  • Hunching shoulders: Shrugging or elevating the shoulders during the movement activates the trapezius instead of the lateral deltoids. Keep shoulders depressed throughout.
  • Straight arms: Completely straight arms place excessive stress on the elbow joints. Maintain a slight bend in the elbows.
  • Improper hand position: Keeping palms facing down throughout the movement reduces lateral deltoid activation. Use a neutral grip at the bottom that transitions to a slight "thumbs up" at the top.
  • Leaning back: Arching the lower back to help lift the weights can lead to back strain. Maintain a neutral spine with core engaged.

Lateral Raise Variations

Cable Lateral Raise

Using a cable machine provides constant tension throughout the movement, increasing time under tension for better muscle development.

Seated Lateral Raise

Performing the exercise seated prevents the use of lower body momentum, ensuring strict form and better deltoid isolation.

Bent-Over Lateral Raise

Performing the movement while bent forward shifts emphasis to the posterior deltoids, creating more balanced shoulder development.

Single-Arm Lateral Raise

Working one arm at a time allows for better focus, form, and mind-muscle connection with the targeted deltoid.
