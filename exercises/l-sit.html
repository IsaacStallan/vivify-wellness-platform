L-Sit

A challenging isometric hold that builds tremendous core strength, hip flexor endurance, and upper body stability

Core, Hip Flexors, Shoulders
Intermediate-Advanced
Parallel Bars/Floor

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Rectus Abdominis, Hip Flexors, Triceps
  • Secondary Muscles: Shoulders, Quads, Lower Back, Chest
  • Equipment: Parallel Bars, Parallettes, Chairs, or Floor
  • Type: Isometric, Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate to Advanced
  • Mechanics: Static Hold
  • Force: Push (Vertical/Support)

Path to the L-Sit

The L-Sit is an intermediate to advanced bodyweight skill that requires significant core strength, compression strength, and shoulder stability. Work through these progression steps to build toward a full L-Sit:

  1. Supported Foot L-Sit: Start with feet on the ground and hands on raised surfaces (blocks, books, parallettes), lifting your hips off the ground and practicing the position.
  2. One-Foot Supported L-Sit: From the supported position, lift one foot off the ground while keeping the other foot supporting. Alternate between legs to build balanced strength.
  3. Tucked L-Sit: Lift both feet off the ground but keep knees bent close to your chest. Focus on pushing down through your hands and maintaining a straight back.
  4. One-Leg Extended L-Sit: From the tucked position, extend one leg fully while keeping the other tucked. Alternate legs to build endurance.
  5. Full L-Sit: Extend both legs fully, creating an L shape with your body. Start with short holds (5-10 seconds) and gradually build duration.
  6. Advanced L-Sit Variations: Once you've mastered the basic L-Sit, progress to variations like the V-sit (greater hip flexion angle) or L-sit to tuck planche transitions.

Progress through each stage at your own pace. You might spend weeks or even months at each level before advancing. Consistency is the key to developing the necessary strength and endurance.

How to Perform the L-Sit

  1. 1

    Setup Position

    Begin seated on the floor with legs extended straight in front of you. Place your hands on the floor beside your hips with fingers pointing forward or slightly outward. If using parallel bars or parallettes, grip them firmly at approximately shoulder width.

  2. 2

    Prepare for Lift-Off

    Press your palms firmly into the surface. Engage your core by drawing your navel toward your spine, and prepare to lift your body off the ground. If needed, bend your knees slightly to make the initial lift easier.

  3. 3

    Press and Lift

    Push down through your hands to lift your entire body off the ground. Keep your shoulders down and away from your ears (depressed). Your arms should be straight with elbows locked.

  4. 4

    Extend Your Legs

    Once your hips are lifted, extend your legs fully, pointing your toes. Your body should form an L shape, with your torso vertical and legs horizontal, parallel to the ground. The closer your legs are to parallel with the floor, the more challenging the exercise.

  5. 5

    Hold the Position

    Maintain the position by continuing to push through your hands and keeping your core and hip flexors engaged. Your back should remain straight, not rounded. Hold for the desired duration, starting with 5-10 seconds and building up over time.

  6. 6

    Breathing Technique

    Breathe normally throughout the hold, avoiding the tendency to hold your breath. This exercise requires significant muscular tension, but consistent breathing is critical for maintaining the position longer.

  7. 7

    Controlled Exit

    To exit the position, either lower your feet back to the ground in a controlled manner or transition to a tucked position first, then lower your feet. Avoid collapsing or dropping out of the position to prevent strain on your shoulders and wrists.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Push the shoulders down: A common cue is to "push the shoulders away from the ears" to maintain proper shoulder depression throughout the hold.
  • Create tension: Generate full-body tension by pointing your toes, squeezing your quads, and engaging your core throughout the movement.
  • Look forward: Keep your gaze forward or slightly down, not up, to maintain proper neck alignment with your spine.
  • Prioritize position over duration: It's better to hold a proper L-sit for 5 seconds than a poor form version for 20 seconds. Quality over quantity.
  • Use compression: Think about actively compressing your body by pulling your legs toward your torso while simultaneously pushing your torso away with your arms.
  • Practice scapular depression: Before attempting the full L-sit, practice shoulder depression by trying to push your shoulders down away from your ears while supporting your weight.
  • Hollow body position: Maintain a slight posterior pelvic tilt (tailbone tucked under) to activate your lower abs and protect your lower back.
  • Progressive training: If the full L-sit is too challenging, work on the progression exercises mentioned earlier and gradually build up your time in each position.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Hunched shoulders: Allowing shoulders to rise toward your ears creates excessive tension in the neck and reduces stability. Keep them pressed down throughout the exercise.
  • Insufficient hip flexor strength: Many beginners struggle because they lack the hip flexor strength to keep legs parallel to the ground. Address this with targeted hip flexor exercises like leg raises.
  • Bent knees: While acceptable during progression work, in a full L-sit, the legs should be completely straight with engaged quads.
  • Rounding the back: Excessive rounding of the back places strain on the spine and reduces core engagement. Maintain a relatively straight back position.
  • Flared elbows: Allowing elbows to bend or flare outward reduces stability and can lead to injury. Keep arms straight with elbows locked.
  • Holding breath: Tension often causes people to hold their breath, which limits hold duration and increases blood pressure. Focus on regular breathing.
  • Poor wrist preparation: The L-sit places significant strain on the wrists. Without proper preparation and strengthening, this can lead to wrist pain or injury.

L-Sit Variations

Floor L-Sit

Performed directly on the floor, requiring greater shoulder depression strength and making it more challenging than on raised surfaces.

V-Sit

An advanced progression where legs are raised higher than parallel, creating a V shape with the upper body, requiring greater core and hip flexor strength.

L-Sit on Rings

Performing the L-sit on gymnastic rings adds significant instability, requiring greater stabilization strength in the shoulders and core.

L-Sit Pull-Up

Combining an L-sit with a pull-up by maintaining the L position throughout a hanging pull-up movement, dramatically increases intensity.
