Push-Up Variations

Master the ultimate bodyweight exercise with progressions for every fitness level

Chest, Shoulders, Triceps, Core
Beginner to Advanced
Bodyweight, No Equipment Needed

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Pectoralis Major (Chest), Anterior Deltoids (Shoulders)
  • Secondary Muscles: Triceps, Serratus Anterior, Core, Trapezius
  • Equipment: None (some variations may use household items)
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: All levels (various progressions available)
  • Mechanics: Push
  • Force: Push (Horizontal)

How to Perform the Standard Push-Up

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Begin in a high plank position with your hands placed slightly wider than shoulder-width apart, fingers pointing forward. Your body should form a straight line from head to heels. Position your shoulders directly above your wrists.

  2. 2

    Bracing

    Engage your core by drawing your navel toward your spine. Squeeze your glutes and thighs to maintain a rigid body position. Think about creating full-body tension.

  3. 3

    Lowering Phase

    Inhale as you bend your elbows to lower your body toward the floor. Keep your elbows at about a 45-degree angle from your torso (not flared out to the sides). Lower until your chest is about an inch from the ground.

  4. 4

    Bottom Position

    Pause briefly at the bottom position with your chest near the floor. Maintain full-body tension and avoid letting your hips sag or pike upward.

  5. 5

    Pushing Phase

    Exhale as you push through your palms to straighten your arms and return to the starting position. Think about pushing the floor away from you rather than just pushing your body up.

  6. 6

    Top Position

    Return to the starting high plank position with arms fully extended but not locked. Maintain the straight line from head to heels.

  7. 7

    Repeat

    Perform the desired number of repetitions while maintaining proper form throughout the set.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Body alignment: Maintain a straight line from head to heels throughout the entire movement. Don't let your hips sag or pike up.
  • Hand placement: Position your hands slightly wider than shoulder-width for standard push-ups. Adjust hand position based on the specific variation you're performing.
  • Elbow angle: Keep your elbows at approximately a 45-degree angle from your body, not flared out wide, to protect your shoulders.
  • Head position: Keep your neck in a neutral position by looking at a spot on the floor about a foot in front of your hands. Avoid dropping your head or craning your neck up.
  • Breathing: Inhale during the lowering phase and exhale during the push-up phase. Never hold your breath.
  • Core engagement: Keep your core tight throughout the entire movement to stabilize your spine and prevent lower back arching.
  • Full range of motion: Lower your chest to about an inch from the floor and fully extend your arms at the top (without locking elbows) for maximum benefit.
  • Tempo: Control the movement, especially the lowering phase. Avoid rushing or using momentum.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Sagging hips: Allowing your hips to drop below the straight line of your body puts strain on your lower back and reduces core engagement.
  • Piking hips: Raising your hips too high creates a V-shape with your body and reduces the work on your chest and shoulders.
  • Flaring elbows: Keeping your elbows at a 90-degree angle from your body places excessive stress on your shoulder joints.
  • Incomplete range of motion: Not lowering deep enough or fully extending at the top cheats you out of full muscle development.
  • Hand placement too wide or narrow: Improper hand placement can strain joints and reduce effectiveness for the target muscles.
  • Shrugging shoulders: Allowing your shoulders to rise toward your ears creates tension in the neck and reduces stability.
  • Looking up: Craning your neck upward disrupts the neutral spine position and can cause neck strain.
  • Holding breath: Failing to breathe properly reduces oxygen to working muscles and increases internal pressure.

Push-Up Variations

Incline Push-Up

Performed with hands elevated on a stable surface like a bench or counter. Ideal for beginners as it reduces the amount of bodyweight you're lifting.

Decline Push-Up

Performed with feet elevated on a bench or step. This increases the load on the upper chest and front deltoids while making the exercise more challenging.

Diamond Push-Up

Hands placed close together forming a diamond shape under your chest. This variation places greater emphasis on the triceps.

Wide Push-Up

Hands placed significantly wider than shoulder-width, emphasizing the chest muscles more than the standard push-up.

Plyometric Push-Up

Explosive push-up where your hands leave the ground at the top of the movement. Excellent for building power and explosiveness.

Staggered Push-Up

One hand positioned farther forward than the other, creating an asymmetrical load that challenges your stability and works each side differently.

Single-Arm Push-Up

Advanced variation performed with one arm, requiring exceptional strength, balance, and core stability.

Push-Up with Rotation

Adding a rotational component at the top of the movement by reaching one arm toward the ceiling, engaging the core and improving shoulder mobility.

Push-Up Progression Path for Beginners

If you're new to fitness or currently unable to perform a full push-up, follow this progression path to build strength gradually:

  • Wall Push-Ups: Stand facing a wall with feet shoulder-width apart, place hands on the wall at shoulder height, and perform push-ups against the wall. This is the easiest variation.
  • Incline Push-Ups: Use a sturdy elevated surface like a desk, counter, or bench. The higher the surface, the easier the exercise. Lower the height as you get stronger.
  • Knee Push-Ups: Perform push-ups with your knees on the ground instead of being in a full plank position. Keep your back straight and core engaged.
  • Negative Push-Ups: Start at the top of a push-up position, then lower yourself as slowly as possible to the ground. Return to the starting position by placing your knees down and pushing up.
  • Full Push-Ups: The standard push-up as described in this guide. Start with small sets and gradually increase repetitions as you build strength.

Remember that consistent practice is key to progress. Even if you can only do a few repetitions, regular practice will lead to improvement over time.

Incorporating Push-Ups Into HIIT Workouts

Push-ups are perfect for HIIT (High-Intensity Interval Training) due to their versatility and effectiveness. Here are ways to include push-up variations in your HIIT sessions:

  • Tabata Push-Ups: Perform push-ups for 20 seconds at maximum effort, rest for 10 seconds, and repeat for 8 rounds (4 minutes total).
  • Push-Up Intervals: Alternate between 30 seconds of push-ups and 30 seconds of a different exercise (like jumping jacks or mountain climbers) for 10-20 minutes.
  • Push-Up Ladder: Start with 10 push-ups, rest 15 seconds, do 9 push-ups, rest 15 seconds, and continue counting down to 1.
  • Push-Up Variation Circuit: Perform 30 seconds each of standard push-ups, diamond push-ups, wide push-ups, and decline push-ups with minimal rest between variations.
  • EMOM (Every Minute On the Minute): At the start of each minute, perform 8-15 push-ups (based on your fitness level), then rest for the remainder of the minute before starting the next set.
  • Push-Up Challenge: Set a target number (like 50 or 100) and complete that many push-ups as quickly as possible, resting only when necessary.

For maximum effectiveness in HIIT, focus on maintaining proper form even when fatigued, and choose push-up variations that match your current fitness level.
