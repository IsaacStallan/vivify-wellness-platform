Lunges

A unilateral lower body exercise that builds strength, balance, and stability while targeting multiple muscle groups

Quads, Glutes, Hamstrings
Beginner
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Quadriceps, Glutes, Hamstrings
  • Secondary Muscles: Calves, Core, Hip Flexors, Adductors
  • Equipment: None Required (Bodyweight)
  • Type: Compound, Unilateral
  • Experience Level: Beginner
  • Mechanics: Lower Body Push/Pull
  • Force: Push (Vertical/Horizontal)

How to Perform Lunges

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand upright with your feet hip-width apart. Keep your shoulders back, chest up, and core engaged. Your arms can rest at your sides or be placed on your hips for balance.

  2. 2

    Step Forward

    Take a controlled step forward with your right foot, landing heel first and then rolling onto the ball of your foot. The step should be long enough that your knee will form a 90-degree angle in the next step.

  3. 3

    Lower Your Body

    Lower your body by bending both knees simultaneously until your right thigh is parallel to the ground and your right knee is positioned directly above your ankle. Your left knee should lower toward the floor without touching it, creating a 90-degree angle.

  4. 4

    Bottom Position

    At the bottom of the movement, your torso should remain upright, with your core engaged. Your front foot is flat on the ground, while the back foot is balancing on the ball of the foot with the heel raised.

  5. 5

    Push Back Up

    Push through the heel of your front foot to drive upward, extending both knees to return to the starting position. Focus on engaging your quads and glutes during this upward phase.

  6. 6

    Alternate Legs

    For forward lunges, return to the starting position completely, then repeat the movement with your left leg stepping forward. Continue alternating legs for the desired number of repetitions.

  7. 7

    Breathing Pattern

    Inhale as you step forward and lower into the lunge position. Exhale as you push back up to the starting position. Maintaining proper breathing helps stabilize your core during the movement.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Maintain alignment: Keep your front knee aligned with your ankle, not pushing forward over your toes, to prevent excess strain on the knee joint.
  • Posture check: Keep your chest up, shoulders back, and spine neutral throughout the movement. Avoid leaning forward or hunching.
  • Step length: Your step should be long enough to create proper angles but not so long that you lose balance. Adjust as needed based on your height and mobility.
  • Core engagement: Keep your core muscles braced throughout the exercise to maintain stability and protect your lower back.
  • Control the descent: Lower yourself with control rather than dropping quickly, which maximizes muscle engagement and reduces injury risk.
  • Weight distribution: In the bottom position, your weight should be evenly distributed between both legs, with emphasis on the heel of the front foot.
  • Hip position: Keep your hips square throughout the movement. Don't let them rotate or tilt to one side.
  • Gaze direction: Look straight ahead or slightly downward, keeping your neck in a neutral position aligned with your spine.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Knee collapsing inward: The front knee should track in line with the foot, not collapse inward, which can stress the knee joint.
  • Front knee extending past toes: This places excessive stress on the knee. Adjust your stance length if this occurs.
  • Leaning too far forward: Keep your torso upright to maintain proper balance and engagement of the target muscles.
  • Rising onto toes: The front foot should remain flat, with weight primarily on the heel during the push phase.
  • Insufficient depth: Not lowering far enough reduces the effectiveness of the exercise. Aim for thigh parallel to ground unless limited by mobility.
  • Dropping back knee too hard: Lower the back knee with control, without letting it slam into the ground.
  • Taking too small a step: A step that's too short makes it difficult to achieve proper form and reduces the exercise's effectiveness.

Lunge Variations

Reverse Lunge

Step backward instead of forward, which is often easier on the knees and helps maintain better balance for beginners.

Walking Lunges

Continuously moving forward by stepping the back leg directly into the next lunge, creating a walking motion.

Lateral Lunge

Stepping to the side rather than forward or backward, targeting the inner and outer thighs more intensely.

Jumping Lunge

Adding an explosive jump to switch legs in mid-air, significantly increasing intensity and cardiovascular demand.
