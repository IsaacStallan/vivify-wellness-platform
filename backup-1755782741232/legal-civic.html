Your Civic Duty: Voting in Australia

Voting is compulsory in Australia once you turn 18. Here's everything you need to know about participating in Australian democracy.

1

Enrol to Vote

You must enrol with the Australian Electoral Commission (AEC) within 28 days of turning 18 or changing address.

2

Understand the System

Learn about preferential voting, how to number your ballot correctly, and the difference between House and Senate voting.

3

Vote on Election Day

Attend your designated polling place or vote early/by post if eligible. Voting is usually on Saturdays with "democracy sausages"!

Did You Know?

Failure to vote without a valid excuse can result in a fine. First-time offenders typically receive a $20 penalty notice, which can increase if not paid.

Renting Rights & Responsibilities

Whether you're in student accommodation, sharing a house, or renting independently, know your rights and responsibilities.

Before You Move In

Always inspect the property thoroughly, document any existing damage with photos, ensure you receive all keys and access codes, and understand your lease terms including break clauses.

Bond & Rent

Your bond (usually 4 weeks' rent) must be lodged with the relevant state authority. Rent can only be increased with proper notice, and you have rights regarding payment methods.

Repairs & Maintenance

Landlords must maintain the property in good repair. You're responsible for minor issues and keeping the property clean. Report urgent repairs immediately.

Sharehouses

Understand if you're on the lease or subletting. Know who's responsible for bills, cleaning, and guest policies. Get agreements in writing to avoid disputes.

Disputes

If issues arise, try to resolve them directly first. Each state has a tenancy tribunal for formal disputes. Keep records of all communications and photos.

Moving Out

Give proper notice (usually 21-28 days), clean thoroughly, attend the final inspection, and ensure utilities are disconnected or transferred.

Know Your Workplace Rights

Whether you're working part-time, casual, or doing internships, Australian employment law protects you.

Pay & Entitlements

  • Minimum wage (currently $23.23/hour)
  • Penalty rates for weekends/nights
  • Superannuation (11% of earnings)
  • Annual leave (if permanent)
  • Sick leave entitlements

Working Hours

  • Maximum 38 hours per week (ordinary)
  • Overtime pay for extra hours
  • Proper meal breaks
  • Rest periods between shifts
  • Reasonable notice of roster changes

Workplace Safety

  • Safe working environment
  • Proper training and equipment
  • Right to refuse unsafe work
  • Workers' compensation if injured
  • Report hazards without penalty

Fair Treatment

  • Protection from discrimination
  • Harassment-free workplace
  • Right to join a union
  • Proper dismissal procedures
  • Equal opportunity employment

Red Flags to Watch For

Unpaid trials over 1-2 hours, cash-only payments, no payslips, excessive unpaid overtime, unsafe working conditions, or discrimination. Contact Fair Work Australia if you experience these issues.

Special Information for International Students

International students have additional rights and obligations under Australian law:

  • Visa Compliance: Must maintain full-time study and work restrictions (usually 48 hours per fortnight)
  • Overseas Student Health Cover (OSHC): Mandatory health insurance requirement
  • Consumer Protection: Protected by Australian Consumer Law like all residents
  • Legal Support: Entitled to legal aid and interpretation services
  • Employment Rights: Same workplace protections as Australian students
  • Education Standards: Protected under the Education Services for Overseas Students (ESOS) Act

Need Help? Contact your university's international student services or the Department of Home Affairs.

Student Legal "First Aid Kit"

What to do in common legal situations students face:

If You're in an Accident

1. Ensure safety and call 000 if needed
2. Exchange details with other parties
3. Take photos of damage and scene
4. Contact your insurance company
5. Don't admit fault or sign anything

If Questioned by Police

1. You have the right to remain silent
2. Ask "Am I under arrest?"
3. Provide name and address if required
4. Ask for a lawyer before answering questions
5. Don't resist even if you disagree

If You're Scammed

1. Stop all contact with the scammer
2. Contact your bank immediately
3. Report to Scamwatch
4. Keep all evidence (emails, receipts)
5. Consider reporting to police

Workplace Issues

1. Document everything (dates, witnesses)
2. Try to resolve with supervisor first
3. Contact Fair Work Australia
4. Consider union support
5. Seek legal advice if serious

Rental Disputes

1. Put complaints in writing
2. Keep copies of all communications
3. Know your state's tenancy laws
4. Contact tenancy advice service
5. Apply to tenancy tribunal if needed

University Issues

1. Review university policies
2. Contact student advocacy service
3. Follow internal appeals process
4. Keep detailed records
5. Seek external review if necessary

Stay Informed, Stay Protected

Knowledge of your rights and responsibilities is your best protection. Stay informed about changes to laws that affect students.
