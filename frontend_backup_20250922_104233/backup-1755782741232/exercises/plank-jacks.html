Plank Jacks

A dynamic plank variation that combines core stability with cardio benefits for a full-body challenge

Core, Cardio, Shoulders
Intermediate
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Core, Shoulders, Chest
  • Secondary Muscles: Hip Flexors, Quads, Glutes, Triceps
  • Equipment: None
  • Type: Compound, Cardio
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Dynamic Plank
  • Force: Push (Isometric), Hip Abduction/Adduction

How to Perform Plank Jacks

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Begin in a high plank position with your hands directly beneath your shoulders, slightly wider than shoulder-width apart. Your body should form a straight line from your head to your heels.

  2. 2

    Core Engagement

    Engage your core by drawing your navel toward your spine. Ensure your shoulders are stacked over your wrists and your glutes are engaged to maintain proper spinal alignment.

  3. 3

    Jump Feet Out

    While maintaining the plank position with your upper body, jump or step your feet outward wider than hip-width apart, similar to the motion of a jumping jack.

  4. 4

    Jump Feet In

    Immediately jump or step your feet back together to return to the starting position, keeping your core engaged and body in a straight line throughout the movement.

  5. 5

    Maintain Upper Body Stability

    Throughout the exercise, focus on keeping your upper body as still as possible. Your shoulders, hips, and spine should remain in the same position without sagging or piking.

  6. 6

    Establish Rhythm

    Continue the in-and-out jumping motion with your legs at a controlled pace. Start with a slower tempo to master the movement, then gradually increase speed as your form and conditioning improve.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Maintain a neutral spine: Keep your back flat throughout the exercise, avoiding any sagging in the lower back or piking of the hips.
  • Distribute weight properly: Balance your weight between your hands and toes, ensuring your shoulders don't collapse forward.
  • Control your breathing: Establish a rhythm of breathing that works with the movement—many find exhaling as the feet jump out and inhaling as they return works well.
  • Focus on core activation: Keep your abdominals engaged throughout the entire exercise to protect your lower back and maintain stability.
  • Start with stepping: If jumping is too challenging, begin by stepping your feet out and in instead, gradually progressing to jumping as strength improves.
  • Mind your shoulder position: Keep your shoulders pulled back and down away from your ears to prevent unnecessary tension in your neck.
  • Modify the width: Adjust how wide you jump your feet based on your hip mobility and stability. Wider isn't always better—focus on what you can control.
  • Land softly: When jumping your feet, aim to land as quietly as possible to reduce impact and maintain control of the movement.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Sagging hips: Allowing your hips to drop toward the floor reduces core engagement and puts stress on the lower back. Keep your body in a straight line from head to heels.
  • Piking hips: Raising your hips too high creates an inverted V-shape that reduces the effectiveness of the exercise and shifts focus away from the core.
  • Hands too far forward: Positioning your hands too far in front of your shoulders creates an inefficient plank and increases shoulder strain. Keep hands directly under shoulders.
  • Bouncing or jerky movements: Using momentum rather than controlled movement reduces the effectiveness and increases injury risk. Focus on smooth, deliberate movements.
  • Holding breath: Forgetting to breathe regularly increases perceived exertion and creates unnecessary tension. Establish a consistent breathing pattern.
  • Excessive foot width: Jumping the feet too wide can compromise your form and put stress on the hips. Use a comfortable width that allows you to maintain proper form.
  • Shoulder shrugging: Allowing shoulders to rise toward ears creates neck tension and reduces stabilization. Keep shoulders down and away from ears.

Plank Jack Variations

Forearm Plank Jacks

Performed in a forearm plank position instead of a high plank, reducing the load on the wrists while maintaining core challenge.

Plank Jack + Push-Up

Combines a plank jack with a push-up for added upper body strength work and increased intensity.

Elevated Plank Jacks

Performed with hands on an elevated surface (bench, step, etc.) to reduce intensity, making it more accessible for beginners.

Plank Jack Toe Taps

After jumping feet out, tap one foot with the opposite hand before jumping feet back in, adding rotation and increased coordination challenge.
