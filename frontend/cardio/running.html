Running & Jogging

The fundamental forms of cardiovascular exercise that improve endurance, burn calories, and enhance overall health

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Benefit: Cardiovascular Endurance
  • Muscles Worked: Quadriceps, Hamstrings, Calves, Glutes, Core
  • Equipment: Running shoes, comfortable clothing
  • Type: Cardiovascular, Aerobic
  • Experience Level: All Levels (Adaptable)
  • Calorie Burn: ~100-700 calories per hour (varies by intensity, terrain, weight)
  • Impact Level: Moderate to High

How to Run with Proper Form

  1. 1

    Preparation

    Wear appropriate running shoes that provide proper support and cushioning. Dress in comfortable, weather-appropriate clothing. For longer runs, consider bringing water or an electrolyte drink.

  2. 2

    Starting Position

    Stand tall with your feet hip-width apart. Keep your shoulders relaxed and down, chest slightly forward, and gaze directed ahead. Engage your core slightly to maintain good posture.

  3. 3

    Warm-Up

    Begin with 5-10 minutes of brisk walking, gradually transitioning to a slow jog. Include dynamic stretches like leg swings, high knees, and butt kicks to prepare your muscles for the activity.

  4. 4

    Arm Movement

    Bend your arms at approximately 90 degrees and swing them naturally from your shoulders, not your elbows. Keep your hands relaxed (not clenched), and swing arms forward and back (not across your body).

  5. 5

    Foot Strike

    Aim for a midfoot strike, landing between your heel and the ball of your foot. Avoid heavy heel striking or excessive toe running. Your foot should land under your body, not far out in front of you.

  6. 6

    Stride & Cadence

    Take shorter, quicker strides rather than long, reaching ones. Aim for approximately 170-180 steps per minute for efficient running. Focus on picking your feet up rather than pushing off forcefully.

  7. 7

    Breathing

    Breathe rhythmically, ideally through both your nose and mouth. For beginners, try inhaling for 2-3 steps and exhaling for 2-3 steps. Adjust your breathing pattern based on intensity and comfort.

  8. 8

    Cool Down

    After completing your run, gradually reduce your pace to a jog and then a walk for 5-10 minutes. Follow with static stretches, holding each stretch for 20-30 seconds to improve flexibility and reduce muscle tightness.

Running & Jogging Tips

  • Start slowly: If you're new to running, begin with a run/walk approach. Try 1 minute running followed by 1-2 minutes walking, and gradually increase your running intervals.
  • Progressive overload: Increase your distance by no more than 10% per week to avoid overuse injuries and allow proper adaptation.
  • Surface awareness: Varied surfaces affect impact. Grass and trails are lower impact but more challenging for stability; asphalt is moderate; concrete is highest impact.
  • Hydration: Hydrate before, during (for runs over 45 minutes), and after your run. In hot weather, consider electrolyte replacement drinks.
  • Run by effort: Focus on perceived exertion rather than pace, especially when starting out. You should be able to hold a conversation during easy runs.
  • Track your progress: Use a running app, watch, or simple journal to track your distances, times, and how you feel to monitor improvements.
  • Rest days: Schedule at least 1-2 rest days per week to allow your body to recover and adapt to the training stimulus.
  • Cross-train: Include other activities like swimming, cycling, or strength training to build complementary fitness and reduce injury risk.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Overstriding: Reaching too far forward with each step increases impact forces and can lead to injuries. Focus on landing with your foot under your center of mass.
  • Poor posture: Slouching or leaning too far forward/backward reduces efficiency and can cause back pain. Maintain an upright posture with a slight forward lean from the ankles.
  • Doing too much too soon: Rapidly increasing mileage or intensity can lead to overuse injuries like shin splints or stress fractures. Follow the 10% rule for weekly increases.
  • Ignoring pain: Pushing through sharp or persistent pain can worsen injuries. Learn to distinguish between normal muscle fatigue and potential injury pain.
  • Neglecting strength training: Relying solely on running without complementary strength work can lead to muscle imbalances and increased injury risk.
  • Heel striking with straight leg: Landing heavily on your heel with your leg fully extended creates excessive impact forces. Work on landing with a slightly bent knee and midfoot strike.
  • Improper shoes: Using worn-out shoes or shoes that don't match your foot type and running style can contribute to injuries. Get fitted at a specialty running store.

Running & Jogging Variations

Interval Training

Alternating between periods of high-intensity running and recovery to improve speed, power, and cardiovascular fitness.

Trail Running

Running on natural terrain like forests, mountains, or parks, which improves balance, strengthens different muscle groups, and provides mental refreshment.

Tempo Run

Running at a "comfortably hard" pace that's just below your lactate threshold, ideal for improving sustained speed and endurance.

Hill Sprints

Short, intense uphill runs that build power, strength, and speed while reducing impact compared to flat-ground sprinting.
