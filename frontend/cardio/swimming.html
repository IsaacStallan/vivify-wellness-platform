Swimming

A full-body, low-impact cardiovascular exercise that builds endurance, muscle strength, and improves overall fitness

Cardiovascular, Full Body
Beginner to Advanced
Pool Access, Swimwear, Optional: Goggles

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Benefit: Cardiovascular Endurance, Muscle Strength
  • Muscles Worked: Shoulders, Back, Core, Legs, Arms (Full Body)
  • Equipment: Swimming Pool, Swimsuit, Optional: Goggles, Swim Cap
  • Type: Cardiovascular, Resistance Training
  • Experience Level: All Levels (Adaptable)
  • Calorie Burn: ~400-700 calories per hour (varies by stroke, intensity)
  • Impact Level: Very Low

How to Swim Freestyle (Front Crawl)

  1. 1

    Preparation

    Wear a comfortable swimsuit that allows for freedom of movement. Goggles are recommended to protect your eyes and improve visibility. If you have long hair, consider using a swim cap to reduce drag. Start in shallow water where you can stand if needed.

  2. 2

    Body Position

    Float face down in the water with your body horizontal and parallel to the bottom of the pool. Your legs should be extended behind you, and your head should be in line with your spine, with your face in the water looking at the bottom of the pool. Your hips and legs should be close to the surface.

  3. 3

    Breathing Technique

    Turn your head to the side to breathe, not lifting it forward. As one arm recovers (moves over the water), rotate your head to that side until your mouth clears the water, take a quick breath, then return your face to the water as the arm enters. Exhale slowly through your nose or mouth while your face is in the water.

  4. 4

    Arm Stroke

    The arm stroke consists of two phases: the pull and the recovery. For the pull, reach forward underwater with one arm, palm facing down and slightly outward. Pull your hand back toward your hip in an S-shaped pattern. For recovery, lift your elbow high and swing your arm forward above the water in a relaxed manner to begin the next stroke.

  5. 5

    Kick Technique

    Use a flutter kick, where your legs move up and down in an alternating pattern. The movement should originate from your hips with relatively straight knees. Keep your ankles relaxed and feet slightly pointed. The kick should be continuous and create minimal splash.

  6. 6

    Coordination

    Coordinate your arms to move in an alternating pattern - as one arm pulls underwater, the other recovers above water. Most swimmers use a bilateral breathing pattern (breathing every third arm stroke) to maintain balance, though beginners may breathe every two strokes (to the same side) until comfortable.

  7. 7

    Body Rotation

    Rotate your body along its long axis with each arm stroke. As your right arm pulls, your body should rotate slightly to the right, and vice versa. This rotation helps with breathing and engages your core muscles for a more efficient stroke.

  8. 8

    Practice Progression

    Start with shorter distances and focus on technique rather than speed or distance. Begin with drills that isolate specific components (like kicking drills using a kickboard) before putting everything together. Gradually increase distance as your technique and endurance improve.

Swimming Tips

  • Focus on technique first: Good technique will make you more efficient and allow you to swim longer distances with less fatigue.
  • Practice breathing: Many beginners struggle with breathing. Practice turning your head to breathe rather than lifting it forward to maintain proper body position.
  • Use swimming drills: Incorporate specific drills like catch-up drill (waiting for one arm to complete its stroke before starting the other) to improve technique.
  • Maintain a streamlined position: Keep your body as straight and long as possible to reduce drag in the water.
  • Progressive training: Gradually increase your distance and intensity over time. Use interval training (e.g., 4 x 50m with rest between each) to build endurance.
  • Use equipment for variety: Kickboards, pull buoys, and fins can help isolate different aspects of your stroke for improvement.
  • Hydrate properly: Even though you're surrounded by water, you still sweat while swimming. Drink water before and after your swim.
  • Take swim lessons: Consider taking formal lessons if you're a beginner or want to refine your technique - a coach can provide valuable feedback.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Lifting your head too high: Looking forward instead of down causes your hips and legs to sink, creating drag and making swimming more difficult. Keep your head in line with your spine.
  • Improper breathing technique: Holding your breath underwater instead of continuously exhaling leads to oxygen debt. Practice exhaling slowly and continuously while your face is in the water.
  • Crossing over the midline: When your hand crosses the centerline of your body during the pull, it creates a zigzag motion that wastes energy. Focus on keeping your arm stroke straight.
  • Kicking too much: Excessive kicking uses significant energy for minimal propulsion gain. Develop an efficient, relaxed kick that helps maintain body position without exhausting your legs.
  • Not fully extending: Failing to reach forward and back completely in each stroke reduces your distance per stroke. Focus on elongating your body and reaching forward with each stroke.
  • Dropping your elbow: Allowing your elbow to drop during the underwater pull reduces power. Keep your elbow higher than your hand during the pull phase.
  • Swimming too fast too soon: Rushing through your workout leads to poor technique and early fatigue. Start at a comfortable pace that allows you to maintain proper form.

Swimming Stroke Variations

Backstroke

Swimming on your back with alternating arm strokes and flutter kicks. Great for improving posture and relieving stress on the neck and shoulders.

Breaststroke

A symmetrical stroke with arm and leg movements that resemble a frog. Excellent for building chest, shoulder, and inner thigh strength.

Butterfly

An advanced stroke with simultaneous arm movements and a dolphin-like kick. Builds significant upper body strength and aerobic capacity.

Sidestroke

A restful stroke performed on your side with asymmetrical arm and leg movements. Good for long-distance swimming and water rescue situations.
