Understanding Credit Scores

In Australia, credit scores range from 0 to 1,200 and indicate your creditworthiness to lenders. Here's how the scoring system works:

833-1200
Excellent
Best rates and terms available. Lenders compete for your business.
726-832
Very Good
Access to most credit products with favorable terms.
622-725
Good
Generally approved with reasonable rates and terms.
510-621
Average
May face higher rates or require additional documentation.
0-509
Below Average
Limited credit options, high rates, or may be declined.

Free Credit Score Access

You can check your credit score for free through services like Credit Savvy, Credit Simple, or directly from Equifax. Check it regularly but remember that checking your own score doesn't hurt your credit.

What Affects Your Credit Score

Payment History

35%

Your track record of making payments on time is the most important factor. Even one late payment can significantly impact your score.

How to Improve:

  • Set up automatic payments for at least the minimum amount
  • Pay bills before due dates, not on them
  • If you miss a payment, pay it ASAP - the sooner, the better
  • Contact creditors immediately if you can't make a payment

Credit Utilization

30%

How much of your available credit you're using. Keep this below 30% of your credit limit, ideally under 10% for the best scores.

How to Improve:

  • Pay down existing balances
  • Make multiple payments per month
  • Request credit limit increases (but don't use the extra credit)
  • Keep old cards open to maintain available credit

Length of Credit History

15%

How long your accounts have been open. Older accounts show stability and help your score, which is why starting early matters.

How to Improve:

  • Start building credit early (student cards are perfect)
  • Keep your oldest credit card open and active
  • Use old cards occasionally to keep them active
  • Avoid closing accounts unless necessary

Credit Mix

10%

Having different types of credit (credit cards, student loans, etc.) shows you can manage various credit products responsibly.

How to Improve:

  • Don't open accounts just for credit mix
  • If you have student loans, ensure they're being reported
  • Consider a mix of revolving (credit cards) and installment (loans) credit
  • Focus on payment history and utilization first

New Credit Inquiries

10%

Hard inquiries from credit applications can temporarily lower your score. Multiple inquiries in a short time can be concerning to lenders.

How to Improve:

  • Only apply for credit when you need it
  • Research cards before applying
  • Rate shopping for loans (car, home) within 14-45 days counts as one inquiry
  • Avoid applying for multiple credit cards in a short period

How to Build Credit From Scratch

Follow these proven steps to establish and build excellent credit as a student.

1

Get a Student Credit Card

Apply for a student credit card designed for people with no credit history. These cards have lower requirements and help establish your credit file.

2

Make Small Purchases

Use your card for small, regular purchases like groceries or subscriptions. This shows consistent usage without overspending.

3

Pay in Full, On Time

Always pay your full balance before the due date. Set up automatic payments to ensure you never miss a payment.

4

Keep Utilization Low

Use less than 30% of your credit limit, ideally under 10%. If your limit is $1,000, keep balances under $100.

5

Monitor Your Credit

Check your credit score monthly and review your credit report annually. Look for errors and dispute any inaccuracies immediately.

6

Gradually Build Up

After 6-12 months of responsible use, request credit limit increases or consider additional credit products to diversify your credit mix.

Credit Mistakes That Can Destroy Your Score

Avoid these common mistakes that can take years to recover from.

Missing Payments

Even one missed payment can drop your score by 50-100 points and stay on your credit report for 7 years.

Impact: Major score drop, difficulty getting approved for future credit, higher interest rates on existing accounts.

Maxing Out Credit Cards

Using all your available credit signals financial distress to lenders and can severely impact your score.

Impact: Immediate score drop, reduced chances of approval, potential credit limit decreases.

Closing Old Credit Cards

Closing your oldest credit card reduces your credit history length and available credit, both harmful to your score.

Impact: Shorter average account age, higher utilization ratio, reduced credit diversity.

Too Many Credit Applications

Applying for multiple credit cards in a short period creates multiple hard inquiries and suggests credit hunger.

Impact: Multiple score drops, appears desperate for credit, reduced approval odds.

Only Making Minimum Payments

While not immediately harmful to your score, carrying balances costs enormous amounts in interest over time.

Impact: Thousands in unnecessary interest, prolonged debt, higher utilization ratios.

Ignoring Your Credit Report

Not monitoring your credit means errors, fraud, or identity theft can go undetected for months or years.

Impact: Undetected fraud, incorrect information hurting score, missed opportunities to improve.

Expert Credit Tips for Students

Start Early, Start Small

The best time to start building credit is now, even if you can only handle a $500 credit limit. Time is your biggest advantage in building excellent credit.

Treat Credit Like Cash

Only charge what you can afford to pay off immediately. If you wouldn't buy it with cash, don't put it on your credit card.

Set Up Automatic Payments

Automate at least the minimum payment to ensure you never miss a due date. You can always pay more manually if needed.

Use Credit for Regular Expenses

Put recurring bills like phone or Netflix on your credit card, then pay them off immediately. This builds credit without changing your spending habits.

Ask for Credit Limit Increases

After 6 months of responsible use, request credit limit increases. This improves your utilization ratio without changing your spending.

Keep Old Cards Active

Use your oldest credit card occasionally for small purchases to keep it active. The length of your credit history is crucial for your score.

Credit Card Debt Warning

Credit card debt can spiral out of control quickly due to high interest rates. If you carry a $2,000 balance and only make minimum payments at 19.74% APR, you'll pay over $1,000 in interest and take 8+ years to pay off. Always pay your full balance when possible!
