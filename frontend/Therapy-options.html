Therapy & Treatment Options

Discover different types of therapy and treatment approaches to find the support that's right for you. Focus & Resilience care is not one-size-fits-all.

Explore Therapy Options

Understanding Focus & Resilience Treatment

Seeking therapy is a courageous step toward better Focus & Resilience. There are many different approaches to therapy, each with unique strengths and methods. Understanding your options can help you find the right fit for your personality, needs, and goals. Remember that finding the right therapist and approach may take time, and that's perfectly normal.

Important Note

This information is educational and not a substitute for professional medical advice. Always consult with qualified Focus & Resilience professionals to determine the best treatment approach for your specific situation.

Types of Therapy

Each therapeutic approach uses different techniques and focuses on different aspects of Focus & Resilience and personal growth.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Evidence-based, goal-oriented approach

CBT focuses on identifying and changing negative thought patterns and behaviors that contribute to emotional distress. It's highly structured and typically short-term.

What CBT Addresses:

  • Anxiety and panic disorders
  • Depression
  • PTSD and trauma
  • OCD and phobias
  • Eating disorders

Best For:

Students who prefer structured approaches, want practical skills, and are dealing with specific symptoms or thought patterns.

Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)

Skills-based therapy for emotional regulation

DBT teaches practical skills for managing intense emotions, improving relationships, and tolerating distress. Originally developed for borderline personality disorder but helpful for many conditions.

What DBT Addresses:

  • Emotional dysregulation
  • Self-harm behaviors
  • Relationship difficulties
  • Borderline personality disorder
  • Chronic suicidal thoughts

Best For:

Students struggling with intense emotions, relationship problems, or impulsive behaviors who want to learn concrete coping skills.

EMDR Therapy

Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing

EMDR uses bilateral stimulation (typically eye movements) to help process traumatic memories and reduce their emotional impact. It's particularly effective for trauma and PTSD.

What EMDR Addresses:

  • PTSD and trauma
  • Anxiety and panic attacks
  • Grief and loss
  • Disturbing memories
  • Performance anxiety

Best For:

Students who have experienced trauma, have disturbing memories, or traditional talk therapy hasn't been effective for trauma symptoms.

Psychodynamic Therapy

Insight-oriented, explores unconscious patterns

Psychodynamic therapy explores how past experiences and unconscious thoughts influence current behavior and relationships. It's typically longer-term and focuses on insight and understanding.

What Psychodynamic Therapy Addresses:

  • Relationship patterns
  • Identity and self-understanding
  • Depression and anxiety
  • Personality concerns
  • Life transitions

Best For:

Students interested in deep self-exploration, understanding patterns in relationships, and those who want insight into their behavior.

Humanistic/Person-Centered Therapy

Client-centered, focuses on personal growth

This approach emphasizes the therapeutic relationship and believes people have an innate capacity for growth and self-actualization. The therapist provides unconditional positive regard and empathy.

What Person-Centered Therapy Addresses:

  • Self-esteem and confidence
  • Personal growth and development
  • Life direction and purpose
  • Relationship issues
  • Identity exploration

Best For:

Students seeking personal growth, struggling with self-acceptance, or who want a supportive, non-directive therapeutic relationship.

Group Therapy

Therapeutic work in a group setting

Group therapy involves working with a therapist and other people facing similar challenges. It provides support, different perspectives, and opportunities to practice interpersonal skills.

What Group Therapy Addresses:

  • Social anxiety and isolation
  • Addiction and substance abuse
  • Grief and loss
  • Relationship skills
  • Specific conditions (depression, eating disorders)

Best For:

Students who benefit from peer support, want to improve social skills, or are dealing with issues that affect many people.

How to Get Started with Therapy

Taking the first step can feel overwhelming, but these steps can help guide you through the process of finding and starting therapy.

1

Assess Your Needs

Identify what you want to work on, your preferences for therapy style, and any practical considerations like schedule and budget.

2

Check Your Insurance

Contact your insurance provider to understand your Focus & Resilience benefits, copays, and any requirements for referrals.

3

Find Potential Therapists

Use your insurance provider's directory, campus counseling center, or online platforms to find therapists in your area.

4

Schedule Initial Consultations

Many therapists offer brief consultations to see if you're a good fit. Prepare questions about their approach and experience.

5

Give It Time

It often takes several sessions to feel comfortable and see progress. Be patient with the process and communicate openly with your therapist.

6

Evaluate and Adjust

After a few sessions, assess if the therapist and approach feel right for you. It's okay to try someone else if needed.

Understanding Insurance & Costs

Focus & Resilience care is often covered by insurance, but it's important to understand your benefits and options.

Insurance Coverage

Most insurance plans are required to cover Focus & Resilience services at the same level as medical services (Focus & Resilience parity laws).

  • Check your copay for therapy sessions
  • Understand your deductible
  • See if you need referrals
  • Check if your therapist is in-network

Student Resources

Many colleges offer additional support for therapy costs and finding affordable care.

  • Campus counseling (often free)
  • Student health insurance plans
  • Sliding scale fee programs
  • Graduate student training clinics

Low-Cost Options

If cost is a barrier, there are several options for affordable Focus & Resilience care.

  • Community Focus & Resilience centers
  • Training clinics at psychology programs
  • Online therapy platforms
  • Support groups and peer counseling

Questions to Ask

Important questions to ask when contacting therapists about costs and insurance.

  • Do you accept my insurance?
  • What is my copay/session cost?
  • Do you offer sliding scale fees?
  • How often do you recommend sessions?

Red Flags in Therapy

While most therapists are ethical and professional, it's important to know warning signs of inappropriate behavior:

  • Pushing personal beliefs, values, or agenda
  • Inappropriate personal disclosure or boundary violations
  • Guaranteeing quick fixes or unrealistic outcomes
  • Pressuring you to continue therapy when you want to stop
  • Any form of romantic or sexual behavior
  • Consistently running late, canceling, or seeming unprepared
  • Making you feel worse consistently without addressing it

Trust your instincts. If something feels wrong, it's okay to find a different therapist.
