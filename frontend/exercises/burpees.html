Burpees

A high-intensity full-body exercise that builds strength, endurance, and cardiovascular fitness

Full Body, Cardio
Intermediate
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Quadriceps, Chest, Shoulders
  • Secondary Muscles: Hamstrings, Glutes, Core, Triceps, Calves
  • Equipment: None
  • Type: Compound, Cardio
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Full Body
  • Force: Push, Pull, Jump

How to Perform Burpees

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart, weight in your heels, and your arms at your sides. Maintain good posture with your spine neutral and core engaged.

  2. 2

    Squat Down

    Lower your body into a squat position by bending your knees and hips. Place your hands on the floor directly in front of your feet, shoulder-width apart or slightly wider.

  3. 3

    Kick Back into Plank

    Kick your feet back to immediately assume a plank position. Your body should form a straight line from your head to your heels, with your arms extended and directly under your shoulders.

  4. 4

    Perform a Push-Up (Optional)

    Keeping your body in a straight line, bend your elbows to lower your chest to the floor and then push back up to the plank position. This step can be skipped for beginners or those building up to the full burpee.

  5. 5

    Jump Feet Forward

    Jump your feet forward, landing them outside your hands or close to your hands in a squat position. Keep your landing soft by bending your knees to absorb the impact.

  6. 6

    Explosive Jump

    Powerfully extend your hips, knees, and ankles to jump vertically into the air. Drive your arms overhead for maximum height. Land softly with knees slightly bent to absorb the impact.

  7. 7

    Land & Repeat

    Land softly back in the standing position with your knees slightly bent. Immediately begin the next repetition by lowering back into the squat position.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Pace yourself: While burpees are often performed quickly, focus on proper form before increasing speed, especially when you're first learning the movement.
  • Engage your core: Keep your core tight throughout the entire movement to protect your lower back and maintain proper body alignment.
  • Control your descent: When transitioning from standing to the squat position, control your descent rather than collapsing downward.
  • Land softly: Whether jumping your feet back, forward, or up, focus on soft landings by absorbing impact through your legs.
  • Breathe rhythmically: Find a breathing pattern that works for you—typically exhaling during exertion (push-up and jump) and inhaling during transitions.
  • Maintain full range of motion: Complete each phase of the movement properly without cutting corners, even as fatigue sets in.
  • Scale appropriately: Modify the exercise based on your fitness level, gradually working up to the full movement.
  • Focus on quality: As with any exercise, quality repetitions are more beneficial than a higher quantity performed poorly.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Sagging or piking hips: When in the plank position, avoid letting your hips sag toward the floor or pike upward. Maintain a straight line from head to heels.
  • Incomplete movements: Skipping components like the full squat, proper plank, or vertical jump reduces effectiveness and can create compensatory movement patterns.
  • Landing with straight legs: Always land with a slight bend in your knees to absorb impact and protect your joints.
  • Hand placement too far forward: Placing your hands too far in front of your feet creates an inefficient transition to the plank position. Hands should be roughly below your shoulders in the squat position.
  • Wrist position: Avoid letting your wrists bend backward excessively in the plank position; keep them directly beneath your shoulders with fingers spread.
  • Rushing between repetitions: Moving too quickly can lead to poor form. Control the movement, especially as fatigue sets in.
  • Poor breathing patterns: Holding your breath or breathing erratically reduces performance and increases perceived exertion. Establish a consistent breathing pattern.

Burpee Variations

Half Burpee

Eliminate the push-up and vertical jump to create a more accessible version that still delivers cardiovascular benefits.

Box Jump Burpee

Replace the vertical jump with a box jump for added power development and increased intensity.

Tuck Jump Burpee

Incorporate a tuck jump (bringing knees toward chest) instead of a regular vertical jump for greater core engagement.

Single-Leg Burpee

Perform the movement with one leg raised throughout to increase core stability demands and unilateral strength.
