Barbell Squat

The king of lower body exercises for building strength, muscle mass, and overall athletic performance

Quadriceps, Glutes, Hamstrings, Core
Intermediate
Barbell, Squat Rack

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Quadriceps, Gluteus Maximus
  • Secondary Muscles: Hamstrings, Adductors, Erector Spinae, Core
  • Equipment: Barbell, Squat Rack/Power Rack
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Multi-joint
  • Force: Push (Lower Body)

How to Perform the Barbell Squat

  1. 1

    Rack Setup

    Set the barbell on a squat rack at approximately upper chest height. Load the appropriate weight plates onto both sides of the barbell, ensuring they're secure with collars if necessary.

  2. 2

    Bar Position

    Step under the bar and position it across your upper back, either across the trapezius muscles (high-bar position) or slightly lower across the posterior deltoids (low-bar position). Grip the bar with hands wider than shoulder-width apart.

  3. 3

    Unracking

    Brace your core, stand up to lift the bar off the rack, and take 2-3 small steps backward to clear the rack. Set your feet slightly wider than shoulder-width apart with toes pointed slightly outward.

  4. 4

    Starting Position

    Stand tall with chest up, shoulders back, and a neutral spine. Distribute your weight evenly across your feet, with pressure through your heels, midfoot, and balls of your feet. Take a deep breath, brace your core, and prepare to descend.

  5. 5

    Descent (Eccentric Phase)

    Initiate the movement by bending at the hips and knees simultaneously, as if sitting back into a chair. Keep your chest up, back straight, and knees tracking in line with your toes. Descend until your thighs are at least parallel to the ground or lower depending on your mobility.

  6. 6

    Bottom Position

    At the bottom of the squat, your hip crease should be below the top of your knee (for a full squat). Maintain tension throughout your body, keeping your core tight and back in a neutral position. Avoid letting your knees cave inward.

  7. 7

    Ascent (Concentric Phase)

    Drive through your heels and midfoot, extending your hips and knees to return to the starting position. Keep your chest up and your back straight throughout the movement. Exhale as you push upward.

  8. 8

    Completing the Rep

    Return to the starting position with hips and knees fully extended, but avoid locking out your knees. Keep your core braced and maintain proper posture before beginning the next repetition.

  9. 9

    Racking the Bar

    After completing your set, carefully walk forward until the bar is over the rack. Bend your knees slightly to lower the bar onto the rack supports, ensuring it's secure before releasing your grip.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Foot position: Find a stance width that feels comfortable for your body proportions, typically shoulder-width or slightly wider. Toes should be pointed slightly outward (around 15-30 degrees).
  • Bar path: The bar should travel in a vertical line over your midfoot throughout the movement. This ensures optimal balance and force transfer.
  • Hip hinge: Initiate the squat by hinging at the hips, as if sitting back into a chair, rather than just bending the knees forward.
  • Knee tracking: Keep your knees aligned with your toes throughout the movement. Avoid letting them collapse inward (knee valgus).
  • Core engagement: Take a deep breath before descending and brace your core as if preparing to take a punch. This creates intra-abdominal pressure that stabilizes your spine.
  • Neck position: Maintain a neutral neck position by looking at a spot on the floor about 6-8 feet in front of you. Avoid excessive looking up or down.
  • Depth consideration: For optimal muscle development and joint health, try to squat until your thighs are at least parallel to the floor. Greater depth is beneficial if your mobility allows it safely.
  • Full-body tension: Create and maintain tension throughout your entire body during the lift. This improves stability and force production.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Knees caving inward: Allowing the knees to collapse medially (valgus) places stress on the knee ligaments and reduces power output. Focus on actively pushing your knees outward in line with your toes.
  • Rounding the lower back: Flexion of the lumbar spine under load increases injury risk. Maintain a neutral spine position throughout the movement by keeping your chest up and core braced.
  • Rising with hips first: Lifting your hips faster than your shoulders creates a "good morning" squat pattern that places excessive stress on the lower back. Focus on driving your shoulders and hips up simultaneously.
  • Heels lifting off the ground: This reduces stability and power transfer while potentially increasing knee stress. Keep your weight distributed across your whole foot, with emphasis on driving through the heels and midfoot.
  • Insufficient depth: Not squatting deep enough limits muscle development and mobility benefits. Aim to reach at least parallel depth (thighs parallel to floor) unless limited by specific mobility restrictions.
  • Looking up excessively: Hyperextending the neck by looking too far upward can strain the cervical spine and disrupt proper alignment. Maintain a neutral head position aligned with your spine.
  • Rushing the eccentric phase: Descending too quickly reduces control and muscle tension. Lower yourself with control, taking 2-3 seconds for the descent.

Squat Variations

Front Squat

The barbell is positioned across the front of the shoulders, placing more emphasis on the quadriceps and requiring greater core stability and upright posture.

Goblet Squat

Using a kettlebell or dumbbell held at chest level, this beginner-friendly variation helps teach proper squat mechanics and depth.

Bulgarian Split Squat

A unilateral variation with the rear foot elevated, which addresses muscle imbalances and increases the stability challenge.

Overhead Squat

Performed with the barbell held overhead throughout the movement, challenging shoulder mobility, core stability, and overall body control.
