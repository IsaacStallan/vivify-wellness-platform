Reverse Curl

A targeted exercise that strengthens the forearms and brachioradialis while enhancing grip strength

Forearms, Brachioradialis, Brachialis
Beginner-Intermediate
Barbell, EZ Bar, or Dumbbells

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Brachioradialis, Brachialis
  • Secondary Muscles: Forearm Extensors, Biceps Brachii (outer head)
  • Equipment: Barbell, EZ Bar, or Dumbbells
  • Type: Isolation
  • Experience Level: Beginner-Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Single Joint
  • Force: Pull (Elbow Flexion)

How to Perform the Reverse Curl

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand upright with feet shoulder-width apart and knees slightly bent. Hold a barbell, EZ bar, or dumbbells with an overhand grip (palms facing down) at shoulder-width or slightly narrower. Let your arms hang fully extended with the weight resting against your thighs.

  2. 2

    Proper Grip

    Ensure your grip is secure but not overly tight. Your hands should be positioned at shoulder width or slightly narrower. The overhand grip (pronated grip) is the key element that differentiates this exercise from standard bicep curls.

  3. 3

    Body Positioning

    Keep your chest up, shoulders back, and maintain a neutral spine. Your elbows should be positioned close to your sides and will serve as the pivot point for the movement. Engage your core to stabilize your upper body.

  4. 4

    Curling Phase

    Exhale as you curl the weight upward by flexing at the elbows, keeping your upper arms stationary against your sides. Focus on contracting the muscles in your forearms and the outer portion of your arms (brachioradialis) to power the movement.

  5. 5

    Top Position

    Continue curling until your forearms are roughly parallel to the floor or slightly higher, with your wrists in a neutral position (not flexed). Your palms should remain facing downward throughout the exercise. Squeeze your forearm muscles at the top of the movement.

  6. 6

    Lowering Phase

    Inhale as you slowly lower the weight back to the starting position in a controlled manner. Focus on resisting gravity to maximize muscle activation during this eccentric phase.

  7. 7

    Full Extension

    Lower the weight until your arms are fully extended at the bottom position, allowing a slight stretch in the target muscles without losing tension completely.

  8. 8

    Repeat

    Perform the desired number of repetitions with controlled form. A typical range for reverse curls is 10-15 reps per set, using a lighter weight than you would for standard bicep curls.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Weight selection: Start with a lighter weight than you would use for standard bicep curls. The muscles targeted by reverse curls are typically weaker than the biceps.
  • Maintain grip: Keep your grip consistent throughout the exercise with palms facing down. Do not allow your wrists to rotate during the movement.
  • Elbow position: Keep your elbows close to your torso throughout the movement, using them as fixed pivot points.
  • Control the movement: Perform the exercise slowly and with control, especially during the lowering phase, to maximize muscle engagement.
  • Wrist position: Maintain neutral wrists (not excessively flexed or extended) to prevent strain and focus tension on the target muscles.
  • Range of motion: Use a full range of motion from complete arm extension to forearms parallel with the floor.
  • Breathing pattern: Exhale during the lifting phase and inhale during the lowering phase.
  • Mind-muscle connection: Focus on feeling the contraction in your forearms and brachioradialis rather than just moving the weight.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using momentum: Swinging the torso or using shoulder movement to lift the weight reduces muscle engagement and increases injury risk.
  • Excessive weight: Trying to lift too much weight compromises form and shifts effort away from the target muscles.
  • Rotating wrists: Allowing the wrists to rotate during the movement changes the exercise from a reverse curl to a standard curl.
  • Moving elbows: Letting the elbows drift forward or away from the sides of your body reduces exercise effectiveness.
  • Incomplete range of motion: Not fully extending the arms at the bottom or not curling high enough at the top limits muscle development.
  • Excessive wrist flexion: Bending the wrists too much during the movement can lead to strain and reduces target muscle activation.
  • Quick repetitions: Performing reps too quickly reduces time under tension and diminishes muscle development.

Reverse Curl Variations

Dumbbell Reverse Curl

Using dumbbells instead of a barbell allows for a more natural range of motion and can address muscle imbalances between arms.

EZ Bar Reverse Curl

Using an EZ bar reduces wrist strain due to its angled grip positions while still effectively targeting the forearm muscles.

Cable Reverse Curl

Using a cable machine provides constant tension throughout the range of motion for increased muscle activation.

Seated Reverse Curl

Performing the exercise while seated eliminates lower body involvement and can help maintain stricter form.
