Cable Wood Chop

A dynamic rotational exercise that builds functional core strength and power

Core, Obliques, Hip Rotators
Intermediate
Cable Machine or Resistance Band

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Internal and External Obliques, Rectus Abdominis
  • Secondary Muscles: Hip Rotators, Shoulders, Lats, Glutes
  • Equipment: Cable Machine or Resistance Band with Anchor
  • Type: Dynamic Rotational
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Force Type: Rotational

Video Demonstration

How to Perform the Cable Wood Chop

  1. 1

    Setup (High to Low Wood Chop)

    Position a cable machine with the pulley set to a high position (above shoulder height). Attach a standard handle or rope attachment. Stand with your side to the cable machine, feet slightly wider than shoulder-width apart for a stable base.

  2. 2

    Starting Position

    Grasp the handle with both hands and step away from the machine until there's tension in the cable. Position your feet in a slight staggered stance for stability. Extend your arms diagonally up toward the cable, with a slight bend in the elbows.

  3. 3

    Core Engagement

    Engage your core by drawing your navel toward your spine. Maintain this bracing throughout the entire movement. Keep your chest up and shoulders back, creating a strong, athletic posture.

  4. 4

    Initiating the Movement

    Begin the movement by rotating your torso, not by pulling with your arms. Your arms should remain at approximately the same length throughout the movement, acting as a connection between your body and the cable.

  5. 5

    The Chopping Motion

    Pull the cable diagonally across your body in a controlled chopping motion from high to low, as if chopping wood. Rotate your hips and shoulders as you pull, pivoting on the balls of your feet if needed. The movement should end with your hands near your opposite hip.

  6. 6

    Full Range of Motion

    Complete the movement by fully rotating your torso and transferring your weight to your lead leg (the leg opposite to the cable machine). Your torso should face away from the cable machine at the end of the movement.

  7. 7

    Return Phase

    Control the return movement by slowly reversing the motion, allowing your torso to rotate back to the starting position. Resist the pull of the cable to maintain tension throughout the movement.

  8. 8

    Repeat & Switch Sides

    Complete the desired number of repetitions on one side (typically 8-12), then turn around to perform the same number on the opposite side. This ensures balanced development of your rotational musculature.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Initiate with core rotation: The power should come from your core rotation, not from pulling with your arms or shoulders.
  • Maintain arm position: Your arms should stay at a relatively consistent distance from your body throughout the movement, with just a slight bend in the elbows.
  • Control the movement: Avoid using momentum or jerky movements. Control the cable in both directions for maximum benefit.
  • Pivot your feet: Allow your back heel to rise and pivot slightly as you rotate for a natural, fluid motion.
  • Breathe properly: Exhale as you pull/chop downward and inhale as you return to the starting position.
  • Keep your spine neutral: Avoid excessive rounding or arching of your back throughout the movement.
  • Start with lighter weight: Begin with a lighter resistance to master the movement pattern before progressing to heavier loads.
  • Vary the height: Experiment with different pulley heights (high, middle, low) to target different angles of rotation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using only arms: Relying on arm strength rather than core rotation reduces the effectiveness and can strain the shoulders.
  • Excessive weight: Using too much resistance leads to compensatory movements and poor form. Focus on quality of movement first.
  • Restricted hip movement: Keeping the hips stationary during the exercise limits the full rotational benefit and places excess strain on the lower back.
  • Moving too quickly: Rushing through the movement with momentum rather than controlled strength diminishes the benefits and increases injury risk.
  • Rounded shoulders: Allowing your shoulders to round forward during the exercise can lead to poor posture and potential shoulder issues.
  • Locking knees: Keeping knees locked creates an unstable base and reduces the power transfer through your kinetic chain.
  • Uneven training: Neglecting to train both sides equally can create muscular imbalances. Always complete the same volume on both sides.

Cable Wood Chop Variations

Low to High Wood Chop

Performed with the cable set to a low position, pulling upward diagonally across the body. Targets the obliques with an upward rotational pattern.

Horizontal Wood Chop

Executed with the cable at mid-torso height, focusing on pure rotational strength through a horizontal plane of motion.

Kneeling Cable Wood Chop

Performed in a half-kneeling position to increase core activation by eliminating lower body contribution and creating an unstable base.

Split Stance Wood Chop

Using a split stance (one foot forward, one back) to emphasize weight transfer and rotational power development through the hips.
