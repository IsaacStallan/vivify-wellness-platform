Face Pull

Target your rear deltoids, mid-back muscles, and rotator cuff to improve posture and shoulder health

Rear Deltoids, Rotator Cuff, Traps
Beginner-Intermediate
Cable Machine, Rope Attachment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Posterior Deltoids (Rear Shoulders)
  • Secondary Muscles: Rhomboids, Middle Trapezius, Rotator Cuff, Biceps
  • Equipment: Cable Machine with Rope Attachment
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Beginner-Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Pull
  • Force: Pull (Horizontal)

Video Demonstration

How to Perform the Face Pull

  1. 1

    Machine Setup

    Set a cable pulley to slightly above eye level. Attach a rope handle to the cable. Select an appropriate weight that allows you to maintain proper form throughout the movement—typically lighter than you would use for rowing exercises.

  2. 2

    Starting Position

    Stand facing the cable machine and grasp the rope with both hands, palms facing each other. Step back until your arms are fully extended and you feel tension in the cable. Your feet should be shoulder-width apart, with a slight bend in your knees for stability.

  3. 3

    Proper Alignment

    Stand tall with a slight lean back from the hips if necessary to maintain balance. Engage your core and pull your shoulders down away from your ears. Your starting posture is critical for maximizing the benefits of this exercise.

  4. 4

    Pulling Phase

    Initiate the movement by retracting your shoulder blades (pull them together). Then, pull the rope toward your face, aiming for the rope ends to reach alongside your ears. Keep your upper arms parallel to the floor, with elbows high and wide.

  5. 5

    External Rotation

    As you pull, rotate your hands outward (external rotation) so that at the end of the movement, your knuckles are facing the ceiling and your thumbs are pointing behind you. This critical component targets the rotator cuff muscles.

  6. 6

    Contracted Position

    At the peak of the movement, your elbows should be high and wide, with the rope ends beside your ears. Hold this position for a moment, focusing on squeezing your rear deltoids and mid-back muscles.

  7. 7

    Return Phase

    Slowly return to the starting position by allowing your arms to extend forward in a controlled manner. Maintain tension throughout this phase—don't let the weight stack pull you forward.

  8. 8

    Repeat

    Complete the desired number of repetitions while maintaining proper form throughout the set. Focus on control rather than speed or weight.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Focus on external rotation: The rotational component is what separates face pulls from standard rows. Ensure you rotate your arms outward as you pull to fully engage the rotator cuff muscles.
  • Keep elbows high: Maintain your elbows at or slightly above shoulder height throughout the movement to maximize rear deltoid engagement.
  • Use lighter weights: This is a corrective/postural exercise—prioritize perfect form with lighter loads over heavier weights with compromised technique.
  • Control the eccentric: Don't let the weight stack control you on the return. Slowly extend your arms while maintaining tension throughout the movement.
  • Maintain tall posture: Avoid excessive leaning forward or backward. A slight lean back is acceptable for balance, but your spine should remain neutral.
  • Vary your elbow height: For variety, you can occasionally perform face pulls with your elbows slightly lower to target different portions of the rear delts and upper back.
  • Add to your warm-up: Consider including light face pulls in your warm-up routine, especially on upper body days, to activate your rotator cuff and prepare the shoulder complex.
  • Focus on high reps: The muscles targeted by face pulls, especially the rotator cuff, respond well to higher repetition ranges (12-15 or even 15-20).

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Neglecting the external rotation: Simply pulling the rope straight back without the rotational component significantly reduces the benefits to the rotator cuff and overall shoulder health.
  • Using momentum: Jerking or rocking your body to move the weight reduces muscle engagement and prevents the targeted muscles from receiving the intended stimulus.
  • Too much weight: Using excessive weight forces you to compromise form and reduces the focus on the smaller, stabilizing muscles that this exercise is designed to target.
  • Shrugging the shoulders: Allowing your shoulders to rise toward your ears during the movement shifts focus away from the rear deltoids and can increase upper trapezius dominance, which many people already struggle with.
  • Pulling to the chin: Aiming the rope toward your chin rather than your face/ears changes the angle of pull and reduces rear deltoid engagement.
  • Rounded posture: Hunching forward or allowing your shoulders to roll forward at the start of each repetition negates the postural benefits of the exercise.
  • Incomplete range of motion: Not fully retracting the shoulder blades or stopping the pull short of optimal contraction reduces the effectiveness of the exercise.

Face Pull Variations

Resistance Band Face Pull

Performing the movement with a resistance band anchored at head height, ideal for home workouts or travel.

TRX/Suspension Face Pull

Using suspension straps for a more challenging variation that increases core engagement and stability demands.

High-to-Low Face Pull

Performing the exercise with the cable set higher than head height, pulling downward for a different angle of resistance.

Single-Arm Face Pull

Working one arm at a time to address muscle imbalances and increase rotational demands.
