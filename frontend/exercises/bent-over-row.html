Bent Over Row

A fundamental compound exercise for building a strong back, improving posture, and increasing overall upper body strength

Back, Lats, Rhomboids, Traps
Intermediate
Barbell, Dumbbells, T-Bar

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Latissimus Dorsi, Rhomboids, Trapezius
  • Secondary Muscles: Biceps, Rear Deltoids, Erector Spinae
  • Equipment: Barbell, Dumbbells, T-Bar, or Cable Machine
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Compound
  • Force: Pull (Horizontal)

How to Perform the Barbell Bent Over Row

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand with feet shoulder-width apart with a barbell in front of you. Hinge at the hips to bend forward, keeping your back straight and knees slightly bent. Grip the barbell with hands slightly wider than shoulder-width, using an overhand grip (palms facing down).

  2. 2

    Proper Body Position

    Position your torso at approximately a 45-degree angle to the floor. Your back should be flat (maintaining its natural curve), with shoulders pulled back and down, and core engaged. The barbell should be hanging directly beneath your shoulders with arms fully extended.

  3. 3

    The Pull

    Keeping your torso angle fixed and core tight, pull the barbell toward your lower ribcage/upper abdomen by driving your elbows back and up. Focus on squeezing your shoulder blades together as you pull. Maintain your body position throughout the movement—don't use your lower back to help lift the weight.

  4. 4

    Top Position

    At the top of the movement, the barbell should touch or nearly touch your lower ribs/upper abdomen. Your elbows should be pointing behind you, and your shoulder blades should be squeezed together. Pause briefly in this contracted position to maximize back muscle engagement.

  5. 5

    Lowering Phase

    Lower the barbell with control back to the starting position, allowing your arms to fully extend and your shoulder blades to protract naturally. Maintain your bent-over position throughout—don't let the torso angle change.

  6. 6

    Repeat

    Repeat for the desired number of repetitions, maintaining proper form and torso position throughout the set. When finished, return to a standing position by driving through your hips, keeping your back straight.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Maintain a neutral spine: Keep your back flat (with its natural curve) throughout the movement—don't round or excessively arch your lower back.
  • Drive with your elbows: Think about pulling your elbows toward the ceiling rather than pulling with your hands to maximize back muscle engagement.
  • Keep a stable torso: Your torso angle should remain fixed throughout the movement—avoid the tendency to raise your chest as you pull.
  • Engage your core: Brace your abdominal muscles to stabilize your spine and protect your lower back.
  • Control the weight: Avoid using momentum to swing the weight up. The movement should be deliberate and controlled.
  • Choose the right grip width: A wider grip emphasizes the upper back (rhomboids, traps), while a narrower grip places more emphasis on the lats.
  • Position the bar correctly: In the starting position, the barbell should hang directly below your shoulders, not in front of or behind them.
  • Mind-muscle connection: Focus on feeling your back muscles working throughout the movement, particularly during the squeeze at the top.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Rounding the back: This puts excessive stress on the spine and can lead to injury. Maintain a neutral spine position throughout.
  • Using too much weight: Lifting weight that's too heavy often leads to compromised form and reduced back engagement. Start lighter and focus on form.
  • Insufficient hip hinge: Not bending far enough at the hips reduces the effectiveness for the back muscles. Aim for approximately a 45-degree torso angle.
  • Pulling too high: Pulling the barbell too high (toward the chest instead of the lower ribs/upper abdomen) shifts emphasis away from the back to the rear deltoids.
  • Using momentum: Jerking or using body English to swing the weight up reduces muscle activation and increases injury risk.
  • Rising during the pull: Raising your torso as you pull the weight takes tension off the back muscles and can strain the lower back.
  • Insufficient range of motion: Not allowing your arms to fully extend at the bottom of the movement or not pulling the bar close enough to your body at the top reduces effectiveness.

Bent Over Row Variations

Dumbbell Bent Over Row

Using dumbbells allows for a more natural range of motion and can help address muscle imbalances between sides.

Single-Arm Dumbbell Row

Performed with one hand supporting your body on a bench, allowing for greater focus on each side individually.

Pendlay Row

A variation where the torso is nearly parallel to the ground and the bar starts on the floor between each rep, emphasizing power development.

T-Bar Row

Using a T-bar apparatus or a barbell secured in a corner, providing a fixed path of motion that can help with stability.
