Bodyweight Squats

A fundamental lower body exercise that builds strength, mobility, and functional fitness using just your bodyweight

Quads, Glutes, Hamstrings
Beginner
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Quadriceps, Glutes
  • Secondary Muscles: Hamstrings, Calves, Core, Lower Back
  • Equipment: None Required
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Beginner
  • Mechanics: Lower Body Push
  • Force: Push (Vertical)

How to Perform Bodyweight Squats

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart or slightly wider. Your toes should be pointing slightly outward (about 5-20 degrees). Keep your chest up, shoulders back, and core engaged. Your arms can be extended in front of you, crossed over your chest, or placed behind your head.

  2. 2

    Initiate the Movement

    Begin the squat by bending at your hips and knees simultaneously, as if you're about to sit in a chair. Keep your weight on your heels and mid-foot, not on your toes. Maintain a neutral spine position throughout the movement.

  3. 3

    Descent Phase

    Lower your body until your thighs are at least parallel to the floor, or as low as your mobility allows while maintaining proper form. Keep your knees tracking in line with your toes (not caving inward). Your chest should remain upright, and your lower back should maintain its natural arch.

  4. 4

    Bottom Position

    At the bottom of the squat, your hips should be below your knees (if mobility allows). Keep your heels firmly on the ground and maintain tension in your core and lower body muscles. Pause briefly in this position.

  5. 5

    Ascent Phase

    Push through your heels and mid-foot to stand back up, extending your hips and knees simultaneously. Drive your hips forward at the top of the movement to fully engage your glutes. Keep your core tight throughout the ascending motion.

  6. 6

    Return to Starting Position

    Return to the upright position, fully extending your hips and knees without locking them. Maintain proper posture with your chest up and shoulders back, ready for the next repetition.

  7. 7

    Breathing Pattern

    Inhale as you descend into the squat, and exhale forcefully as you rise back to the standing position. Proper breathing helps maintain core tension and provides stability throughout the movement.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Foot position: Experiment with stance width to find what's most comfortable for your individual anatomy. Some people perform better with a slightly wider stance.
  • Knee tracking: Keep your knees aligned with your toes throughout the movement. Don't let them collapse inward, which can place stress on the knee joint.
  • Hip hinge: Initiate the movement by hinging at the hips first before bending the knees. This engages your posterior chain more effectively.
  • Chest position: Keep your chest up and proud throughout the movement. Think about pulling your shoulders back and down.
  • Neutral spine: Maintain the natural curves of your spine. Avoid rounding your lower back at the bottom of the squat.
  • Head position: Look slightly forward and down, keeping your neck in line with your spine. Avoid looking up or down excessively.
  • Weight distribution: Keep your weight centered over the middle of your foot or slightly toward your heel, never shifting forward onto your toes.
  • Tempo control: Lower yourself under control (about 2 seconds down) and rise with purpose (about 1 second up) for optimal muscle development.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Knees caving inward: This places stress on the knee joint and indicates weak hip abductors. Focus on pushing your knees outward in line with your toes.
  • Rising onto toes: Weight shifting forward onto your toes can cause knee stress and reduces posterior chain engagement. Keep your heels firmly planted.
  • Rounding the lower back: A rounded lower back under load can lead to disc injuries. Maintain a neutral spine position throughout the movement.
  • Inadequate depth: Not squatting to at least parallel limits range of motion and muscle development. Work on mobility if depth is an issue.
  • Excessive forward lean: While some forward lean is normal, excessive leaning shifts emphasis away from the quads and can stress the lower back.
  • Looking up too much: Hyperextending your neck by looking up at the ceiling can strain neck muscles and throw off your balance.
  • Rushing the movement: Performing squats too quickly reduces muscle tension and increases injury risk. Control the descent phase especially.

Bodyweight Squat Variations

Sumo Squat

A wide-stance variation that places greater emphasis on the inner thighs and glutes while reducing emphasis on the quads.

Prisoner Squat

Performed with hands behind the head, challenging balance and core stability while maintaining squat mechanics.

Jump Squat

Adds an explosive jump at the top of the movement, developing power, athleticism, and cardiovascular conditioning.

Pistol Squat

A single-leg squat variation that significantly increases the strength and balance demands on the working leg.
