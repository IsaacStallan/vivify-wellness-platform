Pull-Up

A fundamental upper body pulling exercise that builds exceptional back, bicep, and grip strength

Back, Biceps, Forearms, Core
Intermediate-Advanced
Pull-up Bar

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Latissimus Dorsi (Back)
  • Secondary Muscles: Biceps, Rhomboids, Rear Deltoids, Trapezius, Forearms
  • Equipment: Pull-up Bar
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate-Advanced
  • Mechanics: Pull
  • Force: Pull (Vertical)

How to Perform the Pull-Up

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Grip a pull-up bar with your hands slightly wider than shoulder-width apart, palms facing away from you (pronated grip). Hang freely with arms fully extended, shoulders engaged (not completely relaxed), and feet off the ground. Your body should form a straight line.

  2. 2

    Initial Engagement

    Before pulling, engage your core and slightly retract your shoulder blades. This creates tension and proper positioning before the movement begins. Think about pulling your shoulders down away from your ears.

  3. 3

    Pulling Phase

    Initiate the movement by driving your elbows down toward your hips. Pull your chest up toward the bar while keeping your body rigid. Imagine squeezing a pencil between your shoulder blades as you pull. Exhale during this upward movement.

  4. 4

    Top Position

    Continue pulling until your chin clears the bar or, ideally, until the bar reaches your upper chest. At the top, your elbows should be pointing down and back. Hold this contracted position briefly, focusing on squeezing your back muscles.

  5. 5

    Lowering Phase

    Lower yourself in a controlled manner by gradually extending your arms. Resist the pull of gravity rather than simply dropping down. Inhale during this descent. Maintain core tension throughout the movement.

  6. 6

    Bottom Position

    Return to the starting position with arms fully extended but not completely relaxed at the shoulders. Maintain tension in your upper back and core. Avoid letting your shoulders shrug up toward your ears.

  7. 7

    Repetition

    Without swinging or using momentum, begin the next repetition from this controlled hanging position. Keep your movement deliberate and focused on proper muscle engagement throughout the set.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Grip strength: Focus on maintaining a solid grip throughout the movement. Consider using chalk if your hands tend to slip.
  • Body position: Keep your body relatively straight without excessive arching or swinging. A slight hollow body position (abs engaged, slight posterior pelvic tilt) can help maintain control.
  • Elbow path: Drive your elbows down and back rather than just pulling up with your arms. This ensures proper back engagement.
  • Shoulder position: Keep your shoulders down and back throughout the movement, away from your ears, to protect your shoulder joints.
  • Head position: Maintain a neutral neck position. Avoid craning your neck to get your chin over the bar—focus on chest-to-bar instead.
  • Breathing: Exhale during the pulling phase and inhale during the lowering phase. Never hold your breath through the entire movement.
  • Range of motion: Aim for full range of motion with complete extension at the bottom and your chin (or ideally chest) reaching the bar at the top.
  • Core engagement: Keep your core tight throughout the movement to prevent swinging and maintain body control.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using momentum: Swinging, kipping, or using jerky movements to complete repetitions. This reduces muscle engagement and development. Focus on controlled, deliberate movement.
  • Incomplete range of motion: Not fully extending at the bottom or not pulling high enough at the top. This limits the benefits of the exercise and can lead to imbalanced development.
  • Excessive body movement: Allowing your legs to swing or your body to twist during the movement. This takes tension off the target muscles and can strain your back.
  • Shrugging the shoulders: Letting your shoulders raise toward your ears, especially at the bottom of the movement. This can lead to improper muscle activation and potential shoulder issues.
  • Overusing biceps: Relying too heavily on arm strength rather than engaging the larger back muscles. Focus on initiating the pull by driving your elbows down.
  • Holding breath: Forgetting to breathe properly throughout the movement. Establish a consistent breathing pattern for optimal performance and safety.
  • Poor grip placement: Hands too narrow or too wide for your body proportions. Experiment to find the optimal width that allows proper back engagement for your frame.

Pull-Up Variations

Chin-Up

Performed with palms facing toward you (supinated grip), placing greater emphasis on the biceps while still engaging the back muscles.

Wide-Grip Pull-Up

Using a wider hand placement to increase emphasis on the outer lats and create greater width in the back.

Commando Pull-Up

Gripping the bar with hands in a parallel position and pulling up with the bar at the side of the head, creating unilateral engagement.

Assisted Pull-Up

Using a resistance band, machine, or partner assistance to reduce the effective body weight, making the exercise more accessible for beginners.
