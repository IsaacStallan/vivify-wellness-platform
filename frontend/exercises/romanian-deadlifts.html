Romanian Deadlift

A powerful hip-hinge movement that targets your posterior chain for improved strength, posture, and athletic performance

Hamstrings, Glutes, Lower Back
Intermediate
Barbell, Dumbbells, or Kettlebell

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Hamstrings, Gluteus Maximus (Glutes)
  • Secondary Muscles: Erector Spinae (Lower Back), Adductors, Core
  • Equipment: Barbell, Dumbbells, or Kettlebell
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Hip Hinge
  • Force: Pull (Posterior Chain)

How to Perform the Romanian Deadlift

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand with your feet hip-width apart, holding a barbell (or dumbbells/kettlebell) in front of your thighs with an overhand grip. Keep your shoulders back and down, chest up, and core engaged. Your knees should be slightly bent (not locked).

  2. 2

    Bracing

    Take a deep breath and brace your core. Maintain a neutral spine position throughout the movement. Think about creating tension throughout your body, especially in your lats, by pulling your shoulders back and down.

  3. 3

    Initiate the Hip Hinge

    Begin by pushing your hips backward as if you're trying to touch a wall behind you with your butt. Keep the barbell close to your body, sliding it down your thighs. This is not a squat—your hips move backward, not primarily downward.

  4. 4

    Lowering Phase

    Continue hinging at the hips while maintaining the slight bend in your knees. Lower the barbell by allowing it to slide down your thighs toward your knees. Keep your back flat (not rounded) and chest up throughout.

  5. 5

    Bottom Position

    Lower the weight until you feel a stretch in your hamstrings, typically when the barbell is just below knee level. For most people, this is the point where you can no longer maintain a flat back. Your torso should be roughly parallel to the floor or slightly above.

  6. 6

    Returning to Starting Position

    Drive your hips forward by contracting your glutes and hamstrings, pushing through your heels. Keep the barbell close to your body as you return to the upright starting position. Think of this as a "hip thrust" motion rather than lifting with your back.

  7. 7

    Top Position

    At the top, stand tall with your shoulders back, chest up, and core engaged. Fully lock out your hips by squeezing your glutes, but maintain the slight bend in your knees.

  8. 8

    Repeat

    Perform the desired number of repetitions, maintaining proper form throughout. For beginners, start with 3 sets of 8-10 reps using lighter weights to master the hip hinge pattern before progressing to heavier loads.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Hip hinge focus: The RDL is primarily a hip hinge pattern, not a squat. Keep the movement focused on pushing your hips back and forward rather than bending and straightening your knees excessively.
  • Back position: Maintain a neutral spine throughout the entire movement. Your back should remain flat, neither rounded nor hyperextended.
  • Bar path: Keep the bar close to your body throughout the movement, essentially dragging it along your legs during both the descent and ascent.
  • Knee position: Maintain a slight, consistent bend in your knees throughout the exercise. Don't bend them more during the descent or straighten them completely at the top.
  • Range of motion: Lower the weight until you feel a significant stretch in your hamstrings, but stop before your back begins to round. For most people, this is when the bar reaches just below the knees.
  • Foot pressure: Keep your weight balanced across the middle of your foot, with a slight emphasis on your heels. Push through the whole foot when standing back up.
  • Neck alignment: Keep your neck in a neutral position aligned with your spine. Avoid looking up or excessively tucking your chin.
  • Breathing: Inhale at the top, brace your core, and maintain that brace as you lower the weight. Exhale during the latter portion of the ascent or once you've returned to the starting position.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Rounding the lower back: This places excessive stress on the spine and can lead to injury. Focus on maintaining a neutral spine position throughout the movement.
  • Turning it into a squat: Bending the knees too much turns the exercise into a squat rather than a hip hinge. Keep the knee angle relatively constant throughout the movement.
  • Not hinging at the hips: The primary movement should come from pushing the hips back and forward, not from bending the knees or back.
  • Allowing the bar to drift away from the body: This increases the lever arm and places unnecessary stress on the back. Keep the bar close to your legs throughout the movement.
  • Looking up/hyperextending the neck: This disrupts proper spinal alignment. Keep your gaze neutral, looking at a spot on the floor several feet in front of you.
  • Using too much weight: This often leads to compromised form and increases injury risk. Master the movement pattern with lighter weights before progressing to heavier loads.
  • Locking the knees: Fully straightening your knees puts them in a vulnerable position and reduces hamstring engagement. Maintain a slight bend in your knees throughout.

Romanian Deadlift Variations

Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift

Performed on one leg to address muscle imbalances and improve stability while requiring less external load for a training effect.

Dumbbell Romanian Deadlift

Using dumbbells instead of a barbell allows for a more natural arm position and can help address left-right imbalances.

Kettlebell Romanian Deadlift

Using kettlebells can provide a different grip stimulus and is often more accessible for beginners or in limited equipment settings.

Banded Romanian Deadlift

Adding resistance bands increases tension at the top of the movement, emphasizing glute activation and power production.
