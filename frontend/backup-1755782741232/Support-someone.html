Supporting a Friend with Focus & Resilience Challenges

Learn how to be a supportive friend to someone struggling with Focus & Resilience. Your support can make a significant difference in their recovery and wellbeing.

Learn How to Help

Being There for Your Friends

When a friend is struggling with Focus & Resilience challenges, you might feel helpless or worried about saying the wrong thing. The good news is that your support as a friend can make a huge difference. Understanding how to recognise when someone is struggling, how to talk to them, and how to help while also looking after yourself is important for being the best friend you can be.

Signs Your Friend Might Be Struggling

Focus & Resilience challenges can show up in different ways. Spotting these signs early can help you offer support when your friend needs it most.

Emotional Changes

  • Seeming sad, hopeless, or down most of the time
  • Getting angry or irritated more easily
  • Having extreme mood swings
  • Not enjoying things they used to love
  • Talking negatively about themselves
  • Seeming more anxious or worried than usual

Social Changes

  • Withdrawing from friends and avoiding hangouts
  • Not participating in group chats or activities
  • Seeming distant even when they're around
  • Having more arguments with friends or family
  • Spending lots of time alone in their room
  • Making excuses to avoid social events

Physical & Behavioural Changes

  • Changes in sleep (sleeping too much or too little)
  • Eating much more or much less than usual
  • Seeming tired all the time
  • Not taking care of personal hygiene
  • Complaining about headaches, stomach aches, or feeling sick
  • Using alcohol, drugs, or vaping more than usual

School Changes

  • Grades dropping or struggling with schoolwork
  • Skipping classes or arriving late frequently
  • Having trouble concentrating or focusing
  • Not completing assignments or forgetting things
  • Losing motivation or not caring about school
  • Talking about dropping out or giving up

How to Help: Do's and Don'ts

DO

  • Listen without judging - Let them talk and share their feelings without trying to fix everything
  • Ask directly - "Are you having thoughts of suicide?" or "How can I support you?"
  • Take them seriously - Believe what they're telling you about how they feel
  • Offer specific help - "Want me to walk you to the counsellor?" instead of "Let me know if you need anything"
  • Encourage them to get help - Suggest talking to a trusted adult, counsellor, or their parents
  • Be patient - Getting better takes time and has good days and bad days
  • Keep checking in - Continue to reach out even if they don't respond straight away
  • Learn about Focus & Resilience - Understand what they're going through
  • Look after yourself too - Make sure you're okay while helping others

DON'T

  • Give simple advice - Avoid saying "just be positive" or "get over it"
  • Make it about you - Don't say "I know exactly how you feel" or share your own problems
  • Promise to keep dangerous secrets - If they're in danger, you need to tell a trusted adult
  • Force them to talk - Respect their space while letting them know you care
  • Cover for harmful behaviour - Don't make excuses for them or enable bad choices
  • Take it personally - Their mood or behaviour isn't your fault
  • Give up on them - Keep showing you care even if they push you away
  • Try to diagnose them - Leave that to Focus & Resilience professionals
  • Ignore serious warning signs - Always take threats of self-harm seriously

How to Have Supportive Conversations

What you say and how you say it can make a big difference in how supported your friend feels.

Active Listening

Put your phone away, make eye contact, and really focus on what they're saying. Use phrases like "That sounds really hard" or "Tell me more about that."

Validation

Let them know their feelings are valid. Say things like "That sounds really tough" or "It makes sense that you'd feel that way" rather than "It could be worse."

Good Questions to Ask

Try: "How are you really going?" "What's been the hardest part?" "What would help you feel better?" "Do you want to talk or just hang out?"

Choose the Right Time

Pick a time when you won't be interrupted and they're not stressed about other things. Don't bring up serious topics in front of other people.

Respect Their Boundaries

If they don't want to talk right now, that's okay. Let them know you're there when they're ready and check in with them later.

Give Them Hope

Remind them that things can get better with help and time. Tell them about their strengths and why you value them as a friend.

What to Do in a Crisis

If your friend is in immediate danger or talking about suicide or self-harm, you need to act fast:

1. Stay Calm

Take a deep breath and stay with them. Your calm presence can help them feel safer and more stable.

2. Don't Leave Them Alone

Stay with them or make sure another trusted person can be with them. If possible, remove any means of self-harm.

3. Get Adult Help Immediately

Tell a trusted adult like a teacher, school counsellor, their parents, or call 000. You can also call Kids Helpline together (1800 55 1800).

4. Follow Up Later

After the immediate crisis, continue to check in and support them as they get professional help.

Call Kids Helpline 1800 55 1800 Call 000 Emergency

Practical Ways to Support Your Friend

Small, consistent actions often mean more than big gestures. Here are concrete ways to help:

Daily Support

  • Include them in group chats and activities
  • Sit with them at lunch or walk with them between classes
  • Share your notes if they've missed school
  • Offer to study together or work on assignments
  • Help them organise their school stuff if they're struggling
  • Remind them about important deadlines or events

Social Connection

  • Include them in low-key hangouts (not big parties)
  • Watch movies or play games together
  • Go for walks or do light activities together
  • Text them regularly to check in
  • Send funny memes or supportive messages
  • Respect when they need alone time

Getting Help

  • Help them find information about school counselling
  • Offer to walk with them to the counsellor's office
  • Help them practice what to say to their parents
  • Research Focus & Resilience resources together
  • Support them in talking to trusted adults
  • Celebrate small steps they take towards getting help

Emotional Support

  • Listen without trying to fix everything
  • Tell them their feelings are valid and understandable
  • Remind them of their good qualities and strengths
  • Be consistent and reliable - do what you say you'll do
  • Share positive memories you have together
  • Let them know you care about them clearly

Looking After Yourself Too

Supporting a friend with Focus & Resilience challenges can be emotionally draining. Your wellbeing matters too.

Set Boundaries

It's okay to have limits on your time and energy. You can't be available 24/7, and that's normal and healthy.

Get Your Own Support

Talk to other friends, family, or a school counsellor about how you're feeling. Don't carry this alone.

Learn About Focus & Resilience

Understanding Focus & Resilience can help, but remember you're their friend, not their therapist. Don't try to be their counsellor.

Keep Up Your Own Activities

Continue doing things you enjoy, hanging out with other friends, and maintaining your own Focus & Resilience routine.

Know Your Limits

You can't cure their Focus & Resilience condition or be responsible for making them better. Your job is to support, not to fix.

Get Help When You Need It

If supporting your friend is making you feel overwhelmed, anxious, or depressed, reach out for help for yourself too.

Getting Help at School

Your school has people whose job it is to help students with Focus & Resilience challenges. Here's who can help:

School Counsellor

Trained to help students with Focus & Resilience, emotional problems, and crisis situations. Usually free and confidential.

Trusted Teacher

A teacher your friend feels comfortable with can provide support and help connect them with other resources at school.

Year Coordinator

Responsible for student wellbeing in your year level and can coordinate support between teachers and counselling services.

Wellbeing Staff

Many schools have dedicated student wellbeing coordinators or youth workers who specialise in Focus & Resilience support.

School Nurse

Can provide immediate support during a crisis and help connect students with medical or Focus & Resilience services.

Deputy Principal

Can arrange accommodations, support plans, and coordinate between school and family when students need extra help.

When You MUST Tell an Adult

Sometimes you need to break confidentiality to keep your friend safe. Tell a trusted adult immediately if your friend:

Talks About Suicide

Any mention of wanting to die, kill themselves, or "not be here anymore" needs immediate adult intervention.

Is Self-Harming

Cutting, burning, hitting themselves, or other forms of self-injury require professional help immediately.

Is Using Drugs/Alcohol Heavily

Dangerous substance use or addiction needs adult intervention and professional support.

Is Being Hurt by Someone

If they're being abused, bullied severely, or hurt by someone else, this needs to be reported to keep them safe.

Remember: Telling an adult might save your friend's life. A real friend gets help when someone is in danger, even if it feels like breaking their trust.

Getting Focus & Resilience Help in Australia

Understanding how Focus & Resilience support works in Australia can help you better support your friend.

GP (Doctor)

Usually the first step. A GP can create a Focus & Resilience Care Plan that gives access to free or low-cost psychology sessions.

Psychologist

With a GP referral, young people can access up to 10 subsidised psychology sessions per year through Medicare.

School Counselling

Most Australian schools offer free counselling services. No referral needed - just ask at the office or speak to a teacher.

Community Health

Community health centres often have youth-specific Focus & Resilience services that are free or low-cost.

Emergency Help

Emergency departments, crisis Focus & Resilience teams, and CAT (Crisis Assessment and Treatment) teams for urgent situations.

Crisis Lines

24/7 support through Kids Helpline, Lifeline, Beyond Blue, and other free crisis support services.

Supporting Different Friends

Different people may need different types of support based on their background and experiences.

LGBTIQA+ Friends

May face additional challenges like family rejection or bullying. Help connect them with LGBTIQA+ affirming services like QLife.

Aboriginal & Torres Strait Islander Friends

May prefer culturally appropriate services. Help them connect with local Aboriginal Community Controlled Health Services.

Friends from Different Cultural Backgrounds

Be sensitive to cultural beliefs about Focus & Resilience. Help find services with interpreters or culturally appropriate support.

Friends with Disabilities

May need accessible services or have different communication needs. Help them find appropriate and accessible support.

Friends in Foster Care or Difficult Home Situations

May not have family support. Help connect them with youth services and ensure they have trusted adults in their lives.

Friends from Low-Income Families

Help them find free services and support. Many Focus & Resilience services for young people are free or very low cost.

Things NOT to Say

Well-meaning comments can sometimes make things worse. Avoid these common phrases:

"Just Be Positive"

Focus & Resilience conditions can't be cured by positive thinking. This makes people feel like their struggles aren't real or valid.

"Others Have It Worse"

This minimises their pain and makes them feel guilty for struggling. Everyone's pain is valid regardless of others' situations.

"You Don't Look Depressed"

Mental illness is invisible. Many people become very good at hiding their struggles and putting on a "normal" face.

"Have You Tried Exercise?"

While exercise can help, Focus & Resilience conditions require proper treatment. Don't suggest simple solutions to complex problems.

"You're Just Attention Seeking"

Even if someone is seeking attention, it usually means they're in genuine distress and need help, not criticism.

"Get Over It"

Focus & Resilience conditions aren't something you can just "get over." Recovery takes time, support, and often professional help.
