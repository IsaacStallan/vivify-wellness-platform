Squats

A fundamental compound exercise for building lower body strength, stability, and functional movement

Quadriceps, Glutes, Hamstrings
Beginner to Advanced
Bodyweight or Barbell

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Quadriceps, Gluteus Maximus (Glutes)
  • Secondary Muscles: Hamstrings, Adductors, Core, Lower Back
  • Equipment: None (Bodyweight) or Barbell
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Beginner to Advanced
  • Mechanics: Hip and Knee Extension
  • Force: Push (Vertical)

How to Perform the Squat

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart or slightly wider. Your toes should be pointing slightly outward (about 5-20 degrees). For the bodyweight version, keep your arms at your sides or in front of you. For barbell squats, position the bar across your upper back (trapezius muscles), not on your neck.

  2. 2

    Bracing

    Take a deep breath and brace your core by contracting your abdominal muscles as if preparing to take a punch. This creates intra-abdominal pressure that helps stabilize your spine throughout the movement.

  3. 3

    Initiate the Movement

    Begin the descent by hinging at your hips, pushing them back as if sitting into a chair. Keep your chest up and your back neutral (avoid excessive rounding or arching). For bodyweight squats, you can extend your arms forward for balance.

  4. 4

    Descending Phase

    Bend your knees and continue lowering yourself by allowing your hips to drop down and slightly back. Keep your weight evenly distributed between the heels and balls of your feet. Your knees should track in line with your toes (not caving inward).

  5. 5

    Bottom Position

    Lower until your thighs are at least parallel to the ground (or further if you have the mobility). At the bottom position, your knees should be at approximately 90 degrees or more, with your weight balanced and back neutral. For beginners, going parallel is a good goal.

  6. 6

    Ascending Phase

    Push through your entire foot (emphasizing the heel and mid-foot) to drive back up to the starting position. Extend your knees and hips simultaneously while maintaining tension in your core and back. Exhale as you rise.

  7. 7

    Top Position

    Return to the starting position with your hips and knees fully extended, but avoid locking out your knees. Keep your core engaged throughout the movement.

  8. 8

    Repeat

    Perform the desired number of repetitions, maintaining proper form throughout. For beginners, start with 2-3 sets of 8-12 reps, focusing on technique before adding weight or increasing volume.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Foot position: Your stance width can vary based on your anatomy and comfort. Experiment to find the stance that allows proper depth while maintaining balance.
  • Knee tracking: Your knees should track in line with your toes throughout the movement. Avoid letting them collapse inward (knee valgus).
  • Hip hinge: Initiate the movement by pushing your hips back, not by bending your knees first. This helps maintain proper mechanics and engages your posterior chain.
  • Back position: Maintain a neutral spine throughout the movement. Avoid excessive arching or rounding of your back.
  • Depth: Aim to squat until your thighs are at least parallel to the ground. Going deeper is beneficial if you have the mobility to maintain proper form.
  • Core engagement: Keep your core braced throughout the entire movement to protect your spine and transfer force effectively.
  • Gaze direction: Look slightly downward about 4-6 feet in front of you to help maintain a neutral neck position.
  • Breathing: Inhale before descending, hold your breath during the descent and initial push, then exhale during the latter portion of the ascent.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Knees caving inward: This places excessive stress on the knee joint and can lead to injury. Focus on pushing your knees outward in line with your toes.
  • Rising onto your toes: This shifts your balance forward and reduces force production. Keep your weight distributed across your entire foot with an emphasis on the heel and mid-foot.
  • Rounding your lower back: This can place excessive stress on your spine. Maintain a neutral spine position by keeping your chest up and core engaged.
  • Looking up excessively: This hyperextends your neck and can affect your overall posture. Keep your gaze neutral, looking slightly downward.
  • Shallow depth: Not reaching proper depth (at least parallel) limits muscle activation and range of motion benefits. Work on mobility if depth is an issue.
  • Heels rising off the ground: This indicates poor ankle mobility or improper weight distribution. Focus on keeping your heels planted throughout the movement.
  • Initiating with knees first: This creates a more quad-dominant movement and places more stress on the knees. Start the movement by hinging at the hips.

Squat Variations

Goblet Squat

Holding a kettlebell or dumbbell in front of your chest, which helps maintain an upright torso and is excellent for beginners learning proper squat form.

Front Squat

A barbell squat variation where the bar is positioned across the front of the shoulders, placing more emphasis on the quadriceps and core.

Bulgarian Split Squat

A unilateral (single-leg) squat variation with the rear foot elevated, excellent for addressing muscle imbalances and improving stability.

Pistol Squat (Single-Leg Squat)

An advanced bodyweight squat performed on one leg with the other leg extended forward, requiring exceptional strength, balance, and mobility.
