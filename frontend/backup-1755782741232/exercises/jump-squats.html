Jump Squats

A powerful plyometric exercise that builds lower body strength, explosive power, and cardiovascular endurance

Lower Body, Cardio
Intermediate
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Quadriceps, Glutes, Hamstrings
  • Secondary Muscles: Calves, Core, Lower Back
  • Equipment: None
  • Type: Plyometric, Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Compound
  • Force: Push (Lower Body), Explosive

How to Perform Jump Squats

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart or slightly wider. Point your toes slightly outward. Keep your chest up, shoulders back, and core engaged.

  2. 2

    Squat Down

    Begin the movement by pushing your hips back and bending your knees, lowering into a squat position. Aim to lower your body until your thighs are about parallel with the floor, or to a comfortable depth based on your mobility.

  3. 3

    Prepare for Takeoff

    At the bottom of the squat, push your weight into your heels and midfoot. Ensure your knees are tracking in line with your toes and not caving inward.

  4. 4

    Explosive Jump

    From the bottom position, explosively drive through your heels and extend your hips, knees, and ankles to propel your body upward into a jump. Simultaneously swing your arms upward to assist with momentum.

  5. 5

    Body Position in Air

    As you rise into the air, fully extend your hips and knees. Keep your core tight and maintain an upright torso. Your feet should leave the ground completely.

  6. 6

    Land Softly

    Land softly on the balls of your feet, then roll back to your heels, immediately bending your knees and hips to absorb the impact. Allow your knees to flex as you transition back into the squat position.

  7. 7

    Repeat the Movement

    Upon landing, immediately lower back into the next squat and continue the sequence for the desired number of repetitions. Focus on maintaining a rhythm while ensuring proper form throughout.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Warm up properly: Due to the high-impact nature of jump squats, ensure your muscles and joints are properly warmed up before beginning.
  • Start with proper squat form: Master the basic bodyweight squat before progressing to jump squats to ensure you have the foundational movement pattern.
  • Use your arms: Swing your arms forward and upward during the jumping phase to help generate momentum and increase jump height.
  • Land softly: Focus on landing as quietly as possible by absorbing the impact through your legs rather than letting your feet slam down.
  • Maintain alignment: Keep your knees tracking over your toes throughout the movement, especially during landing when knee collapse is common.
  • Breathe rhythmically: Exhale during the explosive jumping phase and inhale as you descend back into the squat position.
  • Control the descent: Don't rush back into the squat position after landing—control the movement to protect your knees and maximize muscle engagement.
  • Progress gradually: Increase repetitions, sets, or intensity gradually over time rather than immediately going for maximum effort.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Landing with straight legs: This creates excessive impact on your joints. Always land with soft, bent knees to absorb the shock.
  • Allowing knees to cave inward: Knees collapsing inward (valgus collapse) during landing can increase injury risk. Focus on pushing knees outward in line with your toes.
  • Leaning too far forward: Excessive forward lean places strain on the lower back. Maintain an upright torso with a neutral spine.
  • Shallow squats: Not squatting deep enough reduces the effectiveness of the exercise. Aim to reach at least parallel depth when possible.
  • Rising onto toes during squat: Lifting your heels during the squat phase shifts balance and reduces power. Keep your whole foot grounded during the squat.
  • Neglecting core engagement: A loose core during jumping and landing increases injury risk. Keep your abdominals braced throughout the movement.
  • Overtraining: Due to their high-impact nature, doing too many jump squats too frequently can lead to overuse injuries. Include adequate rest days in your training.

Jump Squat Variations

Half Jump Squats

A modified version using a shallower squat and lower jump height, ideal for beginners or those building up to the full movement.

Weighted Jump Squats

Adds resistance using dumbbells, a medicine ball, or a weighted vest to increase the intensity and strength component.

180-Degree Jump Squats

Incorporates a 180-degree rotation during the jump so you land facing the opposite direction, adding a coordination challenge.

Single-Leg Jump Squats

An advanced variation performed on one leg that significantly increases the balance challenge and unilateral strength demands.
