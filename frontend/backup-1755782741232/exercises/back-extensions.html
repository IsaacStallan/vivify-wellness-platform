Back Extensions

A targeted exercise that strengthens the lower back, hamstrings, and glutes to improve posture and reduce back pain

Lower Back, Glutes, Hamstrings
Beginner-Intermediate
No Equipment or Exercise Ball

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Erector Spinae, Glutes, Hamstrings
  • Secondary Muscles: Adductors, Core Stabilizers, Mid-back Muscles
  • Equipment: Can be done with no equipment (floor version) or with an exercise ball
  • Type: Dynamic, Isolation
  • Experience Level: Beginner-Intermediate
  • Force Type: Extension (Posterior Chain)
  • Benefits: Back Strength, Posture Improvement, Pain Prevention

How to Perform Back Extensions (Floor Version)

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Lie face down on a mat with your arms extended in front of you, palms facing down. Your legs should be straight with toes pointed or flexed. Position your forehead lightly on the mat to maintain a neutral neck position.

  2. 2

    Engage Your Core

    Before beginning the movement, gently engage your core by drawing your navel slightly toward your spine. This creates stability and protects your lower back during the exercise.

  3. 3

    Lift Your Upper Body

    Keeping your feet in contact with the floor, slowly lift your chest, shoulders, and head by engaging your lower back muscles and glutes. Your arms can remain extended in front of you (harder) or be positioned alongside your body (easier) depending on your fitness level.

  4. 4

    Control the Range

    Lift until your chest is about 6-10 inches off the ground or until you feel a comfortable contraction in your lower back muscles. Avoid excessive lifting, which can hyperextend your spine. Keep your gaze directed slightly downward to maintain proper neck alignment.

  5. 5

    Hold the Position

    Hold the lifted position for 1-2 seconds at the top of the movement, focusing on squeezing your glutes and lower back muscles to maximize muscle activation.

  6. 6

    Lower with Control

    Slowly lower your upper body back to the starting position in a controlled manner. Avoid simply dropping back to the floor, which reduces the effectiveness of the exercise.

  7. 7

    Breathing Pattern

    Exhale as you lift your upper body, and inhale as you lower back down. This breathing pattern helps maintain proper core engagement and provides a rhythm for the movement.

  8. 8

    Complete Repetitions

    Repeat the movement for the prescribed number of repetitions (typically 10-15 for beginners, building to 15-20 as strength improves). Focus on quality of movement rather than quantity.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Control the range: The height of your lift is less important than the quality of muscle contraction. A moderate lift with proper engagement is more effective than excessive extension.
  • Neck position: Keep your neck in a neutral position by looking down at the floor or slightly forward. Avoid craning your neck up, which can cause strain.
  • Gluteal emphasis: Consciously squeeze your glutes as you lift to engage more of the posterior chain and reduce excessive lower back stress.
  • Arm positioning options: For beginners, keeping arms by your sides reduces the lever arm and makes the exercise easier. As you progress, extending arms overhead increases difficulty.
  • Leg positioning: Keeping legs together works the adductors (inner thigh muscles) more, while a slightly wider stance may be more comfortable for some individuals.
  • Tempo control: Perform the movement slowly and deliberately (2 seconds up, 1-2 second hold, 2 seconds down) to maximize muscle engagement and minimize risk of injury.
  • Gradual progression: If the floor version becomes too easy, consider progressing to an exercise ball version or adding light weights held across your chest.
  • Balance with flexion: Pair back extensions with complementary core exercises like planks or hollow holds to create balanced strength around the spine.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Hyperextending the spine: Lifting too high can compress the vertebrae and lead to lower back pain. Focus on a moderate range of motion with good muscle engagement.
  • Jerking or using momentum: Quick, uncontrolled movements reduce effectiveness and increase injury risk. Keep the motion slow and deliberate.
  • Lifting the legs: Some beginners lift their legs along with their torso, turning the exercise into a rocking motion. Keep your legs in contact with the floor throughout.
  • Looking up excessively: This creates cervical spine compression. Maintain a neutral neck position by keeping your gaze directed at the floor or slightly forward.
  • Holding breath: This creates unnecessary internal pressure. Maintain a consistent breathing pattern throughout the exercise.
  • Inadequate range of motion: Barely lifting off the floor doesn't sufficiently engage the target muscles. Ensure you're lifting to a point where you feel your back muscles working.
  • No glute engagement: Relying solely on the lower back muscles can lead to strain. Consciously activate your glutes during the extension phase.

Back Extension Variations

Alternating Limb Back Extensions

Lifting opposite arm and leg (right arm with left leg, then left arm with right leg) to reduce lower back strain while still targeting the posterior chain.

Exercise Ball Back Extensions

Performing the movement with your hips supported on a stability ball, allowing for a greater range of motion and increased challenge to core stability.

Weighted Back Extensions

Adding resistance by holding a light weight plate or dumbbell across your chest, increasing the challenge for more advanced practitioners.

Isometric Back Extension

Lifting to the top position and holding for an extended time (20-60 seconds) to build endurance in the lower back and posterior chain muscles.
