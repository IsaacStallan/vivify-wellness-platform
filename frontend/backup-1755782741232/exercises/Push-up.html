Push-Up

A foundational bodyweight exercise that builds upper body strength, stability, and muscle endurance

Chest, Shoulders, Triceps, Core
Beginner-Intermediate
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Pectoralis Major (Chest)
  • Secondary Muscles: Anterior Deltoids (Front Shoulders), Triceps, Core
  • Equipment: None Required
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Beginner-Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Push
  • Force: Push (Horizontal)

How to Perform the Push-Up

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Begin in a plank position with your hands placed slightly wider than shoulder-width apart. Position your hands directly under your shoulders or slightly wider. Your fingers should be pointing forward or slightly turned out. Extend your legs behind you with your feet hip-width apart.

  2. 2

    Body Alignment

    Establish a straight line from your head to your heels by engaging your core. Your body should form a rigid plank with your neck in a neutral position (eyes looking slightly ahead of you at the floor). Squeeze your glutes and quadriceps to maintain this alignment.

  3. 3

    Lowering Phase

    Inhale as you bend your elbows to lower your chest toward the floor. Keep your elbows at approximately a 45-degree angle to your body (not flared out to the sides). Lower until your chest is about an inch from the floor or as far as your strength and mobility allow with good form.

  4. 4

    Bottom Position

    Briefly pause at the bottom position with your chest near the floor. Maintain tension in your muscles and ensure your body remains in a straight line – don't allow your hips to sag or pike upward.

  5. 5

    Pushing Phase

    Exhale as you push through your palms to straighten your arms and return to the starting position. Focus on keeping your core engaged throughout the movement. Think about pushing the floor away from you rather than pushing your body up.

  6. 6

    Top Position

    At the top of the movement, your arms should be fully extended but not locked out. Maintain the straight line from head to heels and continue bracing your core. This completes one repetition.

  7. 7

    Repetition

    Perform the desired number of repetitions while maintaining proper form throughout. If form begins to deteriorate, rest and reset rather than completing repetitions with poor technique.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Hand position: Place your hands slightly wider than shoulder-width for standard push-ups. Wider hand placement emphasizes chest, while narrower emphasizes triceps.
  • Body alignment: Maintain a straight line from head to heels throughout the entire movement. Don't let your hips sag down or pike up.
  • Elbow position: Keep your elbows at a 45-degree angle to your body rather than flaring them out to 90 degrees to reduce shoulder strain.
  • Core engagement: Keep your core muscles tight and engaged throughout the movement to maintain proper position and protect your lower back.
  • Head position: Keep your neck in a neutral position by looking slightly ahead of you on the floor, not up or down.
  • Breathing: Inhale as you lower your body toward the floor and exhale as you push back up to the starting position.
  • Tempo: Control the movement, especially on the way down. Aim for a 2-second lowering phase and a 1-second pushing phase for optimal muscle development.
  • Range of motion: Try to achieve a full range of motion by lowering your chest close to the floor while maintaining proper form.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Sagging hips: Allowing your midsection to drop toward the floor. This puts strain on your lower back and reduces the effectiveness of the exercise. Keep your core engaged throughout the movement.
  • Pike in the hips: Raising your hips too high, creating an inverted V-shape with your body. This reduces chest engagement and makes the exercise easier but less effective.
  • Flaring elbows: Letting your elbows spread too far from your body at a 90-degree angle. This increases shoulder strain and can lead to injury. Keep elbows at approximately 45 degrees from your torso.
  • Incomplete range of motion: Not lowering your body far enough or fully extending at the top. This reduces the effectiveness of the exercise and limits strength development.
  • Head/neck misalignment: Looking up or dropping your head toward the floor. Maintain a neutral neck position with eyes focused slightly ahead of you on the floor.
  • Hand placement too far forward: Placing hands too far in front of your shoulders. This shifts emphasis away from the chest and can strain the shoulders. Hands should be roughly under the shoulders or slightly wider.
  • Holding breath: Forgetting to breathe properly during the exercise. Establish a rhythm of inhaling during the lowering phase and exhaling during the pushing phase.

Push-Up Variations

Incline Push-Up

An easier variation with hands elevated on a bench, table, or wall, reducing the amount of body weight you're pushing. Perfect for beginners building strength.

Decline Push-Up

A more challenging variation with feet elevated on a bench or step, increasing the load on the upper chest and shoulders.

Diamond Push-Up

Hands positioned close together under the chest with thumbs and index fingers touching to form a diamond shape. Increases triceps activation.

Wide Push-Up

Hands placed significantly wider than shoulder-width to increase pectoral engagement while reducing triceps involvement.
