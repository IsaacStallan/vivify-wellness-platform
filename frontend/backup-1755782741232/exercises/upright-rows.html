Upright Rows

A compound movement that targets the shoulders and upper traps for improved shoulder width and upper body strength

Shoulders, Traps, Biceps
Intermediate
Barbell, Dumbbells, Cable, or Resistance Bands

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Deltoids (Shoulders), Trapezius
  • Secondary Muscles: Biceps, Rhomboids, Brachialis
  • Equipment: Barbell, Dumbbells, EZ Bar, Cable, or Resistance Bands
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Compound
  • Force: Pull (Vertical)

Safety Consideration

Upright rows can potentially cause shoulder impingement in some individuals, especially when performed with a narrow grip or when the elbows are raised too high. If you experience any shoulder discomfort during this exercise, consider modifying your grip width, limiting the range of motion, or choosing an alternative exercise.

This exercise may not be suitable for those with existing shoulder issues. Always prioritize proper form over weight, and consult with a fitness professional if you're unsure about your technique.

How to Perform Barbell Upright Rows

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand with feet shoulder-width apart, knees slightly bent, and core engaged. Hold a barbell with a pronated (overhand) grip in front of your thighs. Your hands should be slightly wider than shoulder-width apart to reduce shoulder impingement risk.

  2. 2

    Proper Posture

    Maintain a neutral spine with chest up and shoulders pulled back and down. Keep your core tight and avoid leaning backward or using momentum from your lower body.

  3. 3

    The Pull

    Keeping the barbell close to your body, lift it by raising your elbows up and to the sides. Lead with your elbows rather than your hands, focusing on using your deltoids and traps to power the movement.

  4. 4

    Top Position

    Pull the barbell up until it reaches approximately mid-chest level or just below your chin (generally until your elbows are at shoulder height). Your upper arms should be roughly parallel to the ground or slightly lower to minimize impingement risk.

  5. 5

    Lowering Phase

    Lower the weight with control back to the starting position. Take slightly longer on the lowering phase than the pulling phase to maximize muscle tension and control.

  6. 6

    Repeat

    Repeat for the desired number of repetitions, maintaining proper form throughout. Focus on quality of movement rather than quantity or weight.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Use a wider grip: A wider grip (slightly outside shoulder-width) reduces the risk of shoulder impingement compared to a narrow grip.
  • Lead with elbows: Think about pulling your elbows up and out rather than pulling with your hands to maximize deltoid engagement.
  • Maintain upright posture: Keep your torso upright throughout the movement—avoid leaning back to assist the lift.
  • Control the movement: Perform the exercise with deliberate control, especially during the lowering phase.
  • Limit the range of motion: If you experience any shoulder discomfort, try limiting the height of the pull to below chin level.
  • Consider alternating equipment: Dumbbells or a cable machine can provide more natural movement patterns than a straight barbell.
  • Start light: Begin with lighter weights to perfect your form before progressing to heavier loads.
  • Mind-muscle connection: Focus on feeling the contraction in your deltoids and traps throughout the movement.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using too narrow a grip: A very narrow grip increases the risk of shoulder impingement. Keep hands at least shoulder-width apart.
  • Pulling too high: Raising the barbell too high (above chin level) can increase shoulder impingement risk without adding significant muscle benefit.
  • Using momentum: Swinging the weight up with body movement reduces muscle engagement and increases injury risk. Lift with controlled movements.
  • Leaning back: Arching the back to help lift the weight can lead to lower back strain. Maintain an upright torso throughout.
  • Wrist position: Allowing your wrists to bend backward during the movement creates unnecessary strain. Keep wrists neutral or slightly extended.
  • Lifting too heavy: Using excessive weight compromises form and increases injury risk. Focus on proper technique with appropriate weight.
  • Shrugging only: Turning the exercise into a pure shrug by not bending the elbows enough shifts focus away from the deltoids to just the traps.

Upright Row Variations

Dumbbell Upright Rows

Using dumbbells allows for a more natural movement pattern and can help address muscle imbalances between sides.

Cable Upright Rows

Using a cable machine provides constant tension throughout the movement, potentially increasing muscle activation.

EZ Bar Upright Rows

The curved bar allows for a more neutral wrist position, which can be more comfortable for some lifters.

Smith Machine Upright Rows

Provides a fixed path of motion that can help beginners learn proper form, but limits some natural movement patterns.
