Tricep Dip

A powerful compound exercise for developing stronger, more defined triceps and upper body strength

Arms, Triceps, Chest, Shoulders
Intermediate
Parallel Bars, Bench, or Dip Station

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Triceps Brachii (all three heads)
  • Secondary Muscles: Anterior Deltoid, Pectoralis Major, Rhomboids
  • Equipment: Parallel Bars, Dip Station, or Sturdy Bench/Chair
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Multi-Joint
  • Force: Push (Vertical)

How to Perform the Tricep Dip

  1. 1

    Position Setup

    Grip the parallel bars or edges of a bench with your hands shoulder-width apart. If using parallel bars, face forward; if using a bench, face away from it. Your arms should be fully extended, supporting your entire body weight.

  2. 2

    Starting Position

    Maintain a slight forward lean for parallel bar dips (about 5-10 degrees) to better target the triceps. For bench dips, keep your torso upright. Position your shoulders directly above your hands with arms locked out.

  3. 3

    Body Positioning

    For greater triceps emphasis, keep your elbows close to your body throughout the movement. Cross your ankles and slightly bend your knees. If using a bench, extend your legs with heels on the floor or place them on another bench for increased difficulty.

  4. 4

    Lowering Phase

    Inhale as you slowly lower your body by bending your elbows. Keep your shoulders down and away from your ears. Continue descending until your elbows reach approximately a 90-degree angle, or just below parallel if flexibility allows.

  5. 5

    Bottom Position

    At the bottom of the movement, your shoulders should be below your elbows. Be careful not to go too deep, which could put excessive stress on your shoulder joints, especially if you're a beginner.

  6. 6

    Pressing Phase

    Exhale as you push through your palms to extend your elbows and return to the starting position. Focus on contracting your triceps to power the movement rather than using momentum.

  7. 7

    Top Position

    At the top, your arms should be fully extended but avoid locking out your elbows completely to maintain tension on the triceps. Keep your body stable without swinging or using momentum.

  8. 8

    Repeat

    Perform the desired number of repetitions while maintaining proper form throughout. A typical range for tricep dips is 8-12 reps per set, adjusting based on your fitness level.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Elbow position: Keep your elbows pointing backward, not flaring outward, to maximize triceps engagement and reduce shoulder strain.
  • Control the descent: Lower yourself slowly and with control to maximize muscle tension and reduce injury risk.
  • Shoulder positioning: Keep shoulders down and back, away from your ears, throughout the entire movement.
  • Wrist alignment: Maintain neutral wrists (not bent forward or backward) to prevent strain.
  • Body stability: Keep your core engaged and body movement minimal—avoid swinging to generate momentum.
  • Range of motion: Find the depth that works for your shoulder mobility, typically aiming for elbows at 90 degrees or slightly lower.
  • Breathing: Inhale during the lowering phase and exhale while pushing back up.
  • Head position: Keep your head in a neutral position, looking straight ahead, to maintain proper spinal alignment.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using momentum: Bouncing at the bottom or using a swing-like motion reduces triceps activation and increases injury risk.
  • Flaring the elbows: Allowing elbows to point outward puts excessive stress on the shoulders and reduces triceps involvement.
  • Insufficient depth: Not lowering your body far enough limits muscle development and range of motion benefits.
  • Excessive depth: Going too deep can strain shoulder joints, especially if your mobility is limited or you're a beginner.
  • Shrugging shoulders: Raising your shoulders toward your ears creates unnecessary tension and reduces effectiveness.
  • Insufficient control: Dropping quickly into the bottom position can lead to injury and reduces muscle engagement.
  • Leaning too far forward: While some forward lean is appropriate, excessive leaning shifts focus to the chest instead of the triceps.

Tricep Dip Variations

Bench Dips

An easier variation performed with hands on a bench and feet on the floor or elevated on another bench.

Weighted Dips

Adding external resistance with a dip belt, weighted vest, or dumbbell between the legs for increased difficulty.

Ring Dips

Performed on gymnastic rings, adding an element of instability that increases difficulty and muscle recruitment.

Korean Dips

An advanced variation where elbows are positioned behind the body on a straight bar, maximizing triceps involvement.
