Hip Thrusts

A powerful exercise that specifically targets the glutes with greater range of motion than standard bridges for maximum strength and development

Glutes, Hamstrings, Lower Back
Intermediate
Bench/Chair, Optional Weight

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Gluteus Maximus
  • Secondary Muscles: Hamstrings, Erector Spinae, Hip Adductors, Core
  • Equipment: Bench/Chair, Optional Barbell/Weight
  • Type: Compound, Hip Extension
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Force Type: Push (Hip Extension)
  • Benefits: Glute Strength, Hip Mobility, Athletic Performance

How to Perform Hip Thrusts

  1. 1

    Equipment Setup

    Position a bench or sturdy chair behind you. If you're using a barbell or weight, place it nearby for easy access. You may want to use a pad, folded towel, or specialized barbell pad to cushion the weight across your hips.

  2. 2

    Starting Position

    Sit on the ground with your upper back (shoulder blade area) against the edge of the bench. Your knees should be bent with feet flat on the floor about hip-width apart and positioned so that your lower leg forms a 90-degree angle with the floor at the top position.

  3. 3

    Weight Placement (Optional)

    If using weight, position the barbell or dumbbell across your hip crease (where your legs meet your torso), not on your abdomen. Secure it with your hands on either side of your hips to prevent it from rolling.

  4. 4

    Core Engagement

    Before initiating the movement, engage your core by drawing your navel toward your spine. This creates stability and protects your lower back throughout the exercise.

  5. 5

    Drive Through Your Heels

    Press firmly through your heels and squeeze your glutes to thrust your hips upward. Keep your chin tucked slightly to maintain a neutral spine position. Your weight should be distributed between your upper back (on the bench) and your feet.

  6. 6

    Top Position

    At the top of the movement, your torso and thighs should form a straight line parallel to the ground, with knees bent at 90 degrees. Your shoulders should remain on the bench with your body weight supported between your upper back and feet.

  7. 7

    Maximum Contraction

    At the peak position, focus on squeezing your glutes as hard as possible for 1-2 seconds. This conscious contraction is key for maximum glute activation and development.

  8. 8

    Controlled Descent

    Lower your hips back toward the ground in a slow, controlled manner, maintaining tension in your glutes throughout the movement. Your upper back should remain in contact with the bench throughout the exercise.

  9. 9

    Breathing Pattern

    Exhale as you thrust your hips upward, and inhale as you lower back down. This breathing pattern helps maintain proper core engagement and provides a rhythm for the movement.

  10. 10

    Complete Repetitions

    Repeat the movement for the prescribed number of repetitions (typically 10-15 for beginners, building to 15-20 as strength improves). Focus on quality contractions rather than rushing through repetitions.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Focus on glute activation: The primary purpose of this exercise is to target your glutes. Mentally focus on using them to power the movement rather than your lower back or hamstrings.
  • Proper bench height: The bench should be at a height that allows your shoulders to rest comfortably on it without straining your neck. For most people, this is around 14-16 inches high.
  • Foot placement: Position your feet so that at the top of the movement, your shins are vertical (perpendicular to the ground). This typically means placing them about shoulder-width apart and 12-16 inches from your glutes.
  • Knee alignment: Keep your knees tracking over your toes throughout the movement to protect the knee joints from unnecessary stress.
  • Avoid overextension: At the top position, your body should form a straight line from shoulders to knees. Excessive extension beyond this point can strain your lower back.
  • Padding comfort: If using a barbell, always use cushioning (like a pad or folded towel) at the hip crease to prevent bruising and discomfort.
  • Progressive overload: To continue seeing results, gradually increase either the weight used, the number of repetitions, or the time under tension as your strength improves.
  • Hip position: Think about creating a straight line from your knees to your shoulders at the top position, rather than just lifting as high as possible.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Insufficient hip extension: Not lifting the hips high enough to achieve full extension, which reduces glute engagement. Your hips should reach a position where your torso and thighs form a straight line.
  • Overarching the lower back: Allowing excessive lumbar extension at the top position rather than maintaining a neutral spine. This can lead to lower back strain and reduces glute activation.
  • Rising onto the neck: Allowing the shoulders to roll up onto the neck instead of maintaining stable shoulder blade contact with the bench. This can strain the cervical spine and reduce effectiveness.
  • Pushing through toes: Shifting weight to the front of the feet rather than driving through the heels. This reduces glute engagement and places more emphasis on the quadriceps.
  • Knees caving inward: Allowing the knees to collapse toward each other, which can stress the knee joints and decrease glute activation. Keep knees in line with the toes throughout.
  • Improper bench position: Placing the bench too high or low relative to your upper back, which changes the mechanics of the exercise and can reduce effectiveness or increase injury risk.
  • Insufficient glute squeeze: Not fully contracting the glutes at the top of the movement. This muscular contraction is what makes the exercise effective, not just the height of the lift.

Hip Thrust Variations

Single-Leg Hip Thrust

Performing the movement with one leg elevated or extended, placing all the work on the supporting leg to address strength imbalances and increase difficulty.

Banded Hip Thrust

Adding a resistance band above the knees or around the thighs to increase tension at the top of the movement and enhance gluteal activation, particularly the gluteus medius.

Elevated Hip Thrust

Placing feet on an elevated surface to increase the range of motion and challenge the glutes through a greater stretch at the bottom of the movement.

Paused Hip Thrust

Holding the top contracted position for 3-5 seconds to increase time under tension and maximize glute activation without necessarily increasing weight.
