Planks

A fundamental core stabilization exercise that strengthens multiple muscle groups while improving posture and stability

Core, Shoulders, Back
Beginner
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Transverse Abdominis, Rectus Abdominis, Obliques
  • Secondary Muscles: Shoulders, Back, Glutes, Quads
  • Equipment: None Required
  • Type: Isometric, Stabilization
  • Experience Level: Beginner
  • Mechanics: Anti-Extension
  • Force: Static Hold

How to Perform Planks

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Begin in a face-down position on the floor. Place your forearms on the ground with elbows directly beneath your shoulders, and forearms parallel to each other. Your forearms and elbows should form a 90-degree angle.

  2. 2

    Body Alignment

    Extend your legs behind you, with toes tucked under. Push your body up so that you're balancing on your forearms and toes. Your body should form a straight line from head to heels.

  3. 3

    Core Engagement

    Contract your abdominal muscles by drawing your navel toward your spine. This engagement is crucial for maintaining proper form and getting the most benefit from the exercise.

  4. 4

    Proper Head Position

    Keep your neck in a neutral position by looking at a spot on the floor about a foot in front of your hands. Avoid dropping your head or looking up, which can strain your neck.

  5. 5

    Maintain the Position

    Hold this position while breathing normally. Focus on keeping your body in a straight line, avoiding sagging hips or raising your buttocks too high. Start with a duration that challenges you but allows maintaining proper form.

  6. 6

    Breathing Technique

    Breathe deeply and regularly throughout the hold. Avoid holding your breath, which can increase blood pressure and reduce the effectiveness of the exercise.

  7. 7

    Ending the Exercise

    To finish, gently lower your knees to the floor, then sit back on your heels to release tension in your lower back. You can also stretch your back by performing a child's pose stretch.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Focus on full-body tension: Actively squeeze your quads, glutes, and core throughout the entire hold for maximum effectiveness.
  • Check alignment: Have someone check your form or use a mirror to ensure your body forms a straight line from head to heels.
  • Distribute weight evenly: Your weight should be balanced between your forearms and toes, not shifted forward or backward.
  • Keep shoulders relaxed: While maintaining engagement, avoid shrugging your shoulders toward your ears, which creates unnecessary tension.
  • Quality over quantity: It's better to hold a proper plank for 20 seconds than a poor-form plank for 2 minutes. Focus on form first, then duration.
  • Build gradually: If you're new to planks, start with shorter holds (10-20 seconds) and gradually increase as your strength improves.
  • Create a mind-muscle connection: Actively think about which muscles you're engaging rather than simply enduring the time.
  • Use a timer: Set a timer so you can focus on form rather than counting seconds, which can distract from proper technique.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Sagging hips: Allowing your hips to drop toward the floor puts strain on your lower back and reduces core engagement. Keep your hips in line with your shoulders and ankles.
  • Raising the buttocks: Lifting your hips too high (creating an inverted V shape) reduces the work on your core muscles. Maintain the straight line from head to heels.
  • Looking up: Raising your head to look forward strains your neck and disrupts proper spinal alignment. Keep your neck neutral by gazing at the floor.
  • Holding your breath: This increases internal pressure and can raise blood pressure unnecessarily. Breathe normally throughout the exercise.
  • Incorrect elbow position: Placing elbows too far forward or backward affects leverage and proper muscle engagement. Keep them directly under your shoulders.
  • Shrugging shoulders: This creates unnecessary tension in the neck and shoulders. Keep your shoulders down and away from your ears.
  • Forgetting to engage the core: Simply holding the position without actively engaging your core reduces the effectiveness. Continuously draw your navel toward your spine.

Plank Variations

High Plank

Performed with extended arms and hands placed directly beneath the shoulders, similar to the top position of a push-up.

Side Plank

Performed on one forearm with the body rotated to the side, targeting the obliques and lateral core stabilization.

Plank with Shoulder Taps

From a high plank position, alternately lift one hand to tap the opposite shoulder while maintaining stability.

Plank Jacks

From a forearm or high plank position, jump your feet outward and inward (like a jumping jack), adding a cardiovascular element.
