Hanging Leg Raise

An advanced core exercise that targets the lower abdominals and hip flexors for superior strength and definition

Lower Abs, Hip Flexors
Advanced
Pull-up Bar

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Rectus Abdominis (Lower Portion), Hip Flexors
  • Secondary Muscles: Obliques, Quadriceps, Grip Muscles, Front Deltoids
  • Equipment: Pull-up Bar or Hanging Apparatus
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Advanced
  • Mechanics: Isolation/Compound
  • Force: Hip Flexion

How to Perform the Hanging Leg Raise

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Hang from a pull-up bar with a firm overhand grip slightly wider than shoulder-width apart. Your arms should be fully extended, and your body should hang freely with feet off the ground.

  2. 2

    Establish Stability

    Engage your shoulders by pulling them slightly down and back (away from your ears). This creates a more stable position and prevents excessive swinging. Maintain a slight hollow body position by engaging your core.

  3. 3

    Brace Your Core

    Before initiating the movement, brace your abdominal muscles by drawing your navel toward your spine. This engagement should be maintained throughout the entire exercise to protect your lower back.

  4. 4

    Initiate the Movement

    Keeping your legs straight (or slightly bent for an easier variation), exhale as you slowly raise your legs forward and upward by engaging your lower abdominals and hip flexors. Avoid using momentum or swinging.

  5. 5

    Reach Peak Position

    Continue raising your legs until they are parallel to the ground (90-degree angle to your torso) or higher if possible. For maximum engagement, try to lift your legs until they are perpendicular to the floor (creating an L-shape with your body).

  6. 6

    Controlled Descent

    Inhale as you slowly lower your legs back to the starting position with control. This eccentric (lowering) phase is crucial for development and should not be rushed. Maintain core tension throughout the descent.

  7. 7

    Reset and Repeat

    Briefly reset in the hanging position, ensuring your body is stable before beginning the next repetition. Avoid swinging or using momentum between reps. Typical rep ranges are 8-15 reps for 3-4 sets.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Focus on controlled motion: Quality is far more important than quantity. Perform each repetition with deliberate control rather than rushing through with momentum.
  • Maintain a stable upper body: Keep your shoulders engaged and minimize swinging or excessive movement in your torso.
  • Breathe properly: Exhale during the raising phase and inhale during the lowering phase to maintain proper intra-abdominal pressure.
  • Engage your core continuously: The entire abdominal wall should remain contracted throughout the exercise, especially during the lowering phase.
  • Work within your capability: If you cannot perform a full hanging leg raise with good form, start with one of the progression variations and build up to the full movement.
  • Use grip assistance if needed: If grip strength is limiting your performance, consider using lifting straps or gymnastics grips to focus on the core work.
  • Avoid excessive range of motion: Only go as high as you can while maintaining proper form and core engagement. Excessive range can compromise lower back position.
  • Progress gradually: Add repetitions or sets before increasing technical difficulty with variations like toe-to-bar or L-sit holds.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using momentum: Swinging or kicking the legs up reduces muscle activation and increases risk of injury. Focus on a controlled, deliberate movement.
  • Arching the lower back: Excessive lower back arching during the movement indicates a lack of core control and can lead to lower back strain. Keep your core braced throughout.
  • Incomplete range of motion: Only raising the legs a few inches fails to fully engage the target muscles. Aim to reach at least parallel to the ground.
  • Rapid lowering: Dropping the legs quickly on the descent misses a valuable opportunity for eccentric muscle development and increases injury risk.
  • Pulling with the arms/shoulders: Excessive pulling or shrugging with the upper body shifts focus away from the core muscles. Your arms should only support your body weight.
  • Holding your breath: Breath-holding can increase blood pressure unnecessarily. Maintain a regular breathing pattern throughout the exercise.
  • Bent knees without progression: Using bent knees as a permanent modification rather than as a stepping stone to the full movement will limit development.

Hanging Leg Raise Variations

Hanging Knee Raise

A beginner-friendly variation where the knees are bent throughout the movement, reducing the lever length and making the exercise easier.

Captain's Chair Leg Raise

Performed on a captain's chair station with back support and forearm pads, eliminating grip strength as a limiting factor.

Toes-to-Bar

An advanced variation where you raise your legs until your toes touch the pull-up bar, requiring exceptional core strength and flexibility.

Weighted Hanging Leg Raise

Adding resistance by placing a dumbbell between your feet or using ankle weights to increase difficulty.

Hanging Leg Raise with Rotation

Adding a twisting motion at the top of the movement to engage the obliques while maintaining the leg raise position.

L-Sit Hold

An isometric variation where you hold the legs at a 90-degree angle to the torso for time, developing exceptional core strength and endurance.
