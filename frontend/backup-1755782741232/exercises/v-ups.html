V-Ups

A challenging core exercise that simultaneously targets the upper and lower abdominals for complete core development

Rectus Abdominis, Hip Flexors, Lower Back
Intermediate-Advanced
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Rectus Abdominis, Hip Flexors
  • Secondary Muscles: Lower Back, Adductors, Shoulders
  • Equipment: None (Bodyweight Exercise)
  • Type: Dynamic, Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate-Advanced
  • Force Type: Pull (Flexion)
  • Benefits: Core Strength, Ab Definition, Balance, Coordination

How to Perform V-Ups

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Lie flat on your back on a mat with your arms extended overhead and your legs straight. Your body should form a long line from fingertips to toes. Press your lower back into the mat to engage your core from the beginning.

  2. 2

    Core Engagement

    Before initiating the movement, engage your core by drawing your navel toward your spine. This creates tension in your core and helps protect your lower back throughout the exercise.

  3. 3

    Simultaneous Lift

    In one coordinated movement, simultaneously raise your straight arms and legs toward each other, lifting your shoulder blades and lower back off the mat. Your body will form a "V" shape at the top of the movement, with only your tailbone and lower glutes in contact with the floor.

  4. 4

    Peak Position

    At the top of the movement, try to touch your fingertips to your toes or get them as close as possible while maintaining straight limbs. Focus on using your abdominals to create the lift rather than momentum or hip flexor dominance.

  5. 5

    Controlled Descent

    Slowly lower your arms and legs back to the starting position in a controlled manner, maintaining core engagement throughout. Avoid letting your limbs crash to the floor, which can strain your lower back.

  6. 6

    Bottom Position

    In the bottom position, your arms and legs should hover slightly above the ground (about 2-3 inches) rather than completely resting on the floor. This maintains tension in your core throughout the set.

  7. 7

    Breathing Pattern

    Exhale as you lift into the V position and inhale as you lower back down. This breathing pattern helps maintain core engagement and provides a rhythm for the exercise.

  8. 8

    Complete Repetitions

    Continue performing repetitions with proper form for the prescribed number (typically 8-15 reps per set for most individuals). Focus on quality over quantity, especially when first learning the movement.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Maintain a neutral spine: While your lower back will naturally lift off the ground during the movement, avoid excessive arching or rounding during the exercise.
  • Engage your core throughout: Keep your abdominals contracted during the entire exercise, including the lowering phase and bottom position.
  • Focus on control: The effectiveness of V-ups comes from controlled movement rather than momentum. Avoid jerking or swinging to complete repetitions.
  • Keep limbs straight: Maintaining straight arms and legs increases the leverage and challenge to your core. If this is too difficult initially, a slight bend is acceptable while building strength.
  • Hand position: Keep your hands alongside your ears rather than behind your head to avoid neck strain and to create proper leverage for the movement.
  • Tailbone anchoring: Think about keeping your tailbone in contact with the floor throughout the movement to maintain proper positioning.
  • Gaze direction: Look at your knees or slightly above rather than tucking your chin excessively, which can strain your neck.
  • Range of motion: If you cannot perform the full movement with proper form, reduce the range of motion rather than compromising technique.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using momentum: Swinging or jerking to complete repetitions reduces core engagement and increases injury risk. Focus on slow, controlled movement generated by your abdominals.
  • Allowing lower back to arch excessively: This puts undue stress on the spine and indicates inadequate core engagement. Keep your lower back pressed into the mat at the start and finish of each rep.
  • Pulling on your neck: If you place hands behind your head, there's a tendency to pull forward with the arms, creating neck strain. Keep hands alongside ears or extended straight.
  • Incomplete range of motion: Not lowering fully or lifting high enough reduces the effectiveness of the exercise. Work through the full range that your flexibility and strength allow while maintaining proper form.
  • Bending knees and elbows excessively: While slight bending is acceptable for beginners, excessive bending significantly reduces the core challenge. Work toward straighter limbs as strength improves.
  • Holding breath: This creates unnecessary pressure and tension. Establish a consistent breathing pattern that supports the movement.
  • Hip flexor dominance: Relying too heavily on hip flexors rather than abdominals to lift the legs. Focus on initiating the movement from your core.

V-Up Variations

Bent-Knee V-Ups

A modified version with bent knees, reducing the lever length and making the exercise more accessible for beginners or those building strength.

Alternating V-Ups

Lifting opposite arm and leg (like reaching right arm to left leg), creating rotational challenge while reducing difficulty compared to standard V-ups.

Weighted V-Ups

Adding a light weight (medicine ball or dumbbell) held in the hands to increase resistance and challenge for advanced practitioners.

V-Up Hold

Maintaining the "V" position at the top of the movement for time (typically 10-30 seconds) to build isometric strength and endurance.
