Dead Bug

A beginner-friendly core stability exercise that teaches proper core engagement and spinal alignment while moving limbs

Core, Abs, Lower Back
Beginner
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Transverse Abdominis, Rectus Abdominis
  • Secondary Muscles: Hip Flexors, Obliques, Lower Back
  • Equipment: None Required
  • Type: Core Stability
  • Experience Level: Beginner
  • Mechanics: Anti-Extension
  • Force: Isometric/Dynamic

Video Demonstration

How to Perform the Dead Bug

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Lie flat on your back on a mat with your arms extended straight up toward the ceiling, perpendicular to your torso. Bend your knees and lift your feet off the floor so that your thighs form a 90-degree angle with your torso, and your lower legs are parallel to the floor (tabletop position).

  2. 2

    Core Engagement

    Draw your navel toward your spine by contracting your abdominal muscles. Press your lower back firmly into the mat – this is crucial for proper form. Maintain this core engagement throughout the exercise to protect your spine and effectively target your deep core muscles.

  3. 3

    Breathing Pattern

    Inhale deeply to prepare. As you begin the movement (next step), exhale slowly and continuously while maintaining core tension. Your breathing should be controlled and deliberate throughout the exercise.

  4. 4

    Contralateral Movement

    While maintaining core tension and keeping your lower back pressed into the mat, simultaneously:

    • Lower your right arm back overhead toward the floor behind you
    • Extend your left leg forward, lowering it toward the floor

    Lower your limbs only as far as you can while keeping your lower back firmly pressed into the mat. This might not be all the way to the floor initially.

  5. 5

    Return to Starting Position

    Exhale as you bring your extended arm and leg back to the starting position. The movement should be slow and controlled, not using momentum.

  6. 6

    Alternate Sides

    Repeat the movement on the opposite side, lowering your left arm overhead while extending your right leg forward. Continue alternating sides for the prescribed number of repetitions.

  7. 7

    Maintain Spinal Position

    Throughout the entire exercise, focus on keeping your spine neutral and your lower back pressed firmly into the mat. If your lower back begins to arch away from the mat, reduce the range of motion of your limbs or reset your position.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Lower back position: The most critical aspect of this exercise is maintaining contact between your lower back and the mat. If your back arches, you've extended your limbs too far for your current core strength.
  • Tempo control: Perform the exercise slowly and with control. Aim for 2-3 seconds lowering the limbs and 1-2 seconds returning to the starting position.
  • Core activation cue: Imagine drawing your belly button toward your spine or trying to flatten your lower back into the floor to ensure proper core engagement.
  • Arm position: Keep your arms straight but not locked, with shoulders relaxed away from your ears.
  • Leg position: In the starting position, your hips and knees should be at 90-degree angles. As you extend your leg, keep a slight bend in the knee rather than fully locking it.
  • Movement size: Start with smaller movements and gradually increase the range of motion as you build strength, always prioritizing lower back contact with the mat.
  • Progressive overload: As you get stronger, challenge yourself by bringing limbs closer to the floor, extending both arm and leg on the same side, or eventually moving opposing arm and leg simultaneously.
  • Focus on quality: The dead bug is about controlled, precise movement rather than high repetitions. Concentrate on perfect form for each repetition.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Arching the lower back: The most common mistake is allowing the lower back to arch off the mat during the movement. This reduces core engagement and can stress the spine. If this happens, reduce your range of motion or reset.
  • Moving too quickly: Rushing through repetitions with momentum defeats the purpose of this stability exercise. Maintain slow, controlled movements throughout.
  • Holding your breath: Many beginners hold their breath during difficult movements. Instead, focus on controlled breathing coordinated with your movements.
  • Insufficient core engagement: Simply going through the motions without actively engaging the core won't yield benefits. Keep the core activated throughout the entire set.
  • Head lifting off the mat: Straining to watch your movements can lead to neck strain. Keep your head relaxed on the mat, with your gaze toward the ceiling.
  • Starting too advanced: Beginning with limbs extended too far or moving opposite limbs before mastering the basics. Progress gradually through the variations.
  • Inconsistent tempo: Varying speed throughout the movement or between repetitions. Maintain a steady, controlled pace for maximum benefit.

Dead Bug Variations

Marching Dead Bug

A simplified version where you only extend and lower one limb at a time while maintaining the tabletop position with the other limbs.

Ipsilateral Dead Bug

Extending the arm and leg on the same side of the body simultaneously, which creates different stabilization demands on the core.

Weighted Dead Bug

Adding resistance by holding light dumbbells, a medicine ball, or resistance bands to increase the challenge to the core stabilizing muscles.

Straight Leg Dead Bug

Keeping legs straight throughout the movement, which significantly increases the lever length and demand on the hip flexors and core.
