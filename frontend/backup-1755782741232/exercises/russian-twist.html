Russian Twist

A dynamic rotational core exercise that targets the obliques and builds trunk stability

Obliques, Abs, Hip Flexors
Intermediate
Bodyweight/Optional Weight

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Internal/External Obliques, Rectus Abdominis
  • Secondary Muscles: Hip Flexors, Erector Spinae, Transverse Abdominis
  • Equipment: None (can add weight for progression)
  • Type: Rotational Core
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Isolation
  • Force: Rotation

How to Perform the Russian Twist

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Sit on the floor with your knees bent and feet flat. Lean back slightly to create approximately a 45-degree angle between your torso and the floor. Keep your back straight throughout the exercise—avoid rounding your lower back.

  2. 2

    Elevate Your Feet (Optional)

    For increased difficulty, lift your feet slightly off the ground. This creates an unstable base that increases core engagement. Beginners may keep their feet on the ground for stability.

  3. 3

    Position Your Arms

    Extend your arms straight out in front of your chest with hands together or clasped. If using weight, hold a medicine ball, dumbbell, or weight plate with both hands at chest level.

  4. 4

    Engage Your Core

    Brace your abdominal muscles by drawing your navel in toward your spine. This engagement should be maintained throughout the entire exercise to protect your lower back and maximize effectiveness.

  5. 5

    Initiate Rotation

    Keeping your arms straight and torso fixed at the 45-degree angle, rotate your shoulders and arms to one side. The movement should come from your midsection (obliques), not from your arms or shoulders.

  6. 6

    Complete the Twist

    Rotate as far as comfortably possible to one side, ideally until your arms are parallel to the floor. Pause briefly at the end range to maximize oblique contraction.

  7. 7

    Return and Rotate to Opposite Side

    Rotate back through center and continue the movement to the opposite side, completing one full repetition. Maintain controlled movement throughout; avoid using momentum to swing from side to side.

  8. 8

    Repeat

    Continue alternating from side to side for the prescribed number of repetitions. Typical rep ranges are 10-20 twists per side for 2-3 sets.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Keep your chest up: Maintain good posture with a proud chest to prevent rounding your back, which can strain the lower spine.
  • Focus on rotation quality: Prioritize a full, controlled rotation rather than rushing through high repetitions with poor form.
  • Breathe rhythmically: Exhale as you rotate to each side and inhale as you move through the center position.
  • Maintain height: Don't let your torso collapse down as you fatigue. Keep the 45-degree angle throughout the set.
  • Engage your core continuously: Your abdominals should stay tight throughout the exercise to protect your spine and maximize benefits.
  • Control the tempo: Avoid rapid, jerky movements that use momentum rather than muscle engagement. Aim for a 2-second rotation to each side.
  • Elevate progressively: If you're a beginner, start with feet on the floor and progress to elevated feet once you've mastered the movement.
  • Add weight gradually: If adding resistance, start light and increase slowly only after perfect form is established.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Rounding the lower back: This puts excessive stress on the lumbar spine and reduces core engagement. Maintain a neutral spine throughout the exercise.
  • Moving only the arms: The rotation should come from your torso, not just swinging your arms side to side. Your shoulders should rotate with your arms.
  • Using momentum: Swinging rapidly reduces muscle activation and increases injury risk. Control the movement in both directions.
  • Excessive range of motion: Twisting too far can strain the spine. Rotate only to a comfortable range where you feel your obliques working.
  • Lifting the hips: Your hips should remain stable and in contact with the ground. Avoid letting them shift from side to side during rotation.
  • Starting with too much weight: Adding heavy resistance before mastering proper form can lead to compensation patterns and potential injury.
  • Losing the V-position: As fatigue sets in, many people collapse their torso toward the floor. Maintain the 45-degree angle throughout the entire set.

Russian Twist Variations

Weighted Russian Twist

Adding a dumbbell, medicine ball, or weight plate increases resistance and intensifies the exercise.

Elevated Feet Russian Twist

Lifting the feet off the ground increases instability and engages more core muscles for balance.

Medicine Ball Russian Twist Throw

A dynamic variation where you throw a medicine ball against a wall with each rotation, adding power development.

Weighted Elevated Russian Twist

Combining elevated feet with added resistance for maximum challenge to the core muscles.

Decline Russian Twist

Performed on a decline bench to increase the load on the abdominals and make the exercise more challenging.

Standing Cable Russian Twist

Performed standing with a cable machine for consistent resistance throughout the movement.
