Bicep Curl

The fundamental isolation exercise for developing stronger, more defined biceps

Arms, Biceps
Beginner
Dumbbells, Barbell, or Resistance Bands

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Biceps Brachii
  • Secondary Muscles: Brachialis, Brachioradialis, Forearm Flexors
  • Equipment: Dumbbells, Barbell, EZ Bar, or Resistance Bands
  • Type: Isolation
  • Experience Level: Beginner
  • Mechanics: Single Joint
  • Force: Pull (Elbow Flexion)

How to Perform the Bicep Curl

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand with feet shoulder-width apart and knees slightly bent. Hold a dumbbell in each hand with your arms fully extended by your sides, palms facing forward (supinated grip). Keep your shoulders relaxed and back straight.

  2. 2

    Brace Your Core

    Engage your core muscles and maintain a neutral spine position. Keep your elbows close to your sides throughout the movement - they should act as hinges and remain relatively stationary.

  3. 3

    Curl Phase

    Keeping your upper arms stationary, exhale as you curl the weights while contracting your biceps. Continue lifting until the dumbbells are at shoulder level and your biceps are fully contracted.

  4. 4

    Peak Contraction

    At the top of the movement, squeeze your biceps for a brief pause to maximize contraction. Your palms should be facing your shoulders, with dumbbells near shoulder height.

  5. 5

    Lowering Phase

    Inhale as you slowly lower the weights back to the starting position, controlling the descent. Focus on resisting gravity rather than just dropping your arms.

  6. 6

    Full Extension

    Lower the weights until your arms are fully extended at the bottom position, allowing a slight stretch in the biceps without losing tension completely.

  7. 7

    Repeat

    Perform the desired number of repetitions, maintaining control and proper form throughout each repetition. A typical range for bicep curls is 8-12 reps per set.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Minimize momentum: Avoid swinging your body or using momentum to lift the weights. The movement should come solely from your biceps contracting.
  • Control the eccentric: The lowering (eccentric) phase is just as important as the lifting phase. Lower the weights slowly and with control.
  • Keep elbows stable: Your elbows should remain close to your sides and not move forward or outward during the exercise.
  • Wrist position: Maintain straight wrists throughout the movement - avoid excessive wrist flexion or extension to prevent strain.
  • Focus on contraction: Establish a mind-muscle connection by focusing on feeling your biceps contract through the full range of motion.
  • Appropriate weight: Choose a weight that allows you to perform the exercise with proper form. If you can't maintain form, the weight is too heavy.
  • Breathing: Exhale during the lifting phase (concentric) and inhale during the lowering phase (eccentric).
  • Full range of motion: Allow your arms to fully extend at the bottom to work the biceps through their complete range.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using momentum: Swinging the torso or using shoulder movement to lift the weight reduces bicep engagement and increases injury risk.
  • Moving the elbows: Allowing the elbows to drift forward or away from the body changes the mechanics of the exercise and reduces effectiveness.
  • Incomplete range of motion: Not fully extending the arms at the bottom or not curling completely to the top limits muscle development.
  • Lifting too heavy: Using weights that are too heavy forces you to compromise form and reduces the effectiveness of the exercise.
  • Excessive wrist movement: Excessively flexing or extending the wrists can lead to strain and reduces bicep activation.
  • Rushing repetitions: Performing reps too quickly reduces time under tension and diminishes muscle development.
  • Shoulder involvement: Raising the shoulders during the movement takes tension away from the biceps and can lead to shoulder problems.

Bicep Curl Variations

Barbell Curl

Using a barbell allows for heavier loads and helps address muscle imbalances between arms.

Hammer Curl

Performed with a neutral grip (palms facing each other) to target the brachialis and brachioradialis.

Concentration Curl

Seated variation with elbow braced against inner thigh for maximum isolation and peak contraction.

Preacher Curl

Performed with arms resting on an angled pad to eliminate momentum and focus on the lower portion of the biceps.
