Good Morning

A powerful hip-hinge exercise that strengthens your posterior chain and develops core stability

Lower Back, Hamstrings, Glutes
Intermediate
Barbell or Bodyweight

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Erector Spinae (Lower Back), Hamstrings
  • Secondary Muscles: Gluteus Maximus (Glutes), Core, Upper Back
  • Equipment: Barbell or None (Bodyweight)
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Hip Hinge
  • Force: Hinge (Posterior Chain)

Video Demonstration

How to Perform the Good Morning

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart, toes pointing slightly outward. For the barbell version, position a barbell across your upper back (trapezius muscles), similar to a back squat position. Hold the bar in place with your hands wider than shoulder-width. For the bodyweight version, place your hands behind your head or cross your arms in front of your chest.

  2. 2

    Bracing

    Take a deep breath into your belly and brace your core by contracting your abdominal muscles. Keep your chest up, shoulders back and down, and maintain a neutral spine position. This strong bracing is crucial to protect your lower back throughout the movement.

  3. 3

    Initiate the Hip Hinge

    Begin by pushing your hips backward while maintaining a slight bend in your knees. Think of hinging at the hips rather than bending at the waist. Your shins should stay relatively vertical throughout the movement.

  4. 4

    Lowering Phase

    Continue to push your hips back as your torso leans forward. Keep your back flat (neutral spine) and core engaged throughout the movement. Your gaze should be directed a few feet in front of you on the floor to maintain proper neck alignment.

  5. 5

    Bottom Position

    Lower until you feel a significant stretch in your hamstrings or until your torso is roughly parallel to the floor. For beginners, a 45-degree angle may be a more appropriate depth. Stop before your lower back begins to round.

  6. 6

    Ascending Phase

    Drive your hips forward by contracting your glutes and hamstrings to return to the starting position. Maintain the same flat back position during the ascent as you did during the descent. Think of this as a hip thrust motion rather than lifting with your back.

  7. 7

    Top Position

    Return to the starting position, standing tall with your chest up, shoulders back, and core engaged. Squeeze your glutes at the top to achieve full hip extension. Do not lean backward at the top—maintain a neutral position.

  8. 8

    Repeat

    Perform the desired number of repetitions, maintaining proper form throughout. For beginners, start with 2-3 sets of 8-10 reps using just your bodyweight or a very light barbell to master the hip hinge pattern.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Hip hinge focus: The good morning is a hip hinge exercise, not a squat or a back bend. The primary movement should be your hips moving backward and forward while your spine maintains its natural curve.
  • Back position: Maintain a neutral spine throughout the entire movement. Avoid rounding your lower back or excessively arching it—both can lead to injuries.
  • Bar position: For the barbell version, ensure the bar rests on your trapezius muscles (upper back), not on your neck or cervical spine. It should be positioned similar to where you'd place it for a back squat.
  • Knee bend: Keep a slight, consistent bend in your knees throughout the exercise. Don't lock them out or bend them significantly more during the movement.
  • Range of motion: Only go as far as you can maintain a neutral spine. For most people, this is when the torso is parallel to the floor or at a 45-degree angle for beginners.
  • Foot pressure: Keep your weight distributed evenly across your feet with an emphasis on the mid-foot to heel. Your toes should remain in contact with the ground.
  • Neck alignment: Keep your neck in line with your spine—don't look up or tuck your chin excessively. Focus on a spot on the floor a few feet in front of you.
  • Start light: For barbell good mornings, use significantly less weight than you would for squat or deadlift. This exercise places high demand on your spinal erectors and can be challenging even with light loads.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Rounding the back: This significantly increases the risk of spinal injury. Always maintain a neutral spine position throughout the entire movement.
  • Bending at the waist rather than hinging at the hips: This turns the exercise into a back-dominant movement rather than a posterior chain exercise. Focus on pushing your hips backward rather than just leaning forward.
  • Using too much weight: Good mornings require relatively light loads compared to other barbell exercises. Using too much weight often leads to compromised form and increased injury risk.
  • Insufficient knee bend: Locking the knees places excessive strain on the hamstrings and lower back. Maintain a slight bend in your knees throughout the movement.
  • Looking up/hyperextending the neck: This disrupts proper spinal alignment. Keep your gaze neutral and your neck in line with the rest of your spine.
  • Insufficient bracing: Failing to engage your core properly leaves your spine vulnerable. Create and maintain intra-abdominal pressure throughout the entire movement.
  • Bouncing at the bottom: Using momentum reduces the effectiveness of the exercise and increases injury risk. Move in a controlled manner throughout the entire range of motion.

Good Morning Variations

Bodyweight Good Morning

Performed without external resistance, perfect for beginners to master the hip hinge pattern with minimal risk.

Seated Good Morning

Performed while sitting on a bench, which increases hamstring activation and reduces the ability to cheat using momentum.

Banded Good Morning

Using resistance bands instead of a barbell creates a different resistance profile with more tension at the top of the movement.

Single-Leg Good Morning

An advanced variation performed on one leg that increases balance challenge and addresses left-right asymmetries.
