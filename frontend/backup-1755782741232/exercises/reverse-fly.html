Reverse Fly

Target your rear deltoids and upper back to improve posture and build balanced shoulder development

Rear Deltoids, Rhomboids, Middle Trapezius
Beginner-Intermediate
Dumbbells, Bench (optional)

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Posterior Deltoids (Rear Shoulders)
  • Secondary Muscles: Rhomboids, Middle Trapezius, Infraspinatus
  • Equipment: Dumbbells, Incline Bench (optional)
  • Type: Isolation
  • Experience Level: Beginner-Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Isolation
  • Force: Pull (Horizontal)

How to Perform the Reverse Fly

  1. 1

    Equipment Setup

    Select a pair of light dumbbells—this exercise typically requires lighter weights than many other exercises. You can perform reverse flies while standing, seated on a bench, or bent over an incline bench for added support.

  2. 2

    Starting Position (Bent-Over Variation)

    Stand with your feet shoulder-width apart, knees slightly bent. Hinge at your hips to bend forward until your torso is nearly parallel to the floor (about a 45-60 degree angle). Keep your back flat, not rounded. Let your arms hang straight down from your shoulders with a dumbbell in each hand, palms facing each other (neutral grip).

  3. 3

    Shoulder Positioning

    Before initiating the movement, pull your shoulder blades slightly together and down (retraction and depression). This positioning helps engage the correct muscles and protects your shoulder joints during the exercise.

  4. 4

    The Movement

    Keeping a slight bend in your elbows (not completely straight, not significantly bent), raise the dumbbells out to the sides in an arcing motion. Focus on using your rear deltoids to perform the movement, not your upper traps or biceps. Imagine you're "opening your arms like wings" or "squeezing a pencil between your shoulder blades."

  5. 5

    Top Position

    At the top of the movement, your arms should be out to the sides with your elbows at approximately shoulder height or slightly below. Avoid raising your arms above shoulder level as this shifts the work to different muscles and can increase shoulder strain. Squeeze your shoulder blades together for a moment at the top position.

  6. 6

    Return Phase

    Slowly lower the weights back to the starting position in a controlled manner. Don't let gravity take over—the lowering phase should be deliberate and controlled to maintain muscle tension.

  7. 7

    Breathing Pattern

    Inhale during the lowering phase and exhale as you raise the weights. This breathing pattern helps maintain core stability and proper form throughout the exercise.

  8. 8

    Repeat

    Complete the desired number of repetitions with controlled form. When finished, return to an upright position by engaging your core and glutes, not by rounding your lower back.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Use appropriate weight: Start with lighter dumbbells than you might expect. The rear delts are relatively small muscles and typically weaker than other shoulder muscles.
  • Focus on the rear delts: Concentrate on feeling the contraction in the back of your shoulders. This mind-muscle connection is critical for targeting the correct muscles.
  • Maintain elbow position: Keep a consistent slight bend in your elbows throughout the movement—don't straighten or bend them significantly during the exercise.
  • Control the movement: Perform both the lifting and lowering phases in a controlled manner to maximize muscle engagement and prevent momentum from taking over.
  • Protect your lower back: Maintain a flat back throughout the movement, engaging your core to stabilize your spine. If maintaining this position is difficult, consider using an incline bench for support.
  • Vary hand positions: You can perform this exercise with palms facing down (pronated grip) or facing each other (neutral grip). The pronated grip places more emphasis on the rear delts, while the neutral grip may be more comfortable for those with shoulder issues.
  • Consider range of motion: Only lift to shoulder height or slightly below. Going higher shifts the work to the middle delts and can place stress on the shoulder joint.
  • Add this exercise later in your workout: Since it targets smaller stabilizing muscles, perform reverse flies after your main compound movements when those muscles aren't as fatigued.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using momentum: Swinging the weights or using body movement to lift the dumbbells reduces the effectiveness of the exercise and increases injury risk. Control is key for this isolation movement.
  • Lifting too heavy: Using weights that are too heavy often leads to compensatory movements and poor form. The rear delts are smaller muscles that respond better to lighter weights and proper form.
  • Shrugging the shoulders: Raising your shoulders toward your ears during the movement activates the upper trapezius instead of the targeted rear deltoids. Keep your shoulders down and back.
  • Rounding the back: Allowing your spine to round during the bent-over position places stress on your lower back and reduces the effectiveness of the exercise. Maintain a flat back with core engagement.
  • Raising arms too high: Lifting the dumbbells above shoulder height changes the working muscles and can cause shoulder impingement. Keep the arms at or slightly below shoulder height at the top of the movement.
  • Insufficient range of motion: Not bringing the weights up high enough (stopping well before shoulder level) reduces the effectiveness of the exercise for the rear delts.
  • Flaring the elbows: Significantly changing the elbow angle during the movement can shift the work away from the target muscles and potentially strain the joints.

Reverse Fly Variations

Incline Bench Reverse Fly

Performing the exercise while lying chest-down on an incline bench for enhanced stability and potentially greater range of motion.

Cable Reverse Fly

Using cable machines to perform reverse flies, providing constant tension throughout the movement.

Resistance Band Reverse Fly

Using resistance bands instead of weights, ideal for home workouts or travel.

Single-Arm Reverse Fly

Performing the exercise one arm at a time, allowing greater focus on form and addressing muscle imbalances.
