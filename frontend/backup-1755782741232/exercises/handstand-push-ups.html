Handstand Push-Ups

An advanced bodyweight exercise that builds tremendous shoulder strength, upper body power, and balance

Shoulders, Triceps, Traps
Advanced
Wall (Optional)

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Deltoids (Shoulders), Triceps
  • Secondary Muscles: Trapezius, Upper Chest, Core, Serratus Anterior
  • Equipment: Wall (for support), Optional: Parallettes
  • Type: Compound, Bodyweight
  • Experience Level: Advanced
  • Mechanics: Push (Vertical)
  • Force: Push

Path to Handstand Push-Ups

The handstand push-up is an advanced exercise that requires considerable upper body strength, shoulder mobility, and balance. Before attempting full handstand push-ups, progress through these steps:

  1. Pike Push-Ups: Begin with feet elevated on a bench or chair, hips raised high to form an inverted V, and perform push-ups with your head pointing down.
  2. Box Pike Push-Ups: Same as pike push-ups but with hands elevated on parallel boxes or parallettes for deeper range of motion.
  3. Wall Handstand Hold: Practice getting into and holding a wall-supported handstand position to build shoulder endurance and balance.
  4. Negative Handstand Push-Ups: Start at the top position of a wall handstand and slowly lower yourself down, using your legs to assist in returning to the top.
  5. Partial Handstand Push-Ups: Perform handstand push-ups with limited range of motion, gradually increasing depth as strength improves.
  6. Full Handstand Push-Ups: Complete the full exercise with proper form once you've developed the necessary strength and balance.

Each stage may take weeks or months to master. Progress at your own pace and ensure you can perform 5-8 clean repetitions at each level before advancing.

How to Perform Handstand Push-Ups

  1. 1

    Setup Against a Wall

    Face a wall and place your hands on the floor about 6-8 inches away from the wall. Your hands should be shoulder-width apart or slightly wider, with fingers spread and pointing forward.

  2. 2

    Enter the Handstand Position

    Kick up one leg followed by the other to get into a handstand position with your heels resting against the wall for support. Your body should form a straight line from hands to heels, with your core engaged and head in a neutral position between your arms.

  3. 3

    Stabilize the Position

    Take a moment to stabilize yourself. Ensure your weight is evenly distributed between both arms, your shoulders are engaged, and your body is aligned. Look at a spot on the floor between your hands.

  4. 4

    Lowering Phase

    Maintaining a tight core and straight body, slowly bend your elbows to lower your body toward the floor. Your elbows should move out to the sides at approximately a 45-degree angle from your torso. Lower until the top of your head lightly touches the floor or as far as your shoulder mobility allows.

  5. 5

    Bottom Position

    At the bottom of the movement, your head should lightly touch the ground (or hover just above if using parallettes) with your elbows bent and pointing outward. Maintain tension in your shoulders and core, avoiding any collapse or pike in your hips.

  6. 6

    Pressing Phase

    Push through your palms and extend your arms to drive your body back up to the starting position. Focus on keeping your body in a straight line and engaging your shoulders and triceps fully throughout the movement.

  7. 7

    Repetition

    Once at the top position, stabilize yourself before beginning the next repetition. Continue for the desired number of repetitions or until form begins to deteriorate. To exit, carefully lower your legs one at a time back to the ground.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Hand placement: Position your hands directly under your shoulders or slightly wider, with fingers spread to create a stable base.
  • Body alignment: Maintain a straight line from your hands to your heels throughout the movement, avoiding arching or piking at the hips.
  • Head position: Keep your head in a neutral position, neither looking up nor tucking excessively. Your gaze should be directed at the floor between your hands.
  • Elbow path: As you lower, let your elbows flare out at approximately a 45-degree angle from your torso, not directly to the sides or tucked in close to your body.
  • Breathing: Inhale as you lower and exhale forcefully as you push back up to help generate power.
  • Core engagement: Keep your core tight throughout the movement to maintain proper body alignment and protect your lower back.
  • Control the movement: Lower yourself slowly and with control rather than dropping quickly, which can strain your shoulders and neck.
  • Range of motion: Work within your current mobility limitations. If shoulder mobility is limited, use parallettes or place your hands on elevated surfaces to allow for more range without touching your head to the ground.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Piking at the hips: Bending at the waist during the movement makes it easier but reduces shoulder engagement and can strain the lower back. Maintain a straight body line throughout.
  • Insufficient shoulder strength: Attempting handstand push-ups before developing adequate shoulder strength can lead to injury. Follow the progression path to build the necessary foundation.
  • Collapsing through the shoulders: Allowing the shoulders to collapse toward the ears at the bottom position puts stress on the neck and reduces effectiveness. Maintain tension and avoid fully relaxing at the bottom.
  • Incorrect head position: Looking up or tucking the chin excessively can strain the neck. Keep your head in a neutral position between your arms.
  • Inconsistent depth: Varying how far you lower yourself across repetitions. Aim for consistent depth with each repetition for balanced development.
  • Bouncing off the floor: Using momentum by bouncing your head off the floor is dangerous and ineffective. Control the movement at all times.
  • Overarching the back: Excessive arching of the lower back reduces core engagement and can lead to back strain. Keep your core tight and body straight.

Handstand Push-Up Variations

Pike Push-Up

A more accessible variation with feet on the ground, hips raised high, and upper body inverted—perfect for beginners building toward handstands.

Deficit Handstand Push-Up

Performed with hands elevated on parallettes or blocks to allow for greater range of motion and increased difficulty.

Free-Standing Handstand Push-Up

The ultimate variation performed without wall support, requiring exceptional balance, strength, and body control.

90-Degree Push-Up

A unique variation where the body forms an L-shape with legs parallel to the ground, emphasizing shoulder strength and core stability.
