Straight Arm Pulldown

Isolate your lats while strengthening shoulder stability with this essential cable exercise

Latissimus Dorsi, Serratus, Shoulders
Intermediate
Cable Machine, Straight or Rope Attachment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Latissimus Dorsi (Lats)
  • Secondary Muscles: Serratus Anterior, Teres Major, Posterior Deltoids
  • Equipment: Cable Machine with Straight Bar or Rope Attachment
  • Type: Isolation
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Pull
  • Force: Pull (Vertical)

Video Demonstration

How to Perform the Straight Arm Pulldown

  1. 1

    Machine Setup

    Attach a straight bar or rope attachment to a high pulley on a cable machine. Select an appropriate weight—this exercise typically requires less weight than traditional pulling movements due to the decreased mechanical advantage.

  2. 2

    Starting Position

    Stand facing the cable machine, about 1-2 feet away from it. Grasp the attachment with both hands using an overhand grip (palms facing down). Step back slightly to create tension in the cable. Your feet should be shoulder-width apart, knees slightly bent.

  3. 3

    Body Alignment

    Stand tall with a neutral spine and slight forward lean from the hips (about 20-30 degrees). Engage your core to maintain this position throughout the exercise. Start with your arms extended forward and slightly above shoulder height.

  4. 4

    The Movement

    Keeping your arms completely straight (a slight bend at the elbows is acceptable to reduce joint stress), pull the attachment down in an arcing motion toward your thighs. Focus on using your lats, not your arms or shoulders, to drive the movement.

  5. 5

    Bottom Position

    At the bottom of the movement, the attachment should be near your thighs. Your arms should remain straight, with your shoulders down and back. Squeeze your lats for a moment at this position.

  6. 6

    Return Phase

    Slowly allow your arms to rise back to the starting position in a controlled manner, resisting the pull of the weight. Maintain the slight forward lean and keep your core engaged throughout this phase.

  7. 7

    Top Position

    At the top of the movement, your arms should be extended forward and slightly above shoulder height, with a slight stretch in the lats, but don't allow your shoulders to shrug up toward your ears.

  8. 8

    Repeat

    Complete the desired number of repetitions with controlled form, focusing on feeling the lats work throughout the entire range of motion.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Maintain locked arms: The key to this exercise is keeping your arms straight throughout the movement to isolate the lats and minimize bicep engagement.
  • Focus on the lats: Mentally concentrate on using your back muscles to pull the cable down, not your arms. Think about your arms as hooks connecting your lats to the cable.
  • Avoid excessive lean: While a slight forward lean is normal, excessive bending at the waist turns this into a different exercise and reduces lat activation.
  • Control the eccentric: Don't let the weight stack pull your arms back up rapidly. The controlled return phase is crucial for full benefit.
  • Keep shoulders down: Throughout the movement, keep your shoulders pulled down away from your ears to maximize lat engagement and protect your shoulder joints.
  • Experiment with angles: Slightly adjusting your distance from the cable machine or degree of forward lean can change which part of the lats is emphasized.
  • Use appropriate weight: This exercise is more challenging than it appears—start lighter than you think you need and focus on technique and muscle connection.
  • Breathe properly: Exhale as you pull down, inhale as you return to the starting position.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Bending the arms: This reduces lat engagement and turns the movement into more of a tricep pushdown or standard lat pulldown. Focus on keeping arms straight throughout.
  • Using momentum: Swinging your body or using momentum to move the weight reduces the effectiveness of the exercise and increases injury risk.
  • Insufficient range of motion: Not bringing the attachment all the way down to the thighs, or not allowing a full stretch at the top, limits the benefits of the exercise.
  • Shrugging the shoulders: Allowing your shoulders to rise toward your ears during the movement, especially at the top position, reduces lat activation and can cause shoulder impingement.
  • Excessive forward lean: Bending too far forward from the waist changes the angle of pull and reduces the focus on the lats.
  • Wrist flexion/extension: Allowing your wrists to bend excessively during the movement can cause strain. Keep wrists in a neutral position.
  • Too heavy weight: Using excessive weight forces you to compromise form and often leads to using momentum or bending the arms to complete the movement.

Straight Arm Pulldown Variations

Rope Straight Arm Pulldown

Using a rope attachment allows for a more natural wrist position and can provide a slightly different angle of pull.

Resistance Band Straight Arm Pulldown

Performing the movement with a resistance band anchored above head height, perfect for home workouts or when traveling.

Kneeling Straight Arm Pulldown

Performing the exercise from a kneeling position to increase the range of motion and further isolate the lats.

Single-Arm Straight Arm Pulldown

Working one arm at a time to address muscle imbalances and increase core involvement for stability.
