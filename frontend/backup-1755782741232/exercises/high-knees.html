High Knees

An effective cardio exercise that elevates heart rate, improves coordination, and strengthens your lower body

Cardio, Lower Body
Beginner
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Hip Flexors, Quadriceps
  • Secondary Muscles: Calves, Hamstrings, Glutes, Core
  • Equipment: None
  • Type: Cardio, Dynamic
  • Experience Level: Beginner
  • Mechanics: Compound
  • Force: Pull (Hip Flexion)

How to Perform High Knees

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Stand tall with your feet hip-width apart, arms at your sides. Maintain good posture with your shoulders back, core engaged, and gaze forward.

  2. 2

    Arm Position

    Bend your arms at approximately 90 degrees with elbows at your sides, positioned as if you were running. Your hands should be roughly at waist level.

  3. 3

    Knee Lift

    Lift your right knee upward toward your chest, aiming to raise it at least to hip height or ideally higher. The movement should be driven from your hip flexors.

  4. 4

    Return and Alternate

    Quickly lower your right foot back to the ground while simultaneously lifting your left knee toward your chest in the same manner.

  5. 5

    Establish Rhythm

    Continue alternating knees in a running-in-place motion. Start at a comfortable pace and gradually increase speed as your coordination and cardiovascular fitness improve.

  6. 6

    Arm Movement

    Coordinate your arm and leg movements by pumping your arms oppositely to your legs. As your right knee lifts, your left arm comes forward, and vice versa.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Focus on height, not speed: Especially when learning the movement, prioritize lifting your knees higher rather than moving faster. Speed will come naturally with practice.
  • Maintain upright posture: Keep your chest up and shoulders back throughout the exercise. Avoid leaning forward at the hips or hunching your shoulders.
  • Land softly: Use the balls of your feet to land softly with each step, allowing your ankles, knees, and hips to absorb the impact.
  • Engage your core: Keep your abdominal muscles tight to protect your lower back and maintain stability.
  • Control your breathing: Establish a consistent breathing pattern. For many people, breathing in for 2 knee raises and out for 2 knee raises works well.
  • Use arm movement: Coordinate arm swings with your knee raises to help maintain rhythm and increase caloric burn.
  • Start with shorter intervals: Begin with 20-30 second intervals and gradually increase duration as your conditioning improves.
  • Modify as needed: If you're a beginner or returning from injury, you can perform lower knee raises until you build up the necessary strength and flexibility.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Leaning forward excessively: This reduces the effectiveness of the exercise and can strain your lower back. Maintain an upright torso throughout.
  • Insufficient knee height: Barely lifting your knees diminishes the benefits of the exercise. Aim to bring your knees at least to hip height.
  • Landing heavily: Stomping or landing with flat feet creates unnecessary impact. Focus on landing softly on the balls of your feet.
  • Holding your breath: Forgetting to breathe regularly increases perceived exertion and can lead to dizziness. Maintain a consistent breathing pattern.
  • Rushing from the start: Beginning at too high an intensity can lead to quick fatigue and poor form. Start at a manageable pace and increase gradually.
  • Inactive upper body: Keeping your arms completely still or moving them inefficiently. Proper arm movement helps maintain balance and increases total energy expenditure.
  • Shrugging shoulders: Tension in the shoulders wastes energy and can lead to neck discomfort. Keep your shoulders relaxed and down away from your ears.

High Knees Variations

Marching High Knees

A slower, more controlled version performed at a marching pace, ideal for beginners or warm-ups.

High Knee Twists

Rotate your torso slightly toward the lifted knee to add an oblique component to the exercise.

Traveling High Knees

Move forward, backward, or laterally while performing high knees to increase coordination and spatial awareness.

Cross-Body High Knees

Drive each knee toward the opposite elbow to enhance rotational core engagement and coordination.
