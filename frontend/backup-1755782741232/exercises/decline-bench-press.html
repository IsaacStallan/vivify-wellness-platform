Decline Bench Press

A compound exercise that emphasizes the lower chest for complete pectoral development

Lower Chest, Triceps
Intermediate
Barbell, Decline Bench

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Lower Pectoralis Major (Lower Chest)
  • Secondary Muscles: Triceps, Anterior Deltoids (minor)
  • Equipment: Barbell, Decline Bench, Rack
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Push
  • Force: Push (Downward Angle)

Video Demonstration

How to Perform the Decline Bench Press

  1. 1

    Equipment Setup

    Set the bench to a decline of approximately 15-30 degrees. Secure your legs under the pads provided on the decline bench. Ensure the barbell is racked at an appropriate height and add weight plates as needed, securing them with collars.

  2. 2

    Starting Position

    Lie back on the decline bench with your head lower than your legs, securing your feet under the leg pads. Position yourself so your eyes are under the barbell. Grip the bar with hands slightly wider than shoulder-width apart, palms facing away from you.

  3. 3

    Unrack the Bar

    Take a deep breath, brace your core, and unrack the bar by straightening your arms. Move the bar horizontally until it's directly over your lower chest area.

  4. 4

    Lowering Phase

    Lower the bar with control toward your lower chest (around the bottom of the sternum). Keep your elbows at approximately a 45-75 degree angle from your torso. This should take about 2 seconds.

  5. 5

    Bottom Position

    Lightly touch the bar to your lower chest without bouncing. Maintain tension in your chest, shoulders, and triceps throughout this position.

  6. 6

    Pressing Phase

    Push the bar away from your chest by extending your elbows and driving through your upper back into the bench. Exhale as you push. The bar path should be slightly diagonal, ending up over your lower chest at the top position.

  7. 7

    Top Position

    At the top of the movement, your arms should be fully extended but not locked out, with the barbell directly over your lower chest.

  8. 8

    Repeat & Rack

    Perform the desired number of repetitions by repeating steps 4-7. After completing your set, rack the barbell by moving it horizontally back to the rack position and carefully lowering it onto the supports.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Optimal decline angle: For most people, a 15-30 degree decline provides the best stimulus for the lower chest without creating excessive strain.
  • Secure yourself properly: Ensure your feet are firmly secured under the leg pads to prevent sliding down the bench.
  • Maintain natural neck position: Avoid excessive neck flexion; try to keep your neck in a neutral position throughout the movement.
  • Grip width: A slightly wider-than-shoulder-width grip typically provides the best chest activation while minimizing shoulder strain.
  • Control the bar path: The bar should follow a slight arc, touching the lower chest and ending above the lower chest/upper abdomen area.
  • Maintain tension: Keep your chest elevated and shoulder blades retracted throughout the movement to maximize chest engagement.
  • Consider a spotter: Due to the declined position, having a spotter is especially important for safety when handling heavier weights.
  • Breathing technique: Inhale during the lowering phase and exhale during the pressing phase, maintaining core tension throughout.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using too steep a decline: A decline greater than 30 degrees shifts emphasis away from the chest and creates excessive strain on the shoulders.
  • Bouncing the bar off your chest: This reduces muscle tension and can lead to rib or sternum injuries. Control the weight throughout the movement.
  • Lifting the head off the bench: This creates unnecessary neck strain and changes the exercise mechanics. Keep your head on the bench throughout.
  • Flaring elbows too wide: This places excessive stress on the shoulder joints. Keep elbows at approximately 45-75 degrees from your body.
  • Letting the bar drift too low: The bar should touch your lower chest, not your abdomen. Touching too low reduces chest engagement and increases shoulder stress.
  • Insufficient leg security: Not securing your feet properly can result in sliding down the bench during the exercise, compromising form and safety.
  • Rushing the movement: Moving too quickly reduces muscle tension and increases injury risk. Maintain control throughout the full range of motion.

Decline Bench Press Variations

Decline Dumbbell Press

Using dumbbells instead of a barbell allows for a greater range of motion and can help address muscle imbalances between sides.

Decline Dumbbell Flyes

An isolation movement that provides a greater stretch in the lower chest fibers while in a decline position.

Smith Machine Decline Press

Using the Smith machine provides more stability and can be helpful for those training alone without a spotter.

Decline Push-Up

A bodyweight alternative performed with feet elevated on a bench or step, shifting emphasis to the lower chest.
