Romanian Deadlift

A powerful posterior chain exercise for developing the hamstrings, glutes, and lower back

Hamstrings, Glutes, Lower Back
Intermediate
Barbell, Dumbbells, or Kettlebell

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Hamstrings, Gluteus Maximus
  • Secondary Muscles: Erector Spinae, Adductors, Traps, Forearms
  • Equipment: Barbell, Dumbbells, or Kettlebell
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Hip Hinge
  • Force: Pull (Posterior Chain)

How to Perform the Romanian Deadlift

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Begin standing with feet hip-width apart, holding a barbell (or dumbbells/kettlebell) in front of your thighs with a shoulder-width, overhand grip. Your shoulders should be back, chest up, and core engaged with a slight bend in your knees.

  2. 2

    Initial Hip Hinge

    Initiate the movement by pushing your hips backward while maintaining a slight bend in the knees. Keep your spine in a neutral position (not rounded) and your chest proud as you begin to lower the weight.

  3. 3

    Lowering Phase (Eccentric)

    Continue hinging at the hips while allowing the barbell to travel straight down, keeping it close to your legs. Lower the weight until you feel a stretch in your hamstrings, typically when the bar is just below knee level or mid-shin, depending on your flexibility.

  4. 4

    Bottom Position

    At the bottom of the movement, your torso should be nearly parallel to the floor, or as low as your hamstring flexibility allows while maintaining a neutral spine. Feel a deep stretch in your hamstrings while keeping your back flat, shoulders retracted, and core tight.

  5. 5

    Ascending Phase (Concentric)

    Drive your hips forward to stand up, squeezing your glutes to return to the starting position. The movement should be powered by your hamstrings and glutes, not your lower back. Keep the barbell close to your body throughout the entire movement.

  6. 6

    Top Position

    Return to a standing position with hips fully extended, squeezing your glutes at the top. Your shoulders should be back, chest up, and the weight resting against your thighs.

  7. 7

    Repetition

    Maintain the slight bend in your knees and repeat the movement for the desired number of repetitions, focusing on the hip hinge motion and hamstring stretch with each rep.

  8. 8

    Breathing Pattern

    Inhale as you lower the weight and exhale as you return to the standing position. Maintain core tension throughout the entire movement by bracing your abdominals.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Hip hinge, not squat: The Romanian deadlift is a hip hinge movement, not a squat. Focus on pushing your hips back while maintaining only a slight bend in the knees throughout the entire movement.
  • Neutral spine: Maintain a flat back position throughout the exercise. Avoid rounding your lower back at any point, as this can lead to injury.
  • Shoulder position: Keep your shoulder blades retracted (pulled back and down) throughout the movement to maintain proper upper back positioning and protect your spine.
  • Bar path: The barbell should travel in a straight vertical line, staying close to your legs throughout the movement. This ensures optimal mechanics and safety.
  • Range of motion: Only go as low as your hamstring flexibility allows while maintaining a neutral spine. For many, this will be around mid-shin level, but it varies by individual.
  • Tension focus: Maintain constant tension on the hamstrings throughout the exercise. Think about stretching and then contracting these muscles rather than just moving the weight.
  • Glute activation: Actively squeeze your glutes at the top of the movement to ensure full hip extension and maximize posterior chain engagement.
  • Knee position: Keep a consistent soft bend in the knees throughout the movement—don't straighten or bend them further during the exercise.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Rounding the lower back: This places dangerous stress on the spine and reduces hamstring engagement. Maintain a neutral spine position throughout the movement.
  • Bending the knees too much: Excessive knee bend turns the exercise into a conventional deadlift. Keep only a slight, consistent bend in the knees throughout the movement.
  • Not hinging properly: Failing to push the hips back far enough reduces hamstring engagement. Focus on the hip hinge movement pattern rather than simply bending forward.
  • Looking up excessively: Hyperextending the neck by looking up can strain the cervical spine. Keep your neck in a neutral position aligned with your spine.
  • Moving the weight away from the body: Allowing the bar to drift forward increases stress on the lower back. Keep the bar close to your legs throughout the movement.
  • Using momentum: Bouncing or using momentum reduces muscle activation and increases injury risk. Control the weight through the entire range of motion.
  • Starting with too much weight: Using excessive weight before mastering proper form can lead to technique breakdown and injury. Start light to perfect the movement pattern.

Romanian Deadlift Variations

Single-Leg Romanian Deadlift

Performed on one leg at a time, this variation adds a balance and stability challenge while addressing potential muscle imbalances between sides.

Dumbbell Romanian Deadlift

Using dumbbells instead of a barbell allows for a more natural hand position and can be more accessible for beginners or those with limited equipment.

Banded Romanian Deadlift

Adding resistance bands to the barbell increases the tension at the top of the movement, enhancing glute activation during hip extension.

Deficit Romanian Deadlift

Performed while standing on a small platform to increase range of motion and hamstring stretch, creating greater demands on flexibility and strength.
