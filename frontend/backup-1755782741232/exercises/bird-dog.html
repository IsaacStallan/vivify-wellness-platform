Bird Dog

A fundamental core stability exercise that improves balance, coordination, and strengthens the back and abdominal muscles

Core, Lower Back, Glutes
Beginner
No Equipment

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Erector Spinae, Rectus Abdominis, Glutes
  • Secondary Muscles: Shoulders, Hip Flexors, Hamstrings
  • Equipment: None Required (Exercise Mat Recommended)
  • Type: Core Stability, Balance
  • Experience Level: Beginner
  • Mechanics: Anti-Rotation
  • Force: Isometric/Dynamic

How to Perform the Bird Dog

  1. 1

    Starting Position

    Begin on your hands and knees in a tabletop position. Your hands should be directly under your shoulders, and your knees should be directly under your hips. Keep your back flat (in a neutral position) and your neck aligned with your spine, looking down at the floor.

  2. 2

    Core Engagement

    Engage your core by drawing your navel toward your spine. This creates stability in your midsection and helps protect your lower back throughout the movement. Maintain this engagement throughout the exercise.

  3. 3

    Extend Opposite Limbs

    While keeping your core engaged and your back flat, simultaneously extend your right arm forward (parallel to the floor) and your left leg backward (also parallel to the floor). Reach long through your fingertips and heel.

  4. 4

    Hold the Position

    Hold this extended position for 3-5 seconds. Focus on maintaining balance and stability while keeping your hips and shoulders square to the floor (not rotating). Your arm and leg should be fully extended but not locked at the joints.

  5. 5

    Return to Starting Position

    Slowly return your extended arm and leg back to the starting tabletop position, maintaining control throughout the movement.

  6. 6

    Alternate Sides

    Repeat the movement with the opposite limbs, extending your left arm forward and your right leg backward. This completes one repetition on each side.

  7. 7

    Breathing Technique

    Coordinate your breathing with the movement. Inhale as you prepare, exhale as you extend your arm and leg, and inhale as you return to the starting position. Proper breathing helps maintain core stability and focus.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Keep your back neutral: Maintain a flat back throughout the exercise. Avoid arching or rounding your spine, which can reduce effectiveness and potentially cause discomfort.
  • Hips and shoulders square: Resist the tendency to rotate your hips or shoulders when extending your limbs. Keeping them level and parallel to the floor properly engages your anti-rotation core muscles.
  • Stable base: Keep your supporting hand firmly planted with fingers spread, and your supporting knee directly under your hip.
  • Extension height: Keep your extended limbs at or slightly below torso height. Raising them too high can cause lower back arching.
  • Head position: Maintain a neutral neck position by looking at a spot on the floor about a foot in front of your hands. Your neck should be a natural extension of your spine.
  • Core engagement cue: Imagine pulling your belly button up toward your spine while keeping your ribcage down to ensure proper core engagement.
  • Controlled movement: Perform the exercise slowly and with control rather than using momentum. Quality is more important than quantity.
  • Balanced effort: Focus on equal effort between your arm and leg extensions. One shouldn't feel significantly more challenging than the other.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Arching the lower back: Allowing your back to arch during the extension phase places stress on the lumbar spine. Maintain core engagement to keep your back flat.
  • Rotating the hips: As you extend your leg, your hip may want to rotate up. Keep both hips level and facing the floor throughout the movement.
  • Sagging shoulders: Letting your shoulders sag or hunch during arm extension compromises form and reduces effectiveness. Keep your shoulders away from your ears and engaged.
  • Raising limbs too high: Lifting the arm or leg significantly above torso height often leads to compensation and poor form. Keep extensions parallel to the floor.
  • Moving too quickly: Rushing through repetitions reduces stability benefits. Focus on slow, controlled movements with deliberate holds at full extension.
  • Forgetting to breathe: Holding your breath during the exercise increases tension and makes stabilization more difficult. Maintain a steady breathing pattern throughout.
  • Insufficient core engagement: Simply going through the motion without activating your core muscles diminishes the exercise's benefits. Actively engage your core throughout.

Bird Dog Variations

Single Limb Bird Dog

A simpler version where you extend either just one arm or just one leg at a time, perfect for beginners building stability.

Bird Dog with Elbow to Knee

After extending the limbs, bring your arm and leg back and connect your elbow to knee beneath your torso before extending again.

Bird Dog with Hold and Pulse

At the extended position, hold for 3-5 seconds, then perform small pulses (1-2 inches) with both limbs to increase muscle activation.

Weighted Bird Dog

Adding light weights (1-3 lbs) to the hand and/or ankle to increase resistance and challenge stability further.
