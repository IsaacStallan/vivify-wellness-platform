Leg Press

A machine-based lower body exercise for building strength and size in the quadriceps, hamstrings, and glutes

Quadriceps, Hamstrings, Glutes
Beginner-Intermediate
Leg Press Machine

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscles: Quadriceps
  • Secondary Muscles: Hamstrings, Gluteus Maximus, Calves
  • Equipment: Leg Press Machine
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Beginner-Intermediate
  • Mechanics: Multi-joint
  • Force: Push (Lower Body)

How to Perform the Leg Press

  1. 1

    Machine Setup & Adjustment

    Adjust the seat position so that when seated, your knees form approximately a 90-degree angle in the starting position. Ensure the backrest is at a comfortable angle (typically 45-60 degrees) and that your lower back is firmly against it.

  2. 2

    Starting Position

    Sit on the machine and place your feet on the platform about shoulder-width apart. Your feet should be positioned at a height that allows your knees to bend to about 90 degrees without your buttocks lifting off the seat. Grasp the handles or sides of the seat for stability.

  3. 3

    Safety Catch Disengagement

    Engage your core muscles, prepare for the weight, and carefully release the safety catches or bars that are holding the weight in place. Keep a slight bend in your knees at all times - don't lock them out fully.

  4. 4

    Lowering Phase (Eccentric)

    Slowly bend your knees and allow the weight to descend in a controlled manner. Lower the weight until your knees form an angle of about 90 degrees (or slightly less for those with good mobility). Avoid letting your knees collapse inward during the descent.

  5. 5

    Bottom Position

    At the bottom position, your knees should be bent at approximately 90 degrees with the weight under control. Ensure your lower back remains firmly against the backrest and avoid allowing your buttocks to lift off the seat (posterior pelvic tilt).

  6. 6

    Pressing Phase (Concentric)

    Push through your heels and midfoot to extend your legs, driving the weight back up. Keep your knees aligned with your toes throughout the movement. Exhale as you push the weight upward.

  7. 7

    Top Position

    Extend your legs until they're nearly straight, but always maintain a slight bend in the knees to avoid hyperextension. Do not lock out your knees at the top of the movement.

  8. 8

    Repetition

    Repeat the movement for the desired number of repetitions, maintaining control throughout. When finished, push to the top position and re-engage the safety catches or bars before dismounting the machine.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Foot position variations: Different foot placements target different muscles. Higher foot placement emphasizes hamstrings and glutes, while lower foot placement targets quadriceps more directly. Wider stance works the inner thighs more.
  • Back position: Keep your lower back pressed against the backrest throughout the entire movement to protect your spine. Avoid letting your buttocks rise from the seat.
  • Avoid knee lockout: Never fully lock your knees at the top of the movement as this places unnecessary stress on the knee joint. Always maintain a slight bend.
  • Control the eccentric: Lower the weight slowly and with control, taking 2-3 seconds for the descent. This increases time under tension and promotes muscle growth.
  • Breathing pattern: Inhale as you lower the weight and exhale as you push the weight up. This helps maintain core stability.
  • Range of motion: While a 90-degree knee angle is standard, work within a pain-free range of motion. Those with good mobility may go slightly deeper, while those with knee issues might use a more limited range.
  • Head position: Keep your head resting against the pad and avoid lifting it during the movement, which can strain the neck.
  • Mind-muscle connection: Focus on feeling the target muscles working rather than just moving the weight. This improves activation and results.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Lifting buttocks off the seat: This shifts your pelvis into posterior tilt and places excessive stress on your lower back. Keep your glutes and lower back pressed against the seat and backrest.
  • Allowing knees to cave inward: This creates improper tracking and can lead to knee pain. Focus on pushing your knees outward in line with your toes.
  • Using excessive weight: Prioritizing heavy weights over proper form often leads to partial repetitions and increased risk of injury. Use a weight that allows for complete, controlled reps.
  • Locking out the knees: Fully extending the knees at the top of the movement places unnecessary stress on the knee joint and reduces tension on the muscles. Always maintain a slight bend.
  • Bouncing at the bottom: Using momentum by bouncing out of the bottom position reduces muscle activation and increases injury risk. Control the weight at all times.
  • Placing hands on knees: This can place uneven pressure on the knee joint. Keep your hands on the handles or sides of the seat for support.
  • Improper depth: Going too deep can cause the lower back to round, while not going deep enough limits muscle development. Find the optimal depth where you maintain proper back position.

Leg Press Variations

Single-Leg Press

Performed with one leg at a time to address muscle imbalances and increase the intensity without needing additional weight.

Wide-Stance Leg Press

Using a wider foot position to emphasize the inner thighs (adductors) and glutes while also working the quadriceps.

Close-Stance Leg Press

Using a narrower foot position to place more emphasis on the outer sweep of the quadriceps (vastus lateralis).

High Foot Placement Leg Press

Positioning the feet higher on the platform to increase hamstring and glute involvement while reducing emphasis on the quadriceps.
