Seated Row

Build mid-back thickness, improve posture, and develop pulling strength with this fundamental horizontal pulling exercise

Mid-Back, Lats, Biceps
Beginner
Cable Machine, Row Handle

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Rhomboids, Middle Trapezius
  • Secondary Muscles: Latissimus Dorsi, Biceps, Rear Deltoids, Lower Trapezius
  • Equipment: Cable Machine with Row Attachment
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Beginner
  • Mechanics: Pull
  • Force: Pull (Horizontal)

How to Perform the Seated Row

  1. 1

    Machine Setup

    Adjust the seat height so that the handles align with your mid-chest when you're seated with your arms extended. Select an appropriate weight on the weight stack and attach your preferred grip (V-handle, straight bar, etc.).

  2. 2

    Starting Position

    Sit facing the machine with your feet firmly planted on the footplates or floor. Keep your knees slightly bent. Extend your arms to grasp the handles with a neutral grip (palms facing each other). Sit tall with your chest up and shoulders back.

  3. 3

    Brace Your Core

    Engage your core muscles by drawing your navel slightly in toward your spine. This stabilizes your torso and protects your lower back throughout the movement.

  4. 4

    Pulling Phase

    Initiate the movement by pulling your shoulder blades together (retraction) while maintaining an upright torso. Then, pull the handles toward your abdomen by driving your elbows back behind your body. Exhale as you pull.

  5. 5

    Contracted Position

    At the end of the pull, your hands should be near your lower ribcage/upper abdomen with your elbows close to your sides. Squeeze your shoulder blades together and hold this position briefly to maximize muscle contraction.

  6. 6

    Return Phase

    Inhale as you slowly extend your arms to return to the starting position. Control the weight rather than allowing it to pull you forward. Maintain your upright posture throughout this phase.

  7. 7

    Full Extension

    Allow your arms to extend fully and your shoulder blades to move slightly forward (protraction), but maintain your upright posture—don't round your shoulders or hunch forward.

  8. 8

    Repeat & Complete

    Perform the desired number of repetitions with controlled form. After completing your set, allow the weight to return to the starting position in a controlled manner before releasing your grip.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Engage your lats: Think about driving your elbows back rather than just pulling with your arms to maximize back muscle engagement.
  • Maintain upright posture: Sitting tall with a slight arch in your lower back helps target the correct muscles and protects your spine.
  • Control the negative: Don't let the weight stack pull you forward on the eccentric (return) phase—control the movement both ways.
  • Keep shoulders down: Avoid shrugging your shoulders toward your ears, which can place stress on the trapezius rather than targeting the mid-back muscles.
  • Vary your grip: Different attachments (wide bar, V-handle, rope) can change the muscle emphasis. Experiment to find what works best for your goals.
  • Adjust foot position: If you feel lower back strain, try placing your feet higher on the platform to create a more stable base.
  • Experiment with tempo: Try slowing down the eccentric (lowering) phase to 3-4 seconds to increase time under tension and stimulate growth.
  • Focus on mind-muscle connection: Consciously think about contracting your back muscles during each repetition to improve results.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using momentum: Rocking your torso back and forth to move the weight reduces muscle engagement and increases injury risk. Keep movements controlled and deliberate.
  • Pulling with arms only: Relying solely on bicep strength rather than engaging the back muscles. Focus on initiating the pull with your shoulder blades.
  • Rounded shoulders: Allowing your shoulders to roll forward at the start of the movement, which can lead to shoulder impingement and diminished back activation.
  • Excessive range of motion: Stretching too far forward at the end of the eccentric phase can place stress on the shoulder joints and reduce back muscle tension.
  • Poor posture: Slouching or leaning too far back changes the angle of pull and can place stress on your lower back. Maintain an upright torso with chest up.
  • Jerking movements: Sudden, jerky pulls can strain muscles and connective tissue. Every repetition should be smooth and controlled.
  • Too heavy weight: Using a weight that forces you to compromise form will reduce effectiveness and increase injury risk. Choose weights that allow proper technique throughout all repetitions.

Seated Row Variations

Wide-Grip Seated Row

Using a wide-grip attachment to place greater emphasis on the upper back and rear deltoids.

Single-Arm Seated Row

Working one arm at a time to address muscle imbalances and increase core engagement.

Close-Grip Seated Row

Using a narrow grip to increase the emphasis on the lower lats and biceps.

Rope Seated Row

Using a rope attachment to allow for a greater range of motion at the end of the contraction.
