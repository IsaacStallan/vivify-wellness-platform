Lat Pulldown

A fundamental back exercise that targets the latissimus dorsi muscles to develop upper body width and strength

Back, Biceps, Shoulders
Beginner
Cable Machine, Lat Pulldown Bar

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Muscle: Latissimus Dorsi (Lats)
  • Secondary Muscles: Biceps, Rhomboids, Rear Deltoids, Trapezius
  • Equipment: Cable Machine with Lat Pulldown Attachment
  • Type: Compound
  • Experience Level: Beginner
  • Mechanics: Pull
  • Force: Pull (Vertical)

How to Perform the Lat Pulldown

  1. 1

    Machine Setup

    Adjust the thigh pad of the lat pulldown machine so that it fits snugly over your thighs when seated. Select an appropriate weight on the weight stack. Attach your preferred grip (wide bar, V-handle, etc.).

  2. 2

    Starting Position

    Sit down facing the machine with your thighs secured under the pads. Reach up and grasp the bar with a grip slightly wider than shoulder width, palms facing forward (overhand grip). Sit upright with your chest out, shoulders back, and a slight arch in your lower back.

  3. 3

    Initiate the Movement

    Take a deep breath, engage your core, and begin by pulling your shoulder blades down and together (scapular depression and retraction). Think about pulling your elbows down toward your sides rather than just moving the bar.

  4. 4

    Pulling Phase

    Pull the bar down toward your upper chest by driving your elbows down and slightly back. Maintain an upright torso position—avoid leaning back excessively. Exhale as you pull.

  5. 5

    Contracted Position

    At the bottom of the movement, the bar should be near your upper chest/clavicle. Squeeze your lats for a moment. Your elbows should be pointing toward the floor, not backward.

  6. 6

    Return Phase

    Inhale as you slowly allow the bar to rise back up, controlling the weight rather than letting it pull you. Keep your shoulders down and away from your ears as the bar ascends.

  7. 7

    Full Extension

    Allow your arms to extend fully at the top, feeling a stretch in your lats, but maintain shoulder depression (keep your shoulders away from your ears).

  8. 8

    Repeat & Complete

    Perform the desired number of repetitions with controlled form. After your final rep, allow the weight to return to the starting position before releasing your grip and standing up from the machine.

Form & Technique Tips

  • Focus on pulling with your elbows: Mentally focus on pulling your elbows down toward your hips, not just moving the bar with your hands.
  • Chest up, shoulders back: Maintain good posture throughout the exercise with your chest lifted and shoulders pulled back and down.
  • Control the eccentric: Don't let the weight stack control you on the way up—slow and controlled movement builds more strength and reduces injury risk.
  • Avoid excessive momentum: Rocking your torso to generate momentum reduces the effectiveness of the exercise and can strain your lower back.
  • Experiment with grip width: A wider grip emphasizes lat width, while a narrower grip can target the lower lats more effectively.
  • Minimize wrist flexion: Keep your wrists in a neutral position to prevent strain and maintain proper force transfer.
  • Vary rep ranges: For muscle building, try 8-12 reps. For strength, use heavier weight and 4-6 reps. For endurance, use lighter weight and 15-20 reps.
  • Lead with your chest: Keeping your chest up helps maintain proper spine alignment and optimal lat engagement.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Pulling with the arms only: This turns the exercise into a bicep-dominant movement. Focus on initiating the pull with the shoulder blades to properly engage the lats.
  • Excessive leaning back: While a slight lean is acceptable, excessive leaning turns the exercise into a row rather than a vertical pull. Stay as upright as possible.
  • Using momentum: Jerking or swinging to move the weight reduces muscle engagement and increases injury risk. Control the weight throughout both phases.
  • Shrugging shoulders: Allowing your shoulders to rise toward your ears disengages the lats and can lead to shoulder impingement. Keep shoulders down and back.
  • Incomplete range of motion: Not allowing the arms to fully extend at the top prevents complete lat stretching, while not bringing the bar low enough limits contraction.
  • Pulling to the wrong position: Pulling the bar behind the neck can place stress on the shoulder joint and cervical spine. Pull to the front of your body, toward your upper chest.
  • Using too much weight: Sacrificing form for heavier loads reduces effectiveness and increases injury risk. Choose a weight that allows proper form throughout all repetitions.

Lat Pulldown Variations

Close-Grip Lat Pulldown

Using a narrow grip attachment to better target the lower lats and increase bicep involvement.

Reverse Grip Lat Pulldown

Using an underhand grip to increase bicep activation and target the lower portion of the lats.

Single-Arm Lat Pulldown

Working one arm at a time to address muscle imbalances and increase core engagement.

Wide-Grip Lat Pulldown

Using an extra-wide grip to emphasize lat width and upper back development.
