Recognising Crisis Warning Signs

These signs might indicate that someone is in crisis and needs immediate support. Trust your instincts - if you're worried about someone, reach out for help.

Immediate Warning Signs

  • Talking about wanting to die or hurt oneself
  • Looking for ways to kill oneself
  • Talking about feeling hopeless or having no purpose
  • Talking about feeling trapped or in unbearable pain
  • Talking about being a burden to others

Behavioural Changes

  • Increased use of alcohol or drugs
  • Withdrawing from activities or social contact
  • Isolating from family and friends
  • Sleeping too much or too little
  • Giving away prized possessions
  • Aggressive or reckless behaviour

Emotional Signs

  • Dramatic mood changes
  • Expressing feelings of hopelessness
  • Rage, anger, or seeking revenge
  • Anxiety or agitation
  • Loss of interest in activities once enjoyed

Physical Signs

  • Significant weight loss or gain
  • Unexplained injuries or self-harm marks
  • Extreme fatigue or lack of energy
  • Poor personal hygiene
  • Frequent illness or complaints of pain

How to Support Someone in Crisis

If someone you know is showing warning signs, here's how you can help

Listen Without Judgment

Give them your full attention. Don't try to fix their problems or offer quick solutions. Sometimes just being heard can make a huge difference.

Ask Direct Questions

It's okay to ask "Are you thinking about hurting yourself?" or "Are you considering suicide?" These questions won't plant ideas - they show you care.

Keep Them Safe

Remove any means of self-harm if possible. Stay with them or ensure they're not alone. If they're in immediate danger, call 000.

Connect Them to Help

Encourage them to contact a crisis service or offer to help them make the call. Offer to go with them to seek professional help.

Follow Up

Check in with them regularly. Crisis feelings can return, and your continued support shows you care about their wellbeing.

Look After Yourself

Supporting someone in crisis can be emotionally draining. Make sure you're getting support too - you can't pour from an empty cup.

School-Based Support

Your school has resources available to help - you don't have to face challenges alone

School Counsellor

Most Australian schools have qualified counsellors available for students. They can provide immediate support and help connect you with external services if needed.

Trusted Teacher

If you have a teacher you trust, they can be a great first point of contact. They're trained to recognise when students need help and know how to access school support services.

Student Wellbeing Team

Many schools have dedicated wellbeing coordinators, chaplains, or student support officers who specialise in helping students with personal challenges.

Year Level Coordinator

Your year level coordinator understands the specific pressures of your year group and can provide both academic and personal support.

Deputy Principal/Principal

School leadership teams are trained to handle serious student welfare issues and can coordinate comprehensive support plans.

Student Assistance Services

Many Australian schools are connected to external student assistance programs that provide counselling and support services.
