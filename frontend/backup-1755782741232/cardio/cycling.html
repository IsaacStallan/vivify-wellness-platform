Cycling

A low-impact cardiovascular exercise that builds leg strength, enhances endurance, and improves overall fitness

Cardiovascular, Lower Body
Beginner to Advanced
Bicycle, Stationary Bike, or Spin Bike

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Benefit: Cardiovascular Endurance
  • Muscles Worked: Quadriceps, Hamstrings, Calves, Glutes, Core
  • Equipment: Bicycle, Stationary Bike, or Spinning Bike
  • Type: Cardiovascular, Aerobic
  • Experience Level: All Levels (Adaptable)
  • Calorie Burn: ~400-800 calories per hour (varies by intensity, terrain, weight)
  • Impact Level: Low

How to Cycle with Proper Form

  1. 1

    Bike Setup & Fit

    Ensure your bike is properly fitted to your body. When seated, your leg should be slightly bent (about 25-30 degrees) at the bottom of the pedal stroke. Handlebars should allow for a slight forward lean with relaxed arms and shoulders.

  2. 2

    Safety Preparation

    For outdoor cycling, always wear a properly fitted helmet. Consider using gloves for grip and comfort, and wear appropriate clothing including padded shorts for longer rides. Ensure your bike has working lights and reflectors for visibility.

  3. 3

    Mounting & Starting Position

    Stand over the bike with one foot on the ground and the other on a pedal in the 2 o'clock position. Push off with your grounded foot while pressing down on the raised pedal to begin moving. Once in motion, sit on the saddle and place both feet on the pedals.

  4. 4

    Proper Posture

    Keep your back relatively flat, not rounded. Your shoulders should be relaxed and down, away from your ears. Maintain a slight bend in your elbows, and keep your head up with eyes looking forward. Engage your core for stability.

  5. 5

    Pedaling Technique

    Focus on a smooth, circular pedaling motion rather than just pushing down. Think of scraping mud off the bottom of your shoe on the upstroke. Maintain a consistent cadence (pedaling rate) of 70-90 RPM for optimal efficiency.

  6. 6

    Hand Positioning

    For road bikes, vary your hand position to prevent fatigue. Use the tops for climbing, hoods (curved part of brakes) for regular riding, and drops for descending or fighting headwinds. For flat-bar bikes, periodically adjust grip width.

  7. 7

    Gear Selection

    Use your gears to maintain optimal cadence regardless of terrain. Shift to easier gears (smaller number on front, larger on back) when climbing, and harder gears (larger on front, smaller on back) when descending or on flat ground with tailwind.

  8. 8

    Braking Technique

    Apply both brakes simultaneously for balanced stopping, with slightly more pressure on the front brake. When descending or in wet conditions, feather the brakes (apply and release repeatedly) rather than holding them continuously.

Cycling Tips

  • Build endurance gradually: Start with shorter rides (20-30 minutes) and add 10-15% to your distance or time each week to avoid overtraining.
  • Interval training: Incorporate intervals of higher intensity (e.g., 1 minute hard effort followed by 2-3 minutes recovery) to improve fitness more quickly.
  • Cadence awareness: Practice maintaining a consistent cadence (70-90 RPM) regardless of terrain by shifting gears appropriately.
  • Hydration: Carry water and drink regularly, approximately one bottle (20 oz) per hour of riding, more in hot weather.
  • Nutrition: For rides longer than 90 minutes, consume about 30-60g of carbohydrates per hour to maintain energy levels.
  • Recovery: Include easy rides in your training schedule to promote active recovery and prevent burnout.
  • Cross-training: Supplement cycling with strength training (particularly core and upper body) to prevent muscle imbalances.
  • Bike maintenance: Regularly check tire pressure, brakes, and chain lubrication for optimal performance and safety.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Incorrect saddle height: A seat that's too high causes rocking hips and potential knee strain; too low reduces power and efficiency. Aim for a slight bend in the knee at the bottom of your pedal stroke.
  • Hunched shoulders: Tension in the shoulders leads to neck pain and fatigue. Keep shoulders relaxed and down, away from your ears.
  • Death grip on handlebars: Gripping too tightly wastes energy and can cause hand numbness. Hold the bars with a relaxed but secure grip.
  • Bouncing in the saddle: Excessive up and down movement wastes energy and reduces efficiency. Stabilize your hips and core to minimize bouncing.
  • Pedaling with your toes: Positioning the ball of your foot over the pedal axle provides better power transfer than pedaling with your toes or heels.
  • Neglecting cadence: Pushing very hard in a difficult gear (grinding) can strain knees and lead to fatigue. Aim for a smooth, faster cadence with moderate resistance.
  • Inadequate fueling: Not eating or drinking enough during longer rides can lead to bonking (sudden energy depletion). Consume calories and fluids regularly on rides over 60-90 minutes.

Cycling Variations

Road Cycling

Traditional cycling on paved roads using a road bike with dropped handlebars, focusing on speed and endurance over longer distances.

Mountain Biking

Off-road cycling on varied terrain using bikes with suspension and wider tires, developing balance, handling skills, and explosive power.

Indoor Cycling/Spinning

High-intensity cycling on stationary bikes, often in class formats with structured intervals, standing work, and resistance changes.

Cyclocross

A hybrid of road and off-road cycling that includes dismounting and carrying the bike over obstacles, developing cardiovascular fitness and handling skills.
