High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT)

Maximize results in minimal time with alternating periods of intense exercise and strategic recovery

Cardiovascular, Full Body
Beginner to Advanced (Scalable)
Minimal or None (Adaptable)

Exercise Overview

Exercise Details

  • Primary Benefit: Cardiovascular Fitness, Fat Loss, Metabolic Boost
  • Muscles Worked: Varies by exercise selection (Can target full body)
  • Equipment: Adaptable: Bodyweight, Dumbbells, Kettlebells, etc.
  • Type: Cardiovascular, Metabolic, Strength (Hybrid)
  • Experience Level: All Levels (Can be modified)
  • Calorie Burn: ~400-600 calories per 30 min session (varies by intensity)
  • Duration: 10-30 minutes (typically 20-25 minutes)

How to Structure a HIIT Workout

  1. 1

    Warm-Up Properly

    Begin with 5-10 minutes of light cardio (like jogging in place or jumping jacks) to increase your heart rate gradually. Follow with dynamic stretches such as leg swings, arm circles, and hip rotations to prepare your muscles and joints for intense movement.

  2. 2

    Select Your Work-to-Rest Ratio

    Choose a work-to-rest ratio based on your fitness level. Beginners might start with 1:2 (e.g., 20 seconds of work, 40 seconds of rest), intermediate 1:1 (30 seconds work, 30 seconds rest), and advanced 2:1 (40 seconds work, 20 seconds rest) or higher.

  3. 3

    Choose Your Exercises

    Select 4-8 exercises that target different muscle groups. Mix cardio-focused movements (like burpees, mountain climbers) with strength-based exercises (like squats, push-ups). For maximum effectiveness, include both upper and lower body exercises.

  4. 4

    Determine Your Format

    Decide between a circuit format (completing all exercises once before repeating the circuit) or a Tabata-style workout (focusing on one exercise for 4 minutes before moving to the next). For beginners, the circuit approach is often more accessible.

  5. 5

    Execute with Maximum Effort

    During work intervals, perform the exercise with maximum intensity (appropriate to your fitness level). This should feel challenging—aim for an 8-9 out of 10 in terms of effort. Your breathing should be heavy, and you should struggle to maintain conversation.

  6. 6

    Respect Recovery Periods

    Use rest periods to actively recover by walking in place or performing very light movement rather than standing still. This helps maintain elevated heart rate while preparing for the next intense interval. Focus on controlled breathing during this time.

  7. 7

    Complete Your Rounds

    Most HIIT workouts involve 3-5 rounds of the selected circuit. Begin with fewer rounds if you're a beginner and progressively add more as your fitness improves. The entire workout should typically last 10-30 minutes (excluding warm-up and cool-down).

  8. 8

    Cool Down Thoroughly

    Finish with 5-10 minutes of light activity (such as walking) to gradually lower your heart rate, followed by static stretching to improve flexibility and reduce muscle soreness. Hold each stretch for 20-30 seconds, focusing on the primary muscles used during your workout.

Sample HIIT Workout Routines

Bodyweight HIIT for Beginners

Perfect for newcomers to HIIT, this workout uses only bodyweight exercises and a more forgiving work-to-rest ratio.

Format: 20 seconds work / 40 seconds rest, 3 rounds

Total Time: Approximately 18 minutes

  • Jumping Jacks
  • Bodyweight Squats
  • Modified Push-Ups (from knees if needed)
  • High Knees (marching in place)
  • Glute Bridges
  • Standing Side Bends

Rest 1 minute between rounds if needed. Focus on form over speed.

Intermediate Full-Body HIIT

A balanced workout targeting all major muscle groups with a challenging work-to-rest ratio.

Format: 30 seconds work / 30 seconds rest, 4 rounds

Total Time: Approximately 24 minutes

  • Burpees (modified if needed)
  • Alternating Reverse Lunges
  • Push-Ups
  • Mountain Climbers
  • Plank Jacks
  • Squat Jumps

Keep transitions between exercises quick and maintain intensity throughout all work intervals.

Advanced Tabata HIIT

A challenging Tabata-protocol workout with a demanding work-to-rest ratio for experienced exercisers.

Format: 20 seconds work / 10 seconds rest, 8 rounds per exercise

Total Time: 16 minutes (4 minutes per exercise)

  • Burpees (8 rounds)
  • Jump Squats (8 rounds)
  • Mountain Climbers (8 rounds)
  • Push-Up to Shoulder Tap (8 rounds)

Complete all 8 rounds of one exercise before moving to the next. This is an extremely challenging format requiring high fitness levels.

HIIT Training Tips

  • Start gradually: If you're new to HIIT, begin with 1-2 sessions per week, lower intensity, and more forgiving work-to-rest ratios (like 1:2 or 1:3).
  • Quality over quantity: True HIIT workouts are short but intense. Focus on giving maximum effort during work intervals rather than extending workout duration.
  • Modify as needed: All exercises can be modified to match your fitness level. For example, do push-ups from knees or against a wall if standard push-ups are too challenging.
  • Rest appropriately: Limit HIIT to 2-3 sessions per week with at least 48 hours between sessions to allow for proper recovery.
  • Use a timer: A proper interval timer helps maintain accurate work and rest periods. Many free smartphone apps are available specifically for HIIT.
  • Progress gradually: As your fitness improves, progress by increasing work time, decreasing rest time, adding more challenging exercises, or increasing rounds.
  • Combine with strength training: For a well-rounded fitness program, complement your HIIT workouts with traditional strength training sessions.
  • Listen to your body: While HIIT should feel challenging, pain is a warning sign. Distinguish between productive discomfort and potential injury.

Common HIIT Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping warm-up and cool-down: Due to the intense nature of HIIT, proper preparation and recovery are essential. Always spend 5-10 minutes warming up and cooling down to prevent injury and improve recovery.
  • Sacrificing form for speed: Maintaining proper exercise form is more important than completing a high number of repetitions. Poor form increases injury risk and decreases workout effectiveness.
  • Not working hard enough: True HIIT requires near-maximum effort during work intervals. If you can easily hold a conversation during the work phase, you're likely not pushing hard enough.
  • Working too hard as a beginner: Starting with extremely high intensity before building a fitness base can lead to burnout or injury. Progress gradually as your fitness improves.
  • Doing HIIT every day: The intense nature of HIIT requires recovery time. Doing HIIT daily prevents adequate recovery and can lead to overtraining, decreased performance, and injury.
  • Making workouts too long: Effective HIIT workouts are typically 10-30 minutes (excluding warm-up and cool-down). Extending beyond this often means intensity is being sacrificed for duration.
  • Using inappropriate exercises: Choose exercises that can be performed safely at high intensity. Complex movements requiring significant technique should be mastered at slower speeds before incorporating into HIIT.

HIIT Training Variations

Tabata Protocol

A specific HIIT format consisting of 20 seconds of all-out effort followed by 10 seconds of rest, repeated for 8 rounds (4 minutes total). Extremely effective for improving both aerobic and anaerobic fitness.

EMOM (Every Minute On the Minute)

A timing structure where you perform a specified number of reps at the start of each minute, then rest for the remainder of that minute before beginning the next round.

AMRAP (As Many Rounds As Possible)

A format where you complete as many rounds of a circuit as possible within a set time frame, typically 10-20 minutes, with minimal rest between exercises.

Pyramid HIIT

A structure where work intervals progressively increase then decrease in duration (e.g., 20-30-40-50-40-30-20 seconds) with consistent rest periods, creating a challenging cardiovascular workout.
